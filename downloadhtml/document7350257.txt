<div class="section" id="sec1"><div class="header article-hdr"><div class="kicker">		                        SECTION I.</div><h2>Introduction</h2></div><p>Radio frequency identification (RFID) is a complement to standard bar codes where tracking of objects equipped with RFID tags is done by using radio waves generated from an interrogator or reader <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref1" id="context_ref_1_1">[1]</a>. An RFID tag is an antenna connected to a transponder chip. The signal consists of an ASK modulated carrier. In the case where tags are passive the carrier also acts as power source for the transponder chip.</p><p>Two parameters affecting the performance of a transponder chip are the impedance mismatch at the terminals of the chip and the nonlinear input impedance versus drive power level. Research conducted today on RFID transponders is mostly concerned with design and optimization of the RF frontend interface toward an antenna, for operation in the far field <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref2" id="context_ref_2_1">[2]</a>–<a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref3" id="context_ref_3_1">[3]</a><a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref4" id="context_ref_4_1">[4]</a><a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref5" id="context_ref_5_1">[5]</a>.</p><p>The electrical environment of an RFID tag can be subdivided into three distinctly defined regions. These are reactive near field, propagating near field, and far field <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref6" id="context_ref_6_1">[6]</a>. The impact of an object located in far field can be considered as negligible. This means that the source impedance presented toward the transponder can be considered as fixed. The operation of an RFID tag in the reactive near field, such as in a printer cavity, is significantly different <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref7" id="context_ref_7_1">[7]</a>–<a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref8" id="context_ref_8_1">[8]</a><a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref9" id="context_ref_9_1">[9]</a>. Hence, the assumption of fixed source impedance will generally not hold <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref10" id="context_ref_10_1">[10]</a>. Rather, it changes continuously when the inlay moves along the media path. Furthermore, the source impedance seen by the RFID transponder can normally not be determined in a printer cavity. A printer cavity is still a typical environment for the programming of unique identities to the transponder chip. Thus it is considered as a fundamental component in the RFID logistic chain, although RFID tags are generally not designed to operate in this environment.</p><p>The performance of RFID tags under fixed impedance operation, such as in the far field, with the definition of a figure of merit (FOM) involving the reader and inlay matching coefficients have previously been shown <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref11" id="context_ref_11_1">[11]</a>. However, the need for performance parameters related to near-field operation, such as in a printer cavity, is identified. A relevant FOM for near-field operation needs to include the source impedance as a free variable. A natural choice of measurement interface for determination of FOM would then be the RF front end of the transponder, separating the linear electromagnetic problem from the nonlinear circuit problem.</p><p>Much work has been published on conducted measurement and characterization of RFID transponders. There basically exist three different levels of enhancement in these measurements. The first level uses linear <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX">\$S\$</tex-math></inline-formula>-parameter measurements in a fixed impedance environment <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref12" id="context_ref_12_1">[12]</a>, <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref13" id="context_ref_13_1">[13]</a>. In the second level an altered source impedance is introduced. Several methods exist of which one reported method takes impedance data as function of delivered power for source match <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref14" id="context_ref_14_1">[14]</a>. In another method a source shift from the mutual interaction between two tag antennas is introduced <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref15" id="context_ref_15_1">[15]</a>. In the third level, not only are measurement data at the fundamental frequency gathered, but also at the harmonics <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref16" id="context_ref_16_1">[16]</a>. Nonlinear characterization has also been performed on transponder RF front ends, capturing time-domain waveforms of voltage and current <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref17" id="context_ref_17_1">[17]</a>. A more rigourous modeling would take into account certain fixed topologies with full large-signal characterization <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref18" id="context_ref_18_1">[18]</a>, <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref19" id="context_ref_19_1">[19]</a>.</p><p>Reader functionality can be realized by programming a vector signal generator (VSG) for generation of a wake up signal. Then, the minimum power required for transponder response, or sensitivity, can be measured. In combination with a vector network analyzer (VNA) also, the impedance at this threshold power can be measured <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref12" id="context_ref_12_1">[12]</a>. An extension to the concept of reader functionality with a VSG is the measurement of impedance during high and low pulse in the transponder response <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref20" id="context_ref_20_1">[20]</a>. The VSG is then used as external source to the VNA.</p><p>In this paper a definition of a figure of merit is presented when the drive power level is offset from the threshold of transponder response. The FOM also takes into account the shift to an arbitrary source impedance from a nominal value. This is a typical situation experienced in an RFID printer.</p><p>A new measurement method is presented with an RFID baseband wake up signal, realized as a pulse train. The baseband is directly generated in a VNA with pulse profile option. An additional extension unit contains the hardware for pulse modulation. The big advantage is the small number of instruments needed for the measurement. The VNA and extension unit have a common programming interface and thus appear as a single instrument. To the knowledge of the authors there exists no other work where the RFID wake up signal is generated directly as a VNA pulse profile.</p><p>Measurements are performed on six types of commercial transponder chips. In order to see the variation and find a better real life representative for each type, a probe connection is used, which simplifies the switch from individual to individual <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref21" id="context_ref_21_1">[21]</a>. Since several different transponder types are measured, no particular component technology and topology is considered, but rather an impedance/power relationship at the outside RF interface is modeled.</p><p>The text in the present paper is organized as follows. In <a ref-type="sec" anchor="sec2" class="fulltext-link">Section II</a> the theory of FOM calculation is developed. <a ref-type="sec" anchor="sec3" class="fulltext-link">Section III</a> covers a description of the measurement setup, and <a ref-type="sec" anchor="sec4" class="fulltext-link">Section IV</a> presents measurement results.</p></div>
<div class="section" id="sec2"><div class="header article-hdr"><div class="kicker">		                        SECTION II.</div><h2>Figure of Merit</h2></div><p>The purpose of the presented theory is to develop a FOM for arbitrary choice of source impedance. A theory of FOM will be developed, based on the approximative assumption that the transponder impedance as function of delivered power is independent of the source impedance. This is not true for a general nonlinear load. In this section the approximation in terms of basic facts about nonlinearity is described, followed by the development of FOM theory.</p><div class="section_2" id="sec2a"><h3>A. Background: Nonlinearity With Approximation</h3><p>The RF front end of an RFID transponder chip has a power limiting diode, protecting the transponder toward too high input powers. For excessive power levels heavy distortion occurs, such as clipping effects <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref22" id="context_ref_22_2a">[22]</a>. One example of power limiting is the voltage limiter of a transponder RF frontend <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref3" id="context_ref_3_2a">[3]</a>, <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref4" id="context_ref_4_2a">[4]</a>. The limiting diode will introduce a nonlinearity in the load impedance of the transponder, which will distort the communication with the RFID tag in, e.g., a printer cavity. If the clipping is symmetrical, it can be approximated by an odd order nonlinearity. Also weakly nonlinear devices, such as if the transponder is operated outside the clipping region, in practice contain odd order terms <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref18" id="context_ref_18_2a">[18]</a>, <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref23" id="context_ref_23_2a">[23]</a>. An odd order nonlinearity will cause in-band distortion at the fundamental frequency, and this will have impact on measured data, such as impedance with a linear VNA.</p><p>An assumption central in the present work is that the limiter can be approximated as linear outside the clipping region. The impedance as function of delivered power at the fundamental frequency will then be independent of the source impedance. To validate this, measurements are performed with a linear VNA on a number of transponder chips from different vendors. In the present work, only the balanced antenna port of the transponder chips is accessible, and hence no detailed analysis of the sub circuits, such as the limiter, could be carried out. Consider the impedance of a transponder chip along a source power sweep, as shown in <a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig1" class="fulltext-link">Fig. 1</a>.

For a given offset delivered power <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX">\$\vert\Delta\Gamma\vert\$</tex-math></inline-formula> changes from the threshold value <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX">\$\vert\Delta\Gamma_{th}\vert\$</tex-math></inline-formula> to the offset value <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX">\$\vert\Delta\Gamma_{offset}\vert\$</tex-math></inline-formula>. It was seen from measurements on the available transponder types that the high state impedance is not heavily affected by the power sweep, rather being constant.</p></div><div class="section_2" id="sec2d"><h3>D. Figure of Merit</h3><p>A FOM-function combining the impact of mismatch and <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX">\$\vert\Delta\Gamma\vert\$</tex-math></inline-formula> is defined according to <disp-formula id="deqn13" class="display-formula"><tex-math notation="TeX">$$\displaylines{f_{FOM}(\Delta P,r,\varphi)=\hfill\cr\hfill\vert\Delta\Gamma_{th}(\Delta P,r,\varphi)\Delta\Gamma_{offset}(\Delta P,r,\varphi)\vert^{2}k_{M}(\Delta P,r,\varphi) \quad{\hbox{(13)}}}$$</tex-math><span class="formula"><span class="link">View Source</span><img aria-describedby="qtip-0" style="display:inline;" title="Right-click on figure or equation for MathML and additional features." data-hasqtip="0" class="qtooltip moreInfo" alt="Right-click on figure for MathML and additional features." src="/assets/img/icon.support.gif" border="0" height="20" width="24" /><span class="tex tex2jax_ignore" style="display:none;">\displaylines{f_{FOM}(\Delta P,r,\varphi)=\hfill\cr\hfill\vert\Delta\Gamma_{th}(\Delta P,r,\varphi)\Delta\Gamma_{offset}(\Delta P,r,\varphi)\vert^{2}k_{M}(\Delta P,r,\varphi) \quad{\hbox{(13)}}}</span></span></disp-formula>where <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX">\$P_{xL, offset}=P_{xL, th}+\Delta P\$</tex-math></inline-formula>. <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX">\$(r,\varphi)\$</tex-math></inline-formula> are the polar coordinates, i.e., <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX">\$\Gamma_{s}=re^{j\varphi}\$</tex-math></inline-formula>. <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX">\$f_{FOM}\$</tex-math></inline-formula> expresses an estimate on offset flexibility, i.e., the strength of nonlinearity, weighted by the strength of backscatter modulation. Different alternatives are possible for comparison, by setting one or several of the quantities <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX">\$\Delta\Gamma_{th}\$</tex-math></inline-formula>, <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX">\$\Delta\Gamma_{offset}\$</tex-math></inline-formula> or <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX">\$k_{M}\$</tex-math></inline-formula> to unity.</p><p><inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX">\$\vert\Delta\Gamma_{th}\Delta\Gamma_{offset}\vert^{2}\$</tex-math></inline-formula> represents the product of the backscattered power for threshold and offset delivered power. Note the choice of product rather than quotient <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX">\$(\vert \Delta \Gamma_{offset}/\Delta \Gamma_{th}\vert^{2})\$</tex-math></inline-formula>, in the FOM definition. The choice of product in the FOM guarantees its validity for all possible source impedances. The ratio is undefined for <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX">\$\vert\Delta\Gamma\vert=0\$</tex-math></inline-formula>, which is true if <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX">\$Z_{xL}\approx Z_{xH}\$</tex-math></inline-formula>, or <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX">\$Z_{s}\rightarrow \infty\$</tex-math></inline-formula>.</p><p>The nonlinearity can both present a degraded and improved mismatch condition, depending on the value of source impedance. In order to establish the FOM, only the suppressed region <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX">\$(\Omega_{s})\$</tex-math></inline-formula> is considered, where an increased value of both <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX">\$k_{M}\$</tex-math></inline-formula> and <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX">\$\vert\Delta\Gamma\vert\$</tex-math></inline-formula> indicates a better performance. For an estimate on the entire region, the function average is calculated according to <disp-formula id="deqn14" class="display-formula"><tex-math notation="TeX">$$I_{FOM}(\Delta P)={{1}\over{A}}\int_{\varphi_{1}}^{\varphi_{2}}\int_{r_{1}}^{1}f_{FOM}(\Delta P,r,\varphi)r drd\varphi \eqno{\hbox{(14)}}$$</tex-math><span class="formula"><span class="link">View Source</span><img aria-describedby="qtip-0" style="display:inline;" title="Right-click on figure or equation for MathML and additional features." data-hasqtip="0" class="qtooltip moreInfo" alt="Right-click on figure for MathML and additional features." src="/assets/img/icon.support.gif" border="0" height="20" width="24" /><span class="tex tex2jax_ignore" style="display:none;">I_{FOM}(\Delta P)={{1}\over{A}}\int_{\varphi_{1}}^{\varphi_{2}}\int_{r_{1}}^{1}f_{FOM}(\Delta P,r,\varphi)r drd\varphi \eqno{\hbox{(14)}}</span></span></disp-formula>where <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX">\$A\$</tex-math></inline-formula> is the area of the region of integration. Since <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX">\$f_{FOM}\$</tex-math></inline-formula> is bounded on the entire region of integration it is valid. The boundary of integration is given by the intersection between the unity mismatch quotient circle <a ref-type="disp-formula" anchor="deqn8" href="#deqn8" class="fulltext-link">(8)</a> and the Smith chart. Denote the intersection points <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX">\$z_{is1,2}=(x\mp jy)e^{j\arg(C_{k_{M}=1})}\$</tex-math></inline-formula>, which from the circle definition give the set of equations <disp-formula id="deqn15" class="display-formula"><tex-math notation="TeX">$$\eqalignno{x^{2}+y^{2}=&amp;\,1\cr (x-\vert C_{k_{M}=1}\vert)^{2}+y^{2}=&amp;\,r_{k_{M}=1}^{2}.&amp;{\hbox{(15)}}}$$</tex-math><span class="formula"><span class="link">View Source</span><img aria-describedby="qtip-0" style="display:inline;" title="Right-click on figure or equation for MathML and additional features." data-hasqtip="0" class="qtooltip moreInfo" alt="Right-click on figure for MathML and additional features." src="/assets/img/icon.support.gif" border="0" height="20" width="24" /><span class="tex tex2jax_ignore" style="display:none;">\eqalignno{x^{2}+y^{2}=&amp;\,1\cr (x-\vert C_{k_{M}=1}\vert)^{2}+y^{2}=&amp;\,r_{k_{M}=1}^{2}.&amp;{\hbox{(15)}}}</span></span></disp-formula>Manipulation and solving for <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX">\$(x,y)\$</tex-math></inline-formula> yields <disp-formula id="deqn16" class="display-formula"><tex-math notation="TeX">$$\eqalignno{z_{is1,2}=&amp;\,\Bigg({{\vert C_{k_{M}=1}\vert^{2}-r_{k_{M}=1}^{2}+1}\over{2\vert C_{k_{M}=1}\vert}}\cr&amp; \quad \mp j {{\sqrt{4\vert C_{k_{M}=1}\vert^{2}-\left(\vert C_{k_{M}=1}\vert^{2}-r_{k_{M}=1}^{2}+1\right)^{2}}}\over{2\vert C_{k_{M}=1}\vert}}\Bigg)\cr&amp;\times e^{j\arg \left(C_{k_{M}=1}\right)} &amp;{\hbox{(16)}}}$$</tex-math><span class="formula"><span class="link">View Source</span><img aria-describedby="qtip-0" style="display:inline;" title="Right-click on figure or equation for MathML and additional features." data-hasqtip="0" class="qtooltip moreInfo" alt="Right-click on figure for MathML and additional features." src="/assets/img/icon.support.gif" border="0" height="20" width="24" /><span class="tex tex2jax_ignore" style="display:none;">\eqalignno{z_{is1,2}=&amp;\,\Bigg({{\vert C_{k_{M}=1}\vert^{2}-r_{k_{M}=1}^{2}+1}\over{2\vert C_{k_{M}=1}\vert}}\cr&amp; \quad \mp j {{\sqrt{4\vert C_{k_{M}=1}\vert^{2}-\left(\vert C_{k_{M}=1}\vert^{2}-r_{k_{M}=1}^{2}+1\right)^{2}}}\over{2\vert C_{k_{M}=1}\vert}}\Bigg)\cr&amp;\times e^{j\arg \left(C_{k_{M}=1}\right)} &amp;{\hbox{(16)}}}</span></span></disp-formula>where the boundary is given by <disp-formula id="deqn17" class="display-formula"><tex-math notation="TeX">$$\eqalignno{\varphi_{1}\!=&amp;\,\arg z_{is1}\leq \varphi \leq \arg z_{is2}=\varphi_{2}\cr r_{1}\!=&amp;\,\left\vert C_{k_{M}=1}+r_{k_{M}=1}e^{j\theta}\right\vert\leq r\leq 1 \cr \theta =&amp;\,\pi\!+\!\varphi\!+\!\arcsin\!\left(\!{{\vert C_{k_{M}=1}\!\vert}\over{r_{k_{M}=1}}}\sin\!(\arg(C_{k_{M}=1})\!-\!\varphi)\!\right).&amp;{\hbox{(17)}}}$$</tex-math><span class="formula"><span class="link">View Source</span><img aria-describedby="qtip-0" style="display:inline;" title="Right-click on figure or equation for MathML and additional features." data-hasqtip="0" class="qtooltip moreInfo" alt="Right-click on figure for MathML and additional features." src="/assets/img/icon.support.gif" border="0" height="20" width="24" /><span class="tex tex2jax_ignore" style="display:none;">\eqalignno{\varphi_{1}\!=&amp;\,\arg z_{is1}\leq \varphi \leq \arg z_{is2}=\varphi_{2}\cr r_{1}\!=&amp;\,\left\vert C_{k_{M}=1}+r_{k_{M}=1}e^{j\theta}\right\vert\leq r\leq 1 \cr \theta =&amp;\,\pi\!+\!\varphi\!+\!\arcsin\!\left(\!{{\vert C_{k_{M}=1}\!\vert}\over{r_{k_{M}=1}}}\sin\!(\arg(C_{k_{M}=1})\!-\!\varphi)\!\right).&amp;{\hbox{(17)}}}</span></span></disp-formula></p><p>In summary the described procedure will give an estimate on mismatch roughness under a changed source environment from the conjugate state, when the delivered power needs to be adjusted to a higher value. In other words the FOM will tell how flexible the transponder response is to a shifted source impedance. It also takes into account how the backscatter modulation is affected by including the <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX">\$\vert\Delta\Gamma\vert\$</tex-math></inline-formula>-value. The offset point may represent a state of higher delivered power where some added functionality in the transponder is needed. The adjustment to higher delivered power is in particular common when writing to the transponder memory as compared to only reading from it. The memory in the transponder needs higher power levels for writing. This is a critical measure in reactive near-field RFID, with an altered reactive near field surrounding the RFID tag.</p></div></div>

By shifting its impedance between two distinct states the transponder modulates the CW. Thus the reply from the transponder in a conducted measurement may be detected by a VNA as the reflected signal.</p><div class="section_2" id="sec3a"><h3>A. <div class="section" id="sec4"><div class="header article-hdr"><div class="kicker">		                        SECTION IV.</div><h2>Measurement Results</h2></div><p>Two main electrical parameters are critical for the communication performance of the transponder.
</p><ul style="list-style-type:disc"><li><p>The mismatch between source and transponder in modulated low impedance state, i.e., when the signal excitation is modulated and the transponder is in absorbing mode.</p></li><li><p>The backscatter response of the transponder, which is measured by the <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX">\$\left\vert\Delta\Gamma\right\vert\$</tex-math></inline-formula>-value from <a ref-type="disp-formula" anchor="deqn12" href="#deqn12" class="fulltext-link">(12)</a>.</p></li></ul>To address both the presented measurement method and the performance parameters, focus is put on three main characteristics in the measured results.<p></p><p>First mean and variance between individuals in measured impedance is analyzed. The impedance is calculated as function of delivered power <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX">\$(P_{xL})\$</tex-math></inline-formula>, for each respective transponder type. Note that the calculation as function of delivered power more clearly shows the dependency on source impedance shift. Second, the source impedance dependency is analyzed explicitly. This is done by comparing the deviation in measured impedance between unmatched and matched condition, as function of delivered power. Third the figure of merit is analyzed for the respective transponder type. This is done for the average over individuals, regarded as a real life representative.</p><p>Common for all presented results is the measurement of low state impedance, except for the FOM results where <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX">\$\Delta\Gamma\$</tex-math></inline-formula> is included. Note that the sweep over delivered power ranges over a large interval, due to the mismatch, whereas the measurement range of available power at the VNA port is from <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX">\$-15\$</tex-math></inline-formula> to <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX">\$+10~{\rm dBm}\$</tex-math></inline-formula>. Over this range the calibration of the VNA is valid according to specification.</p><div class="section_2" id="sec4a"><h3>A. Sample Repeatability Modulated Low State Impedance</h3><p>Types A–C are from the same vendor. Also Type D and Type E are from the same vendor. Plotted mean and variance of modulated low state impedance for unmatched and matched condition, are provided for Type C, Type E and Type F, shown in <a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig10" class="fulltext-link">Figs. 10</a>–<a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig11" class="fulltext-link"></a><a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig12" class="fulltext-link">12</a>. <p></p></div><div class="section_2" id="sec4b"><h3>B. Dependency on Source Environment</h3><p>Fundamental to validity of the approximations and FOM calculation discussed in <a ref-type="sec" anchor="sec2" class="fulltext-link">Section II</a> is the independency of source impedance in transponder impedance, when taken as function of the delivered power. In the verification method of the present work the delivered power is calculated from knowledge of the source impedance. Data are taken from the power-swept impedance measurement at low impedance state. The results will partly give information of the individual variation, partly tell how the unmatched to matched condition deviation develops along delivered power. The deviation between unmatched and matched condition, calculated as mean and variance over individuals, is given for Type C, Type E, and Type F, shown in <a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig13" class="fulltext-link">Figs. 13</a>–<a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig14" class="fulltext-link"></a><a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig15" class="fulltext-link">15</a>. Note that <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX">\$\Delta Z_{xL}\$</tex-math></inline-formula> is the difference in complex impedance between unmatched and matched condition, whereas <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX">\$\Delta \Gamma\$</tex-math></inline-formula> is the difference in reflection coefficient between low and high impedance state. A summary of tabled data of maximum unmatched to matched impedance deviation and variance for all types is given in <a ref-type="table" anchor="table3" class="fulltext-link">Tables III</a> and <a ref-type="table" anchor="table4" class="fulltext-link">IV</a> respectively.

This is about <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX">\$-20\$</tex-math></inline-formula> to <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX">\$-15~\hbox {dBm}\$</tex-math></inline-formula> of delivered power, depending on transponder type. Relatively speaking, for Type C and Type D, the deviation is small in imaginary part, since the reactance has its highest values in this power interval.</p><p>From a real life testing perspective rather the low end of the power interval, after threshold of activation, is of interest. The conclusion is that the approximation of source impedance independency holds, as discussed in <a ref-type="sec" anchor="sec2" class="fulltext-link">Section II</a>. It is also concluded that averaging will give a good representative for the calculation of FOM, treated next.</p></div><div class="section_2" id="sec4c"><h3>C. FOM Calculation</h3><p>Central in the present work is to derive a FOM for arbitrary source impedance, since the source impedance is generally unknown in an altered reactive field. The <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX">\$\vert\Delta\Gamma\vert\$</tex-math></inline-formula>-value <a ref-type="disp-formula" anchor="deqn12" href="#deqn12" class="fulltext-link">(12)</a>, discussed in <a ref-type="sec" anchor="sec2" class="fulltext-link">Section II</a>, is the magnitude of the difference between the high and low state reflection coefficient. This is a an extensively used performance parameter in the RFID community <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref5" id="context_ref_5_4c">[5]</a>, <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref20" id="context_ref_20_4c">[20]</a>, <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref28" id="context_ref_28_4c">[28]</a>. Usually the antenna of an RFID tag is designed to present the conjugate of the transponder low state impedance at threshold delivered power. In <a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig16" class="fulltext-link">Fig. 16</a>, <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX">\$\vert\Delta\Gamma\vert\$</tex-math></inline-formula> is plotted as function of offset delivered power with <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX">\$\Gamma_{s}=\Gamma_{xL, th}^{\ast}\$</tex-math></inline-formula>. Note that the source impedance is thus kept fixed over the power sweep.
</p><div class="figure figure-full" id="fig16"><!--          Workaround for combined images.Eg.- 1000116 Fig. 5        --><div class="img-wrap"><a href="/mediastore/IEEE/content/media/22/7370835/7350257/7350257-fig-16-large.gif"><img src="/mediastore/IEEE/content/media/22/7370835/7350257/7350257-fig-16-small.gif" alt="Figure 16" /><div class="zoom" title="View Larger Image"></div></a></div><div class="figcaption"><b class="title">Fig. 16. </b><fig><p>Power sweep of <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX">\$\vert\Delta\Gamma\vert\$</tex-math></inline-formula> for fixed source impedance <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX">\$\Gamma_{s}=\Gamma_{xL, th}^{\ast}\$</tex-math></inline-formula>.</p></fig></div><p class="links"><a href="/document/7350257/all-figures" class="all">View All</a></p></div>
<p></p><p>It is observed that Type A supersedes the remaining types over the power sweep and Type D has a lower maximum value than all other types. All types except Type A show similar performance.</p><p>The results in <a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig16" class="fulltext-link">Fig. 16</a> give useful information of the performance in an RFID system, where the reactive near field can be considered to be unaltered, i.e., the source impedance is constant along the delivered power sweep. New performance criteria are needed in an altered reactive near field, for arbitrary source impedance. The proposed FOM is based on function averaging from integration over specific regions in the source plane, as described in <a ref-type="sec" anchor="sec2b" class="fulltext-link">Section II-B</a>.</p><p>Consider the <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX">\$\vert\Delta\Gamma\vert\$</tex-math></inline-formula>-value as function average. In this case integration <a ref-type="disp-formula" anchor="deqn14" href="#deqn14" class="fulltext-link">(14)</a> is performed over the suppressed region with <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX">\$\vert\Delta\Gamma_{th}\vert =k_{M}=1\$</tex-math></inline-formula> in <a ref-type="disp-formula" anchor="deqn13" href="#deqn13" class="fulltext-link">(13)</a> so <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX">\$f_{FOM}=\vert\Delta\Gamma_{offset}\vert\$</tex-math></inline-formula>. The power sweep is shown in <a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig17" class="fulltext-link">Fig. 17</a>.
</p><div class="figure figure-full" id="fig17"><!--          Workaround for combined images.Eg.- 1000116 Fig. 5        --><div class="img-wrap"><a href="/mediastore/IEEE/content/media/22/7370835/7350257/7350257-fig-17-large.gif"><img src="/mediastore/IEEE/content/media/22/7370835/7350257/7350257-fig-17-small.gif" alt="Figure 17" /><div class="zoom" title="View Larger Image"></div></a></div><div class="figcaption"><b class="title">Fig. 17. </b><fig><p>Power sweep of <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX">\$\vert\Delta\Gamma\vert\$</tex-math></inline-formula> as function average.</p></fig></div><p class="links"><a href="/document/7350257/all-figures" class="all">View All</a></p></div>
<p></p><p>A more conservative value of the maximum value is now seen in <a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig17" class="fulltext-link">Fig. 17</a> compared to <a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig16" class="fulltext-link">Fig. 16</a>. Type D also has a more significant lower <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX">\$\vert\Delta\Gamma\vert\$</tex-math></inline-formula>-value over the power sweep.</p><p>Next the power sweep of mismatch performance with <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX">\$k_{M}\$</tex-math></inline-formula> as function average is considered. In this case <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX">\$\vert\Delta\Gamma_{th}\vert =\vert\Delta\Gamma_{offset}\vert=1\$</tex-math></inline-formula> in <a ref-type="disp-formula" anchor="deqn13" href="#deqn13" class="fulltext-link">(13)</a>. The result is shown in <a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig18" class="fulltext-link">Fig. 18</a>.
</p><div class="figure figure-full" id="fig18"><!--          Workaround for combined images.Eg.- 1000116 Fig. 5        --><div class="img-wrap"><a href="/mediastore/IEEE/content/media/22/7370835/7350257/7350257-fig-18-large.gif"><img src="/mediastore/IEEE/content/media/22/7370835/7350257/7350257-fig-18-small.gif" alt="Figure 18" /><div class="zoom" title="View Larger Image"></div></a></div><div class="figcaption"><b class="title">Fig. 18. </b><fig><p>FOM sweep without <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX">\$\Delta\Gamma\$</tex-math></inline-formula>.</p></fig></div><p class="links"><a href="/document/7350257/all-figures" class="all">View All</a></p></div>
<p></p><p>Type A has the highest FOM and Type F the lowest FOM, which is consistent over the offset sweep. Still the deviation between Type B, C, D, E, and F is not significant.</p><p>Finally, the impact of both <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX">\$\Delta \Gamma_{th}\$</tex-math></inline-formula> and <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX">\$k_{M}\$</tex-math></inline-formula> is plotted in <a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig19" class="fulltext-link">Fig. 19</a>, i.e., all quantities in <a ref-type="disp-formula" anchor="deqn13" href="#deqn13" class="fulltext-link">(13)</a> are included.
</p><div class="figure figure-full" id="fig19"><!--          Workaround for combined images.Eg.- 1000116 Fig. 5        --><div class="img-wrap"><a href="/mediastore/IEEE/content/media/22/7370835/7350257/7350257-fig-19-large.gif"><img src="/mediastore/IEEE/content/media/22/7370835/7350257/7350257-fig-19-small.gif" alt="Figure 19" /><div class="zoom" title="View Larger Image"></div></a></div><div class="figcaption"><b class="title">Fig. 19. </b><fig><p>FOM sweep with <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX">\$\Delta\Gamma\$</tex-math></inline-formula>.</p></fig></div><p class="links"><a href="/document/7350257/all-figures" class="all">View All</a></p></div>
<p></p><p>The results show that Type C exceeds the remaining types in performance, in the lower half of power offset. Types A, B, and C are from the same vendor, of which Type C is the latest released product. For Type D a big difference between excluding and including <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX">\$\Delta\Gamma\$</tex-math></inline-formula> is seen.</p><p>Another observation is that at approximately 1 and 4 dB power offset respectively Type E and Type F transition from higher to lower values of performance as compared to Type A, although this is not as evident for Type E. This indicates that nonlinearity will have a big impact when going from an initial threshold state to an offset state, already at quite low power offsets. This is an interesting fact, since in particular Types E and Type F have shown deviating performance in printer environment tests over the RFID protocol, also known as profiling tests. The coupler, which is used for inlay interrogation in such tests, is usually a specialized design for strong reactive near-field interaction <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref29" id="context_ref_29_4c">[29]</a>. Type A is mentioned as comparison here, since during the testing in particular Types A, E, and F were compared.</p><p>From profiling it has been seen that Type E and Type F have a higher read to write offset although the read threshold is reached at lower power levels. This is the main parameter electrically described by the FOM. A high FOM yields higher flexibility to a change of source impedance, i.e., for a high FOM the mismatch condition does not change drastically during the offset adjustment, independent of the source environment. In other words a reliable reading and writing to the transponder chip can be achieved. It can also be done at low drive power levels. This is another requirement to achieve good RF isolation in a dense tag environment, such as commonly found in a printer cavity.</p><p>Also Type D has shown deviating performance in printer tests, where higher drive levels are needed for any type of response. However the mismatch flexibility (<a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig18" class="fulltext-link">Fig. 18</a>) is in alignment with the remaining types, yielding immediate read and write response, once the threshold level is reached.</p></div></div>
<div class="section" id="sec5"><div class="header article-hdr"><div class="kicker">		                        SECTION V.</div><h2>Conclusions</h2></div><p>A measurement method for characterization of UHF transponder chip response quality, under a shifted source environment, has been presented. A wake up sequence, conforming to the RFID protocol, standard was created, using the pulse modulation and profile options in a network analyzer. It proved successful in triggering the transponder to respond. It was possible to measure the backscatter response, as the in pulse low and high impedance state. A new figure of merit including the <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX">\$\vert\Delta\Gamma\vert\$</tex-math></inline-formula>-value and mismatch under source impedance shift has been introduced. The FOM value, which is based on a modulated power sweep, will predict response behavior from the transponder, when the drive level from an arbitrary source is increased. The measurement results are taken for six different chip types. The results show a behavior, depending on chip type, which correlates with RFID printer tests on the RFID protocol level. In particular it was seen that the high threshold power level and read to write offset in power, occurring for certain types, also gave a changed behavior in the figure of merit defined in the present work.</p></div>
