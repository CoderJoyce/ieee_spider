<div class="section" id="sec1"><div class="header article-hdr"><div class="kicker">		                        SECTION I.</div><h2>Introduction</h2></div><p>RFID technology has been implemented widely for item identification and inventory tracking. Recently there is also strong interest for integrating RFIDs with sensors to wirelessly monitor conditions of structures or devices while in service and possibly hostile environments <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref1" id="context_ref_1_1">[1]</a>, <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref2" id="context_ref_2_1">[2]</a>. Generally speaking, the RFID tag/sensor tag antenna is only designed for a given material to optimize impedance matching and read range. However, a tag's performance deteriorates significantly when mounting location or nearby material changes. Therefore, it is desirable to design a broad impedance bandwidth RFID tag antenna for operation in a variety of material structures.</p><p>Broadband RFID tag antennas have been considered before, but primarily for covering multiple bands <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref3" id="context_ref_3_1">[3]</a>â<a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref4" id="context_ref_4_1">[4]</a><a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref5" id="context_ref_5_1">[5]</a>. Here, we focus on a broadband UHF RFID tag antenna delivering a bandwidth of 263Â MHz (free space) for the purpose of maintaining performance over a wide range of materials. Previous UHF RFID antennas have been designed for operation on metallic objects <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref6" id="context_ref_6_1">[6]</a>, <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref7" id="context_ref_7_1">[7]</a>, extreme environments <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref8" id="context_ref_8_1">[8]</a>, and metallic/dielectrics <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref9" id="context_ref_9_1">[9]</a>. However, there is no solution in the literature for UHF RFIDs that can operate in different dielectrics, including elastic materials.</p><p>

Here, we present an RFID tag antenna constructed from conductive textiles for flexible and stretchable designs <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref10" id="context_ref_10_1">[10]</a>. A single E-fiber thread is employed to embroider a stand-alone RFID wire antenna. This is an attractive approach since former technologies based on stretchable conductive wires (e.g., liquid metal <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref11" id="context_ref_11_1">[11]</a>, copper traces <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref12" id="context_ref_12_1">[12]</a>, silver nanowires <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref13" id="context_ref_13_1">[13]</a>, etc.) are prone to fatigue and wear. Using the proposed textile wires, the RFID antenna exhibits excellent mechanical properties that favor its application in hostile environments.<p></p><p>In this work, a textile-based broadband RFID tag antenna is presented. The proposed tag antenna is designed incorporating three aspects: 1)Â electrical properties: broad bandwidth and electrically small size; 2)Â mechanical properties: elasticity, flexibility, and mechanical robustness; and 3)Â fabrication complexity: low profile and easy to mass produce. The designed antenna achieves a wide frequency bandwidth making it functional over a wide range of materials. Various tags are fabricated with copper wire and E-fiber threads. Their performance is evaluated and compared with a commercial RFID tag for the tire application. Measurements indicate that the designed antenna achieves significant improvement as compared to a conventional one, without loss of flexibility.</p></div>

This implies that its physical integrity and electrical performance remain intact when the tag is subject to external forces. To do so, the shape of the meander-lines was optimized such that the tag's performance is preserved even if the antenna undergoes deformation. Importantly, the meander line structure enables stretching of the tag. <a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig4" class="fulltext-link">FigÂ 4(b)</a> compares the performance of the original antenna and the one stretched by 10% (overall length remains the same). As expected, the simulations show that the antenna's resonance is shifted downwards in frequency after stretching. However, its bandwidth still overlaps the operating frequency band. The end-loading circular loops also allow stretching in any dimension. <a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig4" class="fulltext-link">Fig.Â 4(c)</a> compares the antenna with the original and elongated loops. The elongated loops are of rectangular shape, but maintain the same perimeter.</p></div><div class="section_2" id="sec2a3"><h3>C. 3)Â fabrication Complexity</h3><p>The designed tag antenna incorporates a low profile structure such that the fabrication complexity and manufacturing cost are reduced. Previous textile-based RFID tag antennas use densely stitched conductive textile to produce the surface of planar antennas. In this work, the tag antenna only requires a single thread of the conductive textile. This is because end-loading loops were used instead of patches in order to avoid rigid planar surfaces. The adoption of the loops was inspired by the observation that for end-loading patches, the current is primarily distributed on the edges. <a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig4" class="fulltext-link">Fig.Â 4(d)</a> compares the antenna when the end-loading is formed of loops and patches. The results show that the antenna with end-loading loops achieves equally good performance as that with patches. When fabricated using single textile threads, the circular loop is more attractive as it avoids sharp corners. That is, rectangular loops are challenging to fabricate. Therefore, circular loops were adopted to avoid sharp corners and increase fabrication accuracy. The meander-line does not require embroidering sharp edges either. As will be shown in the following section, the shape of the antenna could be preserved after cured with polymer material.</p></div></div>

<p></p><div class="section_2" id="sec4a"><h3>A. Read Range Test</h3><p>In this test the fabricated copper wire tag embedded in polymer and a commercially available RFID tag for tires (the Speedy Core) were tested and compared. The commercial tag has the same G2XM chip and its structure is shown in <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref20" id="context_ref_20_4a">[20]</a>. Both tags were measured on truck tires. Truck tires are constructed of multiple layers of rubber with different dielectric constants and thicknesses <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref14" id="context_ref_14_4a">[14]</a>. It is necessary to have a broadband antenna such that the RFID tag maintains good performance over a wide range of tires.</p><p>The measurement setup is shown in <a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig7" class="fulltext-link">Fig.Â 7</a>. The space between the two adjacent tires is 15Â cm, equal to the separation of dual tires on trucks. The output power of the RFID reader was set to be 30 dBm. The RFID reader antenna was moved away from the tires until the tag cannot be detected. The distance between the reader antenna and the tire on the left is the read range of the tag. <div class="section" id="sec5"><div class="header article-hdr"><div class="kicker">		                        SECTION V.</div><h2>Conclusion</h2></div><p>In this work, a textile-based broadband elastic RFID tag antenna was designed, fabricated and tested. It was demonstrated that the designed antenna achieves a bandwidth of 263Â MHz in free space, and more importantly, it maintains its tuned behavior when placed on dielectrics with varying permittivity. Different versions of the designed tag antenna were fabricated and tested. Experimental results show that the designed tags achieve consistently better performance as compared to an existing commercial tag. Furthermore, the performance of the designed tag antenna does not degrade under mechanical deformation up to 10%, which makes it a good candidate for elastic and hostile environments.</p></div>
