<div class="section" id="sec1"><div class="header article-hdr"><h2>Handbook of Accelerator Physics and Engineering, 2nd Edition</h2></div>
<p>A.W. Chao, K. H. Mess, M. Tigner, and F. Zimmermann, Editors</p>
<p>World Scientific Publishing Co. Inc.</p>
<p>27 Warren Street, Suite 401-402</p>
<p>Hackensack, NJ 07601</p>
<p>Phone (201)487-9655</p>
<p>Fax (201) 487-9656</p>
<p><a target="_blank" href="www.worldscientific.com">www.worldscientific.com</a></p>
<p>ISBN 978-981-4415-84-2</p>
<p>837 pp.,$138 (Hardcover), 2013</p>
<p>Today, accelerators are applied in many diverse fields. Of the more than 20,000 accelerators in operation world-wide, approximately one-third are used in medical applications and two-thirds are used for industrial applications, with only a few hundred used in applied physics. Electron beam processing is used by many major industries, including automotive, plastics, rubber goods, wire and cable, petrochemical, semiconductors, packaging, and pollution control. In the food industry, electron beams are used for sterilization, and ion beam accelerators are used in a wide variety of industrial applications including micro-machining, x-ray inspection of cargo containers, and nuclear material inspection. Many different types of accelerators are found in industry, e.g., cascade, electrostatic, linear, RFQ, cyclotron, synchrocyclotron, and the synchrotron. New applications for accelerators are growing, leading to new developments in accelerator design and physics.</p><p>This handbook is a collection of information useful for design, construction, and operation of accelerators, and further research. It contains essential information on many different types of accelerators, with primary focus on linear and circular accelerators for high-energy physics and synchrotron radiation. It assumes the reader already has good technical depth in accelerator physics. The background information given consists mainly of formulas and equations used for reference purposes.</p><p>It consists of practical information pertaining to accelerator types and the ancillary power sources used to run the accelerators. Topics include beam effects, dynamics and cooling, optics, nonlinear effects, and polarization. The nuclear and electromagnetic interactions described cover synchrotron radiation, wake functions, particle matter interactions, and impedance functions. The operational considerations include luminosity, brightness, linac operation, space-charge compensation, and operation of various types of circular accelerators. All the major effects on accelerator performance and measurement methods are mentioned. Mechanical and electrical considerations detail very important aspects of accelerator design, including creep and stress relaxation properties, electric and magnetic forces, vacuum systems, ground vibration, dielectric material properties, conductor and superconductor selections, transmission-line design, power-supply design, and HV techniques. Accelerator subsystems such as particle sources, confinement and focusing methods, acceleration, beam instrumentation and diagnostics, polarimetry methods, timing, and controls are discussed. A final section on radiation effects and protection provides reviews of protection principles, radiation sources, radiation transport and shielding, radiation measurements, and damage thresholds.</p><p>This book is an outstanding collection of essential information about accelerator physics and design. It is a concise working tool for designers and operators of accelerators, containing formulae, tables, figures, and, most importantly, references for each subsection. These references, although not exhaustive, represent expert recommendations on reliable sources for more in-depth studies. This is obviously a very specialized handbook, but for those working with accelerators, it would be a very handy source of essential practical information on the design and operation of linear and circular accelerators for high-energy physics and synchrotron radiation.</p></div>
<div class="section" id="sec2"><div class="header article-hdr"><h2>RF Circuit Design Techniques for MF-UHF Applications</h2></div>
<p>A. Eroglu</p>
<p>CRC Press</p>
<p>Taylor &amp; Francis Group</p>
<p>6000 Broken Sound Parkway-NW, Suite 300</p>
<p>Boca Raton, FL 33487-2742</p>
<p>Phone (800) 272-7737</p>
<p>Fax (800) 374-3401</p>
<p><a target="_blank" href="www.taylorandfrancis.com">www.taylorandfrancis.com</a></p>
<p>ISBN 978-1-4398-6165-3</p>
<p>357 pp.,$129.95 (Hardcover), 2013</p>
<p>Radio frequency (RF) technology plays a critical role in many modern-day devices. Cell phones, GPS, wireless communications, toll collection, semiconductor processing, and many other technologies would not be possible without RF circuits and components. Many of these important applications occur in the medium frequency (MF) to ultra-high frequency (UHF) bands. Industrial, scientific, and medical (ISM) applications, which include radio frequency identification (RFID), magnetic resonance imaging (MRI), and semiconductor processing, also use this frequency range. So, there is a need to understand the theory, design, and the practical implementation of components used to construct circuits and devices for these applications.</p><p>This book provides practicing engineers and students with the information to design, model, and build RF components used in the MF or UHF range. Each chapter presents theory and a verification process with computer-aided design (CAD) tools. Several practical application examples that complement theory, and simulations are included. Tables, graphs of properties, and charts based on theory show how to design RF components efficiently. The CAD tools, including MATLAB, Ansoft designer, HFSS, Mathcad, PSpice, and Sonet, are used throughout the book to show how these programs can be used to verify and help optimize a component design.</p><p>Each chapter of the book focuses on a different RF component. After a review of network parameters (i.e., Z, Y, h, ABCB, and S-parameters) important in the design of RF components, RF component design techniques are examined in detail, e.g., methods for designing high-power microstrip circuits, directional couplers, transformers, composite and multilayer inductors, filters, combiner and dividers, and RFID systems.</p><p>Throughout, the book contains practical methods to help designers reduce design time. It is an outstanding resource for RF component designers in the MF and UHF range and for engineering students interested in RF design and theory. It reflects the best design methodology used in RF engineering available today, from the application of theory to modeling for verification and optimization, and experimentation. It is filled with useful methods, tips, and techniques and will be an invaluable reference book for those working in the MF and UHF field.</p></div>
<div class="section" id="sec3"><div class="header article-hdr"><h2>Low Power Emerging Wireless Technologies</h2></div>
<p>R. Mahmoudi and K. Iniewski, Editors</p>
<p>CRC Press</p>
<p>Taylor &amp; Francis Group</p>
<p>6000 Broken Sound Parkway-NW, Suite 300</p>
<p>Boca Raton, FL 33487-2742</p>
<p>Phone (800) 272-7737</p>
<p>Fax (800) 374-3401</p>
<p><a target="_blank" href="http://www.taylorandfrancis.com">http://www.taylorandfrancis.com</a></p>
<p>ISBN 978-1-4665-0701-2</p>
<p>387 pp.,$149.95 (Hardcover), 2013</p>
<p>The future is showing us a world of wireless communication embedded in our surroundings and practically invisible, but available whenever needed. From present radio techniques such as GSM, Bluetooth, or 802.11 to emerging technologies like ultra wideband (UWB) or smart dust motes, the common denominator for future advancements is integrated circuit technology. CMOS processes allow for new scaling but can pose challenges in RF circuit design. Also, low power requirements, crucial to future wireless sensors, have become one of the most critical factors. Many applications in health care, security, electrical equipment, and commerce require low power consumption and long life, imposing severe design constraints. To achieve these requirements, innovative solutions at the circuit and system levels are needed.</p><p>This book focuses on the state of the art in wireless communication for <i>3G/4G</i> cellular telephones, millimeter-wave applications, wireless sensor networks, and wireless medical technologies. Many new and exciting areas in satellite communications, automobile radar detection, wearable electronics, body area networks, and medical applications are discussed. The book consists of 14 chapters, with each chapter covering a specific application or designs relevant to low power RF communications, and IC circuitry used to enhance wireless communication.</p><p>Some of the major applications covered are passive imaging with Si technology, chemical sensors, UWB location, on-chip accelerometers, and magnetic sensors for biomolecular applications. The challenges for sensing applications are described, along with technical details on the most recent designs used to make measurements and send the information back wirelessly to a central reader. Although the applications are sensor specific, not much technical detail on the sensors themselves is given, but numerous references for further study are quoted. Many of the details relate to power consumption reduction and the various options for achieving it.</p><p>The more general background topics on RF communications cover RF radiation exposure and health, receiver front ends with robustness to process variations, efficient multiple-input multiple-output (MIMO) receiver design, low noise and low distortion radio design, ultra-low power design, and open-loop multiphase local-oscillator generation techniques. Background and concepts are presented along with some circuit schematics illustrating basic concepts, chip layout, and the fundamental theory behind each method or technique.</p><p>This is an interesting book for those wanting to know more about emerging trends and technologies in low power wireless communications, but not for those interested in learning about traditional wireless systems. It covers a variety of topics that will appeal to a broad audience, and is intended for engineers with some integrated circuit background in the wireless communication field. It could also be used as supplementary material in a graduate-level course.</p></div>
<div class="section" id="sec4"><div class="header article-hdr"><h2>Chipless RFID Reader Architecture</h2></div>
<p>N. C. Karmakar, R. V. Koswatta,</p>
<p>P. Kalansuriya, and R. E-Azim</p>
<p>Artech House</p>
<p>685 Canton Street</p>
<p>Norwood, MA 02062</p>
<p>Phone (800) 225-9977</p>
<p>Fax (781) 769-6334</p>
<p><a target="_blank" href="www.artechhouse.com">www.artechhouse.com</a></p>
<p>ISBN 978-1-60807-561-4</p>
<p>323 pp.,$149 (Hardcover), 2013</p>
<p>Radio frequency identification (RFID) technology is a sophisticated method for identification, with many applications. A system consists of a passive tag (powered by the RF from a reader antenna) and a reader. Both the tag and the reader have antennas that are used to communicate with each other. One application is replacement of the bar code on grocery items. Imagine walking a cart full of grocery items past an antenna that scanned all the items in one or two seconds. There would be no need to scan each item individually, saving time in the checkout line. However, the high cost of the present-day tags, relative to printed bar-code scanning, is one of the major hurdles in realizing this scenario. The most expensive part of the RFID tag is the application-specific integrated circuit (ASIC) microchip. Researchers have been attempting to eliminate the ASIC chip from the tag in order to reduce costs and to make the tags fully printable. However, printing microwave circuits using low-cost materials is not easy. A considerable amount of design flexibility and high-precision printing with high-conductivity ink is currently necessary to produce reliable printed tags. The use of these materials also adversely affects the maximum read distance between tag and the reader antenna. Also, no current protocols can be used in a chipless RFID tag because it has no onboard signal processing capability. Thus new types of RFID readers are needed to achieve a functional chipless tag.</p><p>This book has three sections covering various aspects of chipless RFID tag and reader designs. The first section consists of a general overview of the operating principles of chipless RFID tags, and more in-depth coverage of the principles of frequency, time, and hybrid domain chipless tag technologies, including methods for reading the tags. The second section presents the three new reader architectures needed to read the three different types of chipless RFID tags discussed in the first section. Operation, design, and results are detailed for each reader type. The third section describes component-level designs, including ultra-wideband reader antenna designs, passive and active reader components, digital components, and the integration of all these components to make the reader. This section concludes with a discussion of new concepts for the chipless reader with frequency-domain-swept interrogation signals and ultra-wideband impulse radio signals. Integration of the chipless RFID reader is also discussed, and a few applications are presented.</p><p>This book presents a good overview of some of the challenges in creating a low-cost chipless RFID tag and corresponding reader. It provides an easy-to-read technical background on the problems and issues facing researchers in this area, and will appeal to a broad audience interested in RFID technology. The book mainly focuses on much of the work done by the authors (located at Monash University in Australia). Many interesting designs are being developed at the university, and this book provides a convenient way for anyone to read about the latest developments in chipless RFID tag and reader design.</p></div>
<div class="section" id="sec5"><div class="header article-hdr"><h2>Plasma Engineering</h2></div>
<p>M. Keidar and I. Beilis</p>
<p>Academic Press</p>
<p>An Imprint of Elsevier</p>
<p>225 Wyman Street</p>
<p>Waltham, MA 02451</p>
<p>Phone (800) 545-2522</p>
<p>Fax (800) 568-5136</p>
<p><a target="_blank" href="www.elsevierdirect.com">www.elsevierdirect.com</a></p>
<p>ISBN 978-0-12-385977-8</p>
<p>440 pp.,$129.95 (Hardcover), 2013</p>
<p>Plasma technology has seen enormous growth over the last few decades. New plasma sources have enabled new applications such as plasma processing of materials, space propulsion, and especially plasma-based nano- and biomedical technology. Like early technology, these modern plasma sources are based on vacuum, low- and high-pressure gases, and RF discharges, but have been enhanced by new geometries and materials.</p><p>This book covers the physics of plasmas, plasma diagnostics, plasma generation under various conditions, and various applications of plasma science. However, its uniqueness lies in the various applications presented. There are many diverse areas that use plasmas, and the authors have chosen an interesting mix covering space propulsion, nanotechnology, and medical applications.</p><p>The book starts with the fundamentals of plasma science. The theory discussed includes plasma basics such as Debye length, particle collisions, waves and instabilities, wall interactions, and surface phenomena. Plasma diagnostic techniques such as the operation of Langmuir probes, emissive probes, HF cutoff diagnostics, optical measurements and fast imaging, spectroscopy, and microwave scattering are described.</p><p>Electrical discharges are a fundamental source of plasma. A review of breakdown theory including electron and streamer theory, Paschen law, and glow discharge properties is presented. Both atmospheric and vacuum arc phenomena are explained, with up-to-date details on cathode and anode spot properties in air and vacuum arcs.</p><p>Space propulsion applications such as the operation of ablative plasma thrusters, Hall thrusters, micro-thrusters, and plasma plume models generated from thrusters are discussed. Nanotechnology applications of plasmas, e.g., synthesis of carbon based nanoparticles and the effects of magnetic fields on their production and properties, are included. The section on biomedical applications of plasmas describes cold atmospheric plasma (CAP) interaction with cells and the selectivity of CAP with cells.</p><p>This is a very well-written, accessible book on a usually very mathematically intensive subject. Many graphs and illustrations are presented to reinforce theory, and there are very few derivations. The equations presented are relevant to the graphs and data and help to explain the theory. The text could be used for a graduate class in physics or material science. It contains homework problems at the end of each chapter and has extensive references for further study. Professionals working in related plasma-science fields would also find this book useful as an up to date source on the latest developments in plasma arc theory and related applications.</p></div>
<div class="section" id="sec6"><div class="header article-hdr"><h2>Analog Circuit Design, Volume 2</h2></div>
<p>B. Dobkin and J. Williams</p>
<p>Newnes</p>
<p>An Imprint of Elsevier</p>
<p>225 Wyman Street</p>
<p>Waltham, MA 02451</p>
<p>Phone (800) 545-2522</p>
<p>Fax (800) 568-5136</p>
<p><a target="_blank" href="www.elsevierdirect.com">www.elsevierdirect.com</a></p>
<p>ISBN 978-0-12-397888-2</p>
<p>1,268 pp.,$84.95 (Hardcover), 2013</p>
<p>This book was compiled from Linear Technology Corporation's original Application notes. Few analog design books complete with circuit schematics and test results are available today, and many application notes are very specific to the IC chip being detailed, not providing general information on how to use that chip in the real world. Also, today's manuals rarely contain circuit schematics but rather block diagrams and hookup schematics, making it difficult to design or troubleshoot. This book is intended to provide insight into analog circuit design, circuit testing, and lab techniques.</p><p>It consists of three parts with numerous sections in each part. Of course, Linear Technology Corporation's ICs appear frequently. Part 1 describes power management circuits, with six sections covering various aspects of analog power management circuits. Topics include three-terminal regulators; switch regulators; linear regulators; high-voltage and high-current DC/DC converter applications; power supply designs for LCDs, cell phones, camera flashes, and automotive; and industrial power circuit design. Clear and detailed schematics are presented for each circuit, along with an excellent description of the circuit operation, board layout, and other real-world effects that need to be considered to ensure proper operation.</p><p>Part two contains three sections. Section one, Data Conversion, describes circuits for single-cell battery operation. It shows how to combine ICs that run on a 1.5-V battery source. Relatively few circuits are shown because most of the Linear Technology chips use dual supplies. Section two, Signal Conditioning, describes op amp, voltage-to-frequency converters, op amp filters, and temperature measurement circuits. Much practical information is presented, e.g., op amp filter design, compensation capacitor effects, and examples showing how changes in voltage probe impedance affect distortion. Section three, HF/RF Design, covers circuits for varactor biasing, RF power detectors, and a circuit for improving RF power detector output accuracy over a wide temperature range.</p><p>Part three deals with a variety of circuits, including timing, video, signal generators, current sensing, pulse, and power conversion. As in the previous sections, clear and detailed schematics are presented for each circuit.</p><p>Analog electronic designers will find this handbook an essential reference source. It contains a broad range of analog circuit design ideas and practical tips for ensuring proper circuit implementation, and more importantly, it provides the reader with the basis for good circuit and board design techniques. Although the circuits presented may not be exactly what the reader needs, they provide an excellent starting point for generating design ideas that lead to new circuit implementations. Volumes 1 and 2 of this series together constitute an all-encompassing hardbound handbook and a very convenient reference source for analog circuit design.</p></div>
<div class="section" id="sec7"><div class="header article-hdr"><h2>DIY Welding</h2></div>
<p>J. Morley</p>
<p>McGraw-Hill</p>
<p>New York, NY</p>
<p>Phone (877) 833-5524</p>
<p>Fax (614) 759-3823</p>
<p><a target="_blank" href="www.mhprofessional.com">www.mhprofessional.com</a></p>
<p>ISBN 978-0-179968-3</p>
<p>367 pp.,$25 (Softcover), 2013</p>
<p>Metal welding is used to create the modern car, bridges, airplanes, skyscrapers, and ships. Without welding, none of these modern marvels would be possible. There are many different types of welding techniques and many different types of materials that can be welded together. There are also many different applications that use welding, ranging from heavy structural steel, requiring certified welders, to casual welding for art structures.</p><p>This is a practical book on the basics of metal-inert-gas (MIG) welding, primarily covering the welding of mild steel. It also includes instruction on the use of a plasma cutter and oxy-acetylene torches, and associated equipment (bench and angle grinders, chop saws) and hand equipment (clamps, vice, scribes, files, etc.). Each chapter builds on the previous one, but specific parts of a chapter could be referenced for someone already familiar with the basics.</p><p>The book begins with the safety aspects of shop welding, including a review of shop hazards (heat, flame, UV radiation, electricity, chemicals), wearing personnel protective equipment (face shields, gloves, clothes, dust masks, etc.), shop safety equipment (welding screens, fire extinguishers, and ventilation), and safe use of an MIG welder, plasma cutter, oxygen-acetylene torch, angle grinders, chop saw, and a drill press.</p><p>Next, the fundamentals of welding steel are presented. Various types of welders are introduced-torch, MIG, tung-sten-inert gas (TIG), and stick. The types of welds and joints are also reviewed to provide a basic understanding of what various weld types should look like.</p><p>A review of mild steel properties gives the reader an understanding of steel characteristics, and other very practical information on sizes, shapes, and simple methods for identifying an unknown material.</p><p>Plans for building a metal working studio with a welding table, cutting table, and grinding and storage areas are presented. Proper use of various hand and power tools for preparing steel for welding is also covered. The hand tools include clamps, hammers, files, and scribes, and the power tools include angle grinders, bench grinders, chop saws, drill presses, and a roller. Techniques for producing different types of cuts in steel using grinders, and holes using a drill press, and for bending steel to form cylinders using a roller, are described.</p><p>The next section of the book covers the operation of the oxy-acetylene torch, the plasma cutter, and the MIG welder. The basic operation and construction of each piece of equipment is given. Setup, adjustment, and cutting techniques are detailed for both the torch and the plasma cutter, along with their advantages and limitations. Setup and operation of the MIG welder are described in considerable detail, including information on shield gas, wire type, feed speed, and voltage. Details are also given on weld methods for weld orientation, and practice methods for making tee joints and tee corners, weaving, and bead formation. Tips for cleaning and finishing painting and powder coating are presented.</p><p>Various welding projects appear at the end of the book. They include attractive, detailed photographs showing each step in the construction of an end table, fireplace log holder, garden cart, and a barbeque made from a 55-gallon drum. These projects lead to useful end products and demonstrate many of the methods illustrated in the book, thus providing good practice in learning the art of welding.</p><p>Anyone with the desire to learn how to weld steel using an MIG welder would find this book an excellent way to master the fundamentals of MIG welding. It is well illustrated and a pleasure to read. It covers the essentials to get started, e.g., finding or building a metal workshop, buying steel and tools, and knowing how to manipulate steel in a number of useful ways.</p></div>

<p>Annual Reviews is a nonprofit, scientific publisher established to promote the advancement of the sciences. The volumes are organized by editors and editorial committees, who invite qualified authors to contribute critical articles reviewing significant developments within various major disciplines. One of these disciplines, materials research, is presented in this volume.</p><p>The book has three focus areas-computational materials, modern optical microscopy techniques in materials research, and various current interest topics. The computational-materials area is concerned with theoretical models developed to predict material properties. One of the relevant articles describes the self-assembly of polymeric materials, which involves intricate nanoscale polymeric materials whose free energies differ by only a fraction of the thermal energy per molecule. Such small free-energy differences present difficulties in modeling, but experimental results show exciting opportunities for creating morphologies whose properties differ from those of the corresponding bulk. This review discusses opportunities and challenges of recent models which predict the dynamics of self-assembly of polymers.</p><p>The optical microscopy techniques apply mainly to biomedical applications. However, one of the topics, namely real-time subwavelength terahertz imaging, has some interesting applications in biosensing, nanospectroscopy, and plasmonies. Electro-optic imaging and the brightness of recently developed intense terahertz sources together permit the imaging of subwavelength-sized samples without compromising spacial resolution or acquisition time. Recent advances in optimizing spacial resolution and acquisition time are described, and near-field imaging examples on field enhancement in metallic-based resonators and metamaterials are presented. This work can be used to improve our understanding of light-matter interactions at terahertz frequencies and enable new spectroscopic applications.</p><p>Some of the current topics that may be of interest include “Electroplating of Ionic Liquids,” “Flexoelectric Effects in Solids,” “Nanowire Heterostructures,” and “Polymer Electrolytes.” The “Electroplating of Ionic Liquids” article describes the advantages of ionic fluids in reducing the toxicity of traditional plating chemicals, increases in plating efficiencies, and the interactions of ionic liquids that underlie their advantageous properties in the electroplating process. It also summarizes recent research and the properties affected by the ionic plating fluids.</p><p>The “Flexoelectric Effects in Solids” article describes the coupling between polarization and strain gradients in various insulating materials. This effect is very small and not well known in bulk materials, but in nanoscale materials very large strain gradients may generate strong flexoelectric effects. The article reviews the flexoelectric effect in solid insulators and examines potential applications of this electromechnical phenomenon.</p><p>“Nanowire Heterostructures” is concerned with the high aspect ratio of nanowires that results in the unique properties derived from the confinement, interface effect, and mechanical degrees of freedom. It is a tutorial review of heterostructures with a semiconductor nanowire as the central component. It describes the properties of nanoscale components and interfaces, and summarizes the advantages derived from these structure-property relationships in the context of the current understanding of these materials.</p><p>The review article on “Polymer Electrolytes” deals with applications in which polymer electrolytes are used, e.g., lithium batteries, fuel cells, and water desali-nization. The concepts of electrochemical potential, salt activity, and ion transport are explained. Three types of conducting polymer, electrochemical stability of electrolytes, and the effects of water contamination in lithium ion batteries are discussed.</p></div>
