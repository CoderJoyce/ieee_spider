<div class="section" id="sec3"><div class="header article-hdr"><div class="kicker">		                        SECTION III.</div><h2>Environmental Effects</h2></div><p>Each RFID system is always required to operate in real environment, and therefore it may be afflicted by several possible environmental impairments which should be carefully taken into account since they may strongly reduce the real performances with respect to the ideal ones <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref3" id="context_ref_3_3">[3]</a>, <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref7" id="context_ref_7_3">[7]</a>.</p><p><i>Multipath propagation</i>—because of the usual presence of objects around and/or between the RFID devices, multipath propagation may arise <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref3" id="context_ref_3_3">[3]</a>. Multipath interference may have a strong impact on <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">{\rm P}_{{\rm READER}\_{\rm IN}\_{\rm TPM}}</span><span class="MathJax MathJax_Processing" id="MathJax-Element-20-Frame" tabindex="0"></span><script type="math/tex" id="MathJax-Element-20">{\rm P}_{{\rm READER}\_{\rm IN}\_{\rm TPM}}</script></tex-math></inline-formula> (and, therefore, <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">{\rm V}_{0}</span><span class="MathJax MathJax_Processing" id="MathJax-Element-21-Frame" tabindex="0"></span><script type="math/tex" id="MathJax-Element-21">{\rm V}_{0}</script></tex-math></inline-formula>), whose value can be no longer estimated by means of simple free space formulas <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref3" id="context_ref_3_3">[3]</a> which must be corrected by a proper fading margin (according to a suitable statistical description of the RFID channel <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref10" id="context_ref_10_3">[10]</a>) or completely discarded in favour of more adequate deterministic models (such as ray tracing tools). On the contrary, if flat frequency fading is assumed (since the RFID signals bandwidth can be usually supposed less than the channel coherence bandwidth), then no changes are suffered by the modulation index, which is independent of the presence of multipath <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref3" id="context_ref_3_3">[3]</a>.</p><p><i>EM coupling</i>—the presence of objects in the proximity of the transponder may produce electromagnetic coupling and therefore unwanted variations in the tag antenna impedance <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">{\rm Z}_{\rm AT}</span><span class="MathJax MathJax_Processing" id="MathJax-Element-22-Frame" tabindex="0"></span><script type="math/tex" id="MathJax-Element-22">{\rm Z}_{\rm AT}</script></tex-math></inline-formula> <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref7" id="context_ref_7_3">[7]</a>, <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref8" id="context_ref_8_3">[8]</a>; this effect involves not only changes in all the related coefficients (such as the modulation index) but also creates both phase and amplitude distortion and therefore a mixed (phase-and-amplitude) modulation <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref3" id="context_ref_3_3">[3]</a>. It turns out that the formula <a ref-type="disp-formula" anchor="deqn1" href="#deqn1" class="fulltext-link">(1)</a> could be inappropriate, since it is commonly achieved and used assuming just amplitude or phase modulation <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref6" id="context_ref_6_3">[6]</a>, <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref9" id="context_ref_9_3">[9]</a>. Therefore, the reliability of <a ref-type="disp-formula" anchor="deqn1" href="#deqn1" class="fulltext-link">(1)</a> in presence of environmental coupling can't be taken for granted and must be properly investigated. </p><div class="figure figure-full" id="fig4"><!--          Workaround for combined images.Eg.- 1000116 Fig. 5        --><div class="img-wrap"><a href="/mediastore/IEEE/content/media/7727/4446029/4476048/4476048-fig-4-large.gif"><img src="/mediastore/IEEE/content/media/7727/4446029/4476048/4476048-fig-4-small.gif" alt="Figure 4" /><div class="zoom" title="View Larger Image"></div></a></div><div class="figcaption"><b class="title">Fig. 4. </b><fig><p>BER versus SNR according to <a ref-type="disp-formula" anchor="deqn6" href="#deqn6" class="fulltext-link">(6)</a>.</p></fig></div><p class="links"><a href="/document/4476048/all-figures" class="all">View All</a></p></div><p></p><p>The environmental conditions may produce a deep decrease in the m value (with respect to ideal, free space condition) <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref3" id="context_ref_3_3a">[3]</a>. For instance, if m drops from 0.8 (ideal, reference value) to 0.2 and assuming <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">{\rm BER}_{\rm th} = 10^{-3}</span><span class="MathJax MathJax_Processing" id="MathJax-Element-61-Frame" tabindex="0"></span><script type="math/tex" id="MathJax-Element-61">{\rm BER}_{\rm th} = 10^{-3}</script></tex-math></inline-formula>, then an increase of about 6 dB in the ratio <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">{}\vert{\rm V}_{0}\vert/\sigma</span><span class="MathJax MathJax_Processing" id="MathJax-Element-62-Frame" tabindex="0"></span><script type="math/tex" id="MathJax-Element-62">{}\vert{\rm V}_{0}\vert/\sigma</script></tex-math></inline-formula> is required to keep a successful demodulation.</p><p>If r denotes the radio link distance, then the median path loss can be assumed proportional to <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">1/{\rm r}^{4}</span><span class="MathJax MathJax_Processing" id="MathJax-Element-63-Frame" tabindex="0"></span><script type="math/tex" id="MathJax-Element-63">1/{\rm r}^{4}</script></tex-math></inline-formula> <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref3" id="context_ref_3_3a">[3]</a>, <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref10" id="context_ref_10_3a">[10]</a>; this implies that the mean <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">{}\vert {\rm V}_{0}\vert</span><span class="MathJax MathJax_Processing" id="MathJax-Element-64-Frame" tabindex="0"></span><script type="math/tex" id="MathJax-Element-64">{}\vert {\rm V}_{0}\vert</script></tex-math></inline-formula> decreases as <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">1/{\rm r}^{2}</span><span class="MathJax MathJax_Processing" id="MathJax-Element-65-Frame" tabindex="0"></span><script type="math/tex" id="MathJax-Element-65">1/{\rm r}^{2}</script></tex-math></inline-formula> and therefore <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">{\rm r}\approx 0.7 {\rm r}_{\rm ideal}</span><span class="MathJax MathJax_Processing" id="MathJax-Element-66-Frame" tabindex="0"></span><script type="math/tex" id="MathJax-Element-66">{\rm r}\approx 0.7 {\rm r}_{\rm ideal}</script></tex-math></inline-formula>. Considering that “hostile” materials (such as metals and liquids) can often cause a drop in index modulation down to values much lower than 0.2, it is evident that the real properties of the operating environment can strongly reduce the performance of an UHF-RFID system with respect to the expected ones if free space conditions are assumed.</p><p>Moreover, <a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig4" class="fulltext-link">Fig. 4</a> also accounts for the dependence of the BER on the communication data rate. In fact, for given <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">{\rm V}_{0}</span><span class="MathJax MathJax_Processing" id="MathJax-Element-67-Frame" tabindex="0"></span><script type="math/tex" id="MathJax-Element-67">{\rm V}_{0}</script></tex-math></inline-formula>, m and <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">{\rm N}_{0}</span><span class="MathJax MathJax_Processing" id="MathJax-Element-68-Frame" tabindex="0"></span><script type="math/tex" id="MathJax-Element-68">{\rm N}_{0}</script></tex-math></inline-formula> (noise spectral density), <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">{}\sigma</span><span class="MathJax MathJax_Processing" id="MathJax-Element-69-Frame" tabindex="0"></span><script type="math/tex" id="MathJax-Element-69">{}\sigma</script></tex-math></inline-formula> varies according to the signal bandwidth, which is strictly related to the possible data rate provided in <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref1" id="context_ref_1_3a">[1]</a>.</p></div><div class="section_2" id="sec3b"><h3>B. Conclusion</h3><p>RFID systems are usually planned and evaluated in ideal, free space condition, even though the EM properties of the environment are known to threaten the actual system performance (in practice, the real read range).</p><p>In this letter, a closed-form expression for the bit error probability at the reader side has been achieved, assuming an ideal, coherent receiver and by means of a rigorous analytical approach accounting for the most usual environmental effects. Therefore, the obtained formula represents a useful upper bound of the real system performance, whose worsening due to the real environment impairments can be estimated and weighed, in order to allow more reliable planning and analyses of RFID systems.</p></div></div>
