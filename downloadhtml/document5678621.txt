<div class="section" id="sec1"><div class="header article-hdr"><div class="kicker">		                        SECTION I.</div><h2>Introduction</h2></div><p>Radio frequency identification (rfid) in the ultrahigh frequency (UHF) range is a widespread and rapidly developing technology in many fields of applications <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref1" id="context_ref_1_1">[1]</a>. In particular, there is a growing interest in tagging metal items for tracking containers, automotive parts, construction materials, and so on <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref2" id="context_ref_2_1">[2]</a>. Indeed, this is a challenging task since meandered dipoles and loop antennas, which are commonly used in RFID tag designs, do not operate efficiently when mounted on metallic objects. Recently, some solutions have been proposed to overcome this problem, most of which are tailored to match chips with a reactance around â120<inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview"></span><span class="MathJax" id="MathJax-Element-1-Frame" tabindex="0"><nobr><span class="math" id="MathJax-Span-1" role="math" style="width: 0.844em; display: inline-block;"><span style="display: inline-block; position: relative; width: 0.724em; height: 0px; font-size: 111%;"><span style="position: absolute; clip: rect(1.324em 1000.66em 2.405em -999.997em); top: -2.219em; left: 0.003em;"><span class="mrow" id="MathJax-Span-2"><span class="mi" id="MathJax-Span-3" style="font-family: MathJax_Main;">Î©</span></span><span style="display: inline-block; width: 0px; height: 2.225em;"></span></span></span><span style="display: inline-block; overflow: hidden; vertical-align: -0.063em; border-left: 0px solid; width: 0px; height: 0.937em;"></span></span></nobr></span><script type="math/tex" id="MathJax-Element-1"> \Omega</script></tex-math></inline-formula> within the UHF band (860â960 MHz). These configurations are based on folded dipoles <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref3" id="context_ref_3_1">[3]</a>, meandered dipoles with T-matching networks <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref4" id="context_ref_4_1">[4]</a>, planar inverted-F antennas(PIFAs) <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref5" id="context_ref_5_1">[5]</a>, or high-impedance unit cell structures <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref6" id="context_ref_6_1">[6]</a>.</p><p>However, very few <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref7" id="context_ref_7_1">[7]</a> have addressed their attention to the European UHF band (865â868 MHz) for tag chips with an imaginary part of the impedance around â400 <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview"></span><span class="MathJax" id="MathJax-Element-2-Frame" tabindex="0"><nobr><span class="math" id="MathJax-Span-4" role="math" style="width: 0.844em; <div class="section" id="sec2"><div class="header article-hdr"><div class="kicker">		                        SECTION II.</div><h2>Antenna Design</h2></div><p>It is well known from the image theory that, when placing a dipole closer and closer parallel to a perfect electric conducting(PEC) plate, the dipole and its image current cancel each other, thus decreasing the magnitude of the radiated electric field. As pointed out in <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref8" id="context_ref_8_2">[8]</a>, the decreased radiation efficiency of a dipole closely placed to a PEC plane is related to the dipole impedance approaching zero. Therefore, an improvement in the design of a dipole parallel to a PEC ground can be obtained by increasing the dipole radiation resistance in order to raise the radiation efficiency. As it is apparent from <a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig1" class="fulltext-link">Fig.Â 1</a>, starting from a distance around a quarter of lambda, the radiation resistance of a half-wave dipole close to the ground plane tends to diminish more and more as the distance gets closer to zero.

<div class="section" id="sec5"><div class="header article-hdr"><div class="kicker">		                        SECTION V.</div><h2>Conclusion</h2></div><p>The proposed RFID tag antenna is able to operate when mounted on metallic objects and does not require any via or shorting plate in its basic configuration. It is easy to fabricate, has low cost, and has a thickness of only 1.5 mm. It operates within the European UHF band and guarantees a good match with a chip reactance of about <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview"></span><span class="MathJax" id="MathJax-Element-35-Frame" tabindex="0"><nobr><span class="math" id="MathJax-Span-286" role="math" style="width: 3.607em; display: inline-block;"><span style="display: inline-block; position: relative; width: 3.246em; height: 0px; font-size: 111%;"><span style="position: absolute; clip: rect(1.324em 1003.19em 2.405em -999.997em); top: -2.219em; left: 0.003em;"><span class="mrow" id="MathJax-Span-287"><span class="texatom" id="MathJax-Span-288"><span class="mrow" id="MathJax-Span-289"><span class="mo" id="MathJax-Span-290" style="font-family: MathJax_Main;">â</span></span></span><span class="texatom" id="MathJax-Span-291"><span class="mrow" id="MathJax-Span-292"><span class="mstyle" id="MathJax-Span-293"><span class="mrow" id="MathJax-Span-294"><span class="mtext" id="MathJax-Span-295" style="font-family: MathJax_Main;">420</span></span></span></span></span><span class="mtext" id="MathJax-Span-296" style="font-family: MathJax_Main;">Â </span><span class="mi" id="MathJax-Span-297" style="font-family: MathJax_Main;">Î©</span></span><span style="display: inline-block; width: 0px; height: 2.225em;"></span></span></span><span style="display: inline-block; overflow: hidden; vertical-align: -0.063em; border-left: 0px solid; width: 0px; height: 0.937em;"></span></span></nobr></span><script type="math/tex" id="MathJax-Element-35">{-}{\hbox{420}}\ \Omega</script></tex-math></inline-formula>. The estimated read range is close to 5 m if mounted on a metallic plate, but it can cover a distance of more than 2.0 m in standalone configuration providing a metallization of the bottom part of the tag. A simple tuning element can be included in the tag to allow a better control of the resonance frequency. A more compact tag can be employed if vias connecting to the metal plate are introduced in the design.</p></div>
