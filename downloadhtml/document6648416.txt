<div class="section" id="sec1"><div class="header article-hdr"><div class="kicker">		                        SECTION I.</div><h2>Introduction</h2></div><p>Radio frequency identification (RFID) is a rapidly developing automatic identification technology, which uses RF signals for automatic identification of objects. The object to be recognized carries a transponder, called a tag, which can be uniquely identified with a code stored in its microchip.</p><p>Passive RFID principles date back to 1948 <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref1" id="context_ref_1_1">[1]</a>, but only in recent years <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref2" id="context_ref_2_1">[2]</a> it has become a standard in fields such as retail and distribution logistics. Several standards have been defined for RFID systems, such as ISO, Class 0, Class 1, and Gen 2, and each country has its own frequency allocation and power limit for RFIDs. RFID systems operating in a range greater than a meter are classified as long-haul systems <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref3" id="context_ref_3_1">[3]</a>. Devices that belong to this class operate using electromagnetic waves at UHF frequencies (866â869 MHz in Europe, 902â928 MHz in North and South America, and 950â956 MHz in Japan and some others Asian countries) or at microwave frequencies (generally 2.5â5.4 GHz). These devices must therefore operate in the far-field region.</p><p>A passive RFID tag consists in an integrated antenna connected to a logic circuit (chip). The most important requirements for passive RFID transponders are a drastic reduction of costs, weight, size, complexity, and no need of special maintenance. In the past literature, a number of RFID antennas have been proposed and implemented using robust and cheap materials as dielectric substrates <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref4" id="context_ref_4_1">[4]</a>, <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref5" id="context_ref_5_1">[5]</a>. However, the current leading technology for RFIDs makes use of plastic or paper substrates, and metal tracks are implemented through conductive inks. As a matter of fact, in the last years, a large number of studies have been developed in printable electronics since it uses additive manufacturing methods (such as inkjet printing, flexography, gravure printing), and this can significantly reduce the overall production costs, furthermore allowing a more efficient use of the materials. Therefore, printable electronics can help the wide spread of RFID by lowering the production costs of a single RFID tag and allowing the RFID tag antenna to be printed directly on the product package. Nowadays, several cost-effective inkjet-printable RFID tags have been developed, using different cheap materials, including polyethylene terephthalate (PET) <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref6" id="context_ref_6_1">[6]</a>â<a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref7" id="context_ref_7_1">[7]</a><a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref8" id="context_ref_8_1">[8]</a>.</p><p>In order to fully exploit the advantages of these technologies, new antenna concepts should be devised. A tag antenna must fulfill some peculiar specifications. The tag should be small (typically less than 15Ã10 cm<sup>2</sup>) and easily tunable to any frequency within UHF RFID band, and its far-field pattern should be omnidirectional in order to maximize the read range performances. All such requirements push toward a meandered dipole antenna on cheap substrates since meandering allows the antenna to be as compact as possible, furthermore providing an omnidirectional far-field pattern in the plane perpendicular to the axis of the meander <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref9" id="context_ref_9_1">[9]</a>, <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref10" id="context_ref_10_1">[10]</a>.</p><p>The aim of this letter is to present a tag antenna designed on a PET substrate and implemented by inkjet printing using a conductive ink, working in the whole UHF frequency band used for RFID (from 860 to 960 MHz). The proposed antenna is realized without a ground plane, and therefore has a larger bandwidth with respect to the antennas proposed in the literature <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref4" id="context_ref_4_1">[4]</a>â<a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref5" id="context_ref_5_1">[5]</a><a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref6" id="context_ref_6_1">[6]</a><a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref7" id="context_ref_7_1">[7]</a><a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref8" id="context_ref_8_1">[8]</a>, despite of its very simple geometry. Moreover, it is versatile and can be easily tuned to feed many of the commercial chips (rather than being tuned to a specific chip, as in many other tag antennas <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref4" id="context_ref_4_1">[4]</a>â<a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref5" id="context_ref_5_1">[5]</a><a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref6" id="context_ref_6_1">[6]</a><a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref7" id="context_ref_7_1">[7]</a><a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref8" id="context_ref_8_1">[8]</a>) by using a serpentine matching circuit, which allows a wideband matching of the antenna to the most popular commercial RFID chips.</p><p>The antenna has been designed using a general purpose 3-D computer-aided design (CAD), CST Microwave Studio, and then manufactured. The measured results are in very good agreement with the simulations.</p></div>

<div class="section" id="sec4"><div class="header article-hdr"><div class="kicker">		                        SECTION IV.</div><h2>Conclusion</h2></div><p>An antenna designed on a PET substrate and implemented by inkjet printing using a conductive ink is proposed as a passive tag for UHF RFID. The antenna has a half-power bandwidth of 300 MHz and a gain of 1.9 dBi, and measured results are in very good agreement with the simulations. The proposed antenna is simple, very versatile, and can be easily tuned to feed many of the commercial chips. Moreover, it is very robust with respect to realization tolerances, and, if compared to the existing antennas with the same structures, a significantly greater operational bandwidth is achieved. It can be easily adapted to other areas of wireless applications and is able to operate at the UHF frequencies of Europe, the US, and Asia, helping to the circulation and practical applications of the RFID merchandise in the world.</p></div>
