<div class="section" id="sec1"><div class="header article-hdr"><div class="kicker">		                        SECTION I.</div><h2>Introduction</h2></div><p>The near-field radio frequency identification systems (NF RFIDs) are a momentous development of the past decade in tracking and detection systems. They are based on magnetic coupling of RF power <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref1" id="context_ref_1_1">[1]</a>. The Super High Frequency RFID systems have a problem with obstructions like metals and water. Thus, the ultra-high frequency (UHF) range is selected for these NF RFID readers <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref2" id="context_ref_2_1">[2]</a>. The main features like reading stability, maximum power transfer, and smaller tag size <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref3" id="context_ref_3_1">[3]</a> make UHF NF RFIDs a front runner in many applications like smart shelves and item-level tagging <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref4" id="context_ref_4_1">[4]</a>. Recent developments are NF RFID systems with large interrogation area and long read distance <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref5" id="context_ref_5_1">[5]</a>. The tracking in chemical and pharmaceutical industries has problems due to obstructions in near field. The tags over the borosil test tube or plastic bottle containing liquids like drugs and acids become the obstruction for reader-to-tag communication.</p><p>The second problem is associated with the size of the antenna. In order to produce a high amount of magnetic field, usually electrically large structures are used, which in turn increases the antenna size <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref5" id="context_ref_5_1">[5]</a>, <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref6" id="context_ref_6_1">[6]</a>. To produce a strong magnetic field near to the antenna, a loop or segmented loop <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref7" id="context_ref_7_1">[7]</a> can be used. The main disadvantage is nonuniform magnetic field <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref6" id="context_ref_6_1">[6]</a>. The classic oppositely flowing current (opposite direction current or ODC) [<a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig1" class="fulltext-link">Fig. 1(a)</a>] can produce a strong as well as uniform magnetic field. ODC is employed in <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref3" id="context_ref_3_1">[3]</a>–<a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref4" id="context_ref_4_1">[4]</a><a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref5" id="context_ref_5_1">[5]</a>.

This letter contributed the method of generation of strong magnetic field by means of variable distance ODC and presented the magnetic-field analysis of a novel fractal RFID reader antenna. The antenna operates at 897 MHz with a measured impedance bandwidth of 101 MHz. This is ample to contain the USA and Canadian UHF RFID bandwidth of 902–928 MHz and European UHF RFID bandwidth of 865–868 MHz.
