<div class="section" id="sec1"><div class="header article-hdr"><div class="kicker">		                        SECTION I.</div><h2>Introduction</h2></div><p>Rodents and other larger animals are widely used in medical research for various purposes including studies of breathing, hypertension and cardiovascular systems, the development of new treatments for human diseases, and the assessment of new drugs for pharmaceutical trials. During these experiments, it is often necessary to monitor continuously physiological signals that include temperature, blood pressure, pH, nerve activity, electrocardiogram (ECG), and blood glucose levels. Wired telemetry systems are widely used for these purposes, but skin infections and other problems due to wires being passed percutaneously through the skin often create complications. Although implantable wireless telemetry systems are available, they have limitations such as high power consumption, less flexibility, and high cost. A major disadvantage of current telemetry systems is that the area of the cage is limited, thereby restricting accurate simulation of the natural environment.</p><p>To efficiently address the limitations of currently available implantable wireless systems, we started with an extremely low-power wireless telemetry system provided by our industry partner BCS International, Sydney, N.S.W., Australia. This system was designed and tested for operation outside the body, i.e., in air (or free space). The major modification that was required to convert this system to an implantable system was the replacement of its original âfree-spaceâ antenna with a pin-compatible and biocompatible antenna, which functions well when placed under rat skin and fat. This paper focuses on the process of this conversion and demonstration of the biotelemetry system operation after conversion.</p><p>In the last decade, significant progress has been made in implantable antenna research and their medical applications. Medical implant communication service (MICS)-band and industrialâscientificâmedical (ISM)-band planar inverted-F antennas (PIFAs) have been presented for implantation in the head, and the corresponding specific absorption rates and wireless link budgets have been investigated <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref1" id="context_ref_1_1">[1]</a>. A differentially fed dual-band implantable antenna has been proposed for a fully implantable neuro-microsystem <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref2" id="context_ref_2_1">[2]</a>. A microstrip patch antenna can be designed for communication with medical implants following the process and guidelines in <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref3" id="context_ref_3_1">[3]</a>. A small dual-band (400 MHz and 2.45 GHz) implantable antenna exists for continuous glucose monitoring and it has been tested with gels mimicking the electrical properties of human skin <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref4" id="context_ref_4_1">[4]</a>. By coating antennas with appropriate dielectrics, broadband matching can be achieved across a range of host medium (environment) properties <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref5" id="context_ref_5_1">[5]</a>. An experiment conducted with a glycerine-loaded ultra-wideband capsule-shaped antenna has verified this concept. Electrical properties of rat skin have been measured and used for the design of implantable antennas <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref6" id="context_ref_6_1">[6]</a>. An implantable antenna must be designed with in-depth consideration given to its surrounding environment <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref7" id="context_ref_7_1">[7]</a>. A recent review of implantable antennas for wireless telemetry can be found in <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref8" id="context_ref_8_1">[8]</a>. A 900-MHz folded-dipole antenna with a gain of <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview"></span><span class="MathJax" id="MathJax-Element-1-Frame" tabindex="0"><nobr><span class="math" id="MathJax-Span-1" role="math" style="width: 4.808em; display: inline-block;"><span style="display: inline-block; position: relative; width: 4.327em; height: 0px; font-size: 111%;"><span style="position: absolute; clip: rect(1.324em 1004.33em 2.405em -999.997em); top: -2.219em; left: 0.003em;"><span class="mrow" id="MathJax-Span-2"><span class="mo" id="MathJax-Span-3" style="font-family: MathJax_Main;">â</span><span class="texatom" id="MathJax-Span-4"><span class="mrow" id="MathJax-Span-5"><span class="mstyle" id="MathJax-Span-6"><span class="mrow" id="MathJax-Span-7"><span class="mtext" id="MathJax-Span-8" style="font-family: MathJax_Main;">23.5 dBi</span></span></span></span></span></span><span style="display: inline-block; width: 0px; height: 2.225em;"></span></span></span><span style="display: inline-block; overflow: hidden; vertical-align: -0.063em; border-left: 0px solid; width: 0px; height: 0.937em;"></span></span></nobr></span><script type="math/tex" id="MathJax-Element-1">-{\hbox{23.5 dBi}}</script></tex-math></inline-formula> meets the requirements of a specific application when implanted in a human arm <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref9" id="context_ref_9_1">[9]</a>. It is also possible to achieve dual-band operation in implantable antennas with open-end slots in the ground <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref10" id="context_ref_10_1">[10]</a>. A specially designed cavity-slot antenna can be implanted into the human arm <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref11" id="context_ref_11_1">[11]</a>. Another implantable solution for biotelemetry is the compact broadband circular stacked antenna in <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref12" id="context_ref_12_1">[12]</a>. Implantable antennas are used in rectenna applications for wireless power transfer together with biotelemetry <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref13" id="context_ref_13_1">[13]</a>. Radiation performance of sources implanted in the stomach, bladder, and heart have been compared <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref14" id="context_ref_14_1">[14]</a>. The electromagnetic effects caused by ultra-wideband antennas implanted in a human have also been investigated <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref15" id="context_ref_15_1">[15]</a>. An in-vivo communication channel between an antenna placed in the chest and another on the surface of a human body has been characterized <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref16" id="context_ref_16_1">[16]</a>. A medical implant communication service band antenna has been combined with an electrode for body channel communication, enabling a body sensor network controller to communicate with health-care devices located on and inside a patient's body <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref17" id="context_ref_17_1">[17]</a>.</p><p>Recently, the authors proposed several implantable antennas as potentially suitable for the wireless telemetry system considered here <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref18" id="context_ref_18_1">[18]</a>â<a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref19" id="context_ref_19_1">[19]</a><a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref20" id="context_ref_20_1">[20]</a><a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref21" id="context_ref_21_1">[21]</a><a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref22" id="context_ref_22_1">[22]</a><a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref23" id="context_ref_23_1">[23]</a><a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref24" id="context_ref_24_1">[24]</a><a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref25" id="context_ref_25_1">[25]</a>, but these publications were limited to antenna designs and theoretical results only. A compact multi-layer PIFA with a U-shaped radiating patch was proposed and the challenges of designing such antennas discussed <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref18" id="context_ref_18_1">[18]</a>. An implantable Hilbert-curve PIFA, although larger than other antennas, demonstrated better performance <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref21" id="context_ref_21_1">[21]</a>. Its radiation efficiency is about 1.3%, whereas that of a planar strip implantable PIFA is about 0.38% <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref19" id="context_ref_19_1">[19]</a>. Another implantable PIFA with a proximity J-feed described in <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref20" id="context_ref_20_1">[20]</a> is compact and has a sufficient bandwidth of 5% for the application concerned, but it requires four substrate layers and five metal layers for implementation. The effects of the ground plane on resonance frequency as well as impedance matching of a PIFA were investigated in <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref22" id="context_ref_22_1">[22]</a>. Bandwidth enhancements of an implantable antenna, designed for the ground-plane side of our RF identification (RFID) tag, were presented in <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref23" id="context_ref_23_1">[23]</a>. This paper describes a new antenna design with a J-shaped radiating patch, its fabrication, measured results, and wireless telemetry demonstration with the new antenna.</p><p>It is well known that when a âfree-spaceâ antenna is placed under human or rat tissues, it becomes detuned due to the changes in permittivity of the surrounding. To quantify the change in antenna resonance frequency, two 400-MHz âfree-spaceâ antennas (that are very similar to the original 900-MHz âfree-spaceâ antenna in the tag under investigation here) have been previously installed on two types of (microstrip and coplanar waveguide) circuit boards and their shifts in resonance have been measured when placed under rat skin and a thin (approximately 2-mm thick) layer of fat <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref26" id="context_ref_26_1">[26]</a>. The variation in resonance frequency is as large as 20%. This knowledge was useful in the first stage of our antenna design process.</p></div>
<div class="section" id="sec2"><div class="header article-hdr"><div class="kicker">		                        SECTION II.</div><h2>Wireless Telemetry System</h2></div><p>Our RFID-based telemetry system consists of two types of units, which are: 1) battery-powered extremely low-power active RFID tags that are connected to sensors and transducers and 2) a reader unit (base station) that is connected to a monitoring station via a USB cable. The top view of a tag and the original âfree-spaceâ antenna is shown in <a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig1" class="fulltext-link">Fig.Â 1</a>. A reader unit can be seen in <a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig2" class="fulltext-link">Fig.Â 2</a>. This system operates in the ISM band around 900 MHz. The battery-powered tag has a temperature sensor and an ECG sensor, and it can be expanded, through a daughter card, to interface with other transducers for monitoring other physiological signals. The tag circuit also includes a Texas Instrument Incorporated MSP 430F5438A microcontroller, an IDS microchip AG SL900A RFID chip, and a three-axis gyroscope.

<div class="section" id="sec3"><div class="header article-hdr"><div class="kicker">		                        SECTION III.</div><h2>Antenna Environment</h2></div><p>Reasonably accurate yet computationally efficient representation of the environment surrounding the antenna is necessary to design antennas that are suitable for implantation <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref7" id="context_ref_7_3">[7]</a>. This tag will be implanted under the skin of rats. It is inevitable that there will be a thin layer of fat between the top of the antenna and skin layer. The antenna (and any other parts of the tag that are exposed to rat tissues) should be coated with a biocompatible layer. The tag will be surrounded by muscles, etc. These features of a typical rat were represented in our design process using a simplified ârat boxâ model shown in <a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig3" class="fulltext-link">Fig.Â 3</a>. The simplicity of the model helped to speed up the computations during design and optimization.

Design B</h3><p>Following advice from colleagues in the medical implant industry, NuSil's MED-1134 was selected as the biocompatible material for our implantable tags due to its long-term stability. Its dielectric constant is also approximately 2.8, which would enable Design A to resonate above 900 MHz even under rat skin and fat. To retune it back to 900 MHz, an optimized extension [shown in <a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig4" class="fulltext-link">Fig.Â 4(a)</a>] was provided, and hence, Design B was reached.</p><p>The extension was fabricated on another block of FR4 and was integrated with the Antenna I prototype to form an extended prototype of Design B (referred to as Antenna II here) shown in <a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig10" class="fulltext-link">Fig.Â 10</a>. Next, the coated Antenna II was tested under rat skin and fat. In this instance, its resonance frequency was within the Australian UHF ISM band, as shown in <a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig11" class="fulltext-link">Fig.Â 11</a>. <div class="section" id="sec6"><div class="header article-hdr"><div class="kicker">		                        SECTION VI.</div><h2>Parametric Analyses</h2></div><p>The sensitivity of Antenna II input matching to changes in the environment is discussed in this section. Let us first investigate the effect of biocompatible coating. <a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig13" class="fulltext-link">Fig.Â 13</a> shows the variation of the antenna input reflection coefficient with the coating dielectric constant, when all other antenna and environment model parameters are fixed to nominal values. It can be seen that the antenna resonance frequency, peak return loss, and the bandwidth change significantly with the coating dielectric constant. These key antenna parameters are also sensitive to the thickness of the biocompatible coating, as illustrated in <a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig14" class="fulltext-link">Fig.Â 14</a>. The reason for this high sensitivity is the close proximity of the biocompatible layer, which is right over the main radiating element of the antenna.
</p><div class="figure figure-full" id="fig13"><!--          Workaround for combined images.Eg.- 1000116 Fig. 5        --><div class="img-wrap"><a href="/mediastore/IEEE/content/media/22/6888537/6872610/6872610-fig-13-large.gif"><img src="/mediastore/IEEE/content/media/22/6888537/6872610/6872610-fig-13-small.gif" alt="Figure 13" /><div class="zoom" title="View Larger Image"></div></a></div><div class="figcaption"><b class="title">Fig.Â 13. </b><fig>Sensitivity of antenna matching to the dielectric constant of the bicompatible (bcm) layer.</fig></div><p class="links"><a href="/document/6872610/all-figures" class="all">View All</a></p></div>
<div class="figure figure-full" id="fig14"><!--          Workaround for combined images.Eg.- 1000116 Fig. 5        --><div class="img-wrap"><a href="/mediastore/IEEE/content/media/22/6888537/6872610/6872610-fig-14-large.gif"><img src="/mediastore/IEEE/content/media/22/6888537/6872610/6872610-fig-14-small.gif" alt="Figure 14" /><div class="zoom" title="View Larger Image"></div></a></div><div class="figcaption"><b class="title">Fig.Â 14. </b><fig>Sensitivity of antenna matching to the thickness of the bicompatible (bcm) layer.</fig></div><p class="links"><a href="/document/6872610/all-figures" class="all">View All</a></p></div>
<p></p><p>It is also important to assess to what extent antenna matching is affected by any variations in the electrical properties of skin and fat between different rats or between other host animals. It was found that once the antenna is coated with a sufficiently thick biocompatible layer (e.g., 2 mm), antenna matching is almost unchanged due to changes of the skin-layer dielectric constant (between 30â34) as well as the fat-layer dielectric constant (between 3.6â7.6). The same is true for variations in the skin thickness (between 1â3 mm) or fat layer thickness (between 1â3 mm). The antenna input reflection coefficient for all these cases are shown in <a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig15" class="fulltext-link">Fig.Â 15</a>.
</p><div class="figure figure-full" id="fig15"><!--          Workaround for combined images.Eg.- 1000116 Fig. 5        --><div class="img-wrap"><a href="/mediastore/IEEE/content/media/22/6888537/6872610/6872610-fig-15-large.gif"><img src="/mediastore/IEEE/content/media/22/6888537/6872610/6872610-fig-15-small.gif" alt="Figure 15" /><div class="zoom" title="View Larger Image"></div></a></div><div class="figcaption"><b class="title">Fig.Â 15. </b><fig>Sensitivity of antenna matching to the dielctric constant of the skin layer (Er_skin), dielctric constant of the fat layer (Er_fat), thickness of the skin layer (t_skin), and thickness of the fat layer (t_fat).</fig></div><p class="links"><a href="/document/6872610/all-figures" class="all">View All</a></p></div>

<div class="section" id="sec7"><div class="header article-hdr"><div class="kicker">		                        SECTION VII.</div><h2>Biotelemetry System Test and Range</h2></div><p>After achieving a good measured input reflection coefficient and a matched bandwidth, Antenna II was integrated to a tag board and connected to the RFID chip to test the operation of the wireless telemetry system. The coated antenna and the tag were covered with rat skin (that has an attached fat layer) for this test, as shown in <a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig16" class="fulltext-link">Fig.Â 16</a>. A wireless link was established between the tag and the reader. The temperature reading (from the temperature probe attached to the tag) was continuously transmitted to the reader to demonstrate the intended operation of the telemetry system. This temperature was displayed on the computer monitor connected to the reader when it was being transmitted wirelessly through rat tissues, etc. This test further confirmed that Antenna II is indeed operating well in the UHF ISM band. It is worth noting that, with the original antenna, it was impossible to establish such a link between the tag and the reader when the antenna is under rat skin, even when the distance between them is extremely short.
</p><div class="figure figure-full" id="fig16"><!--          Workaround for combined images.Eg.- 1000116 Fig. 5        --><div class="img-wrap"><a href="/mediastore/IEEE/content/media/22/6888537/6872610/6872610-fig-16-large.gif"><img src="/mediastore/IEEE/content/media/22/6888537/6872610/6872610-fig-16-small.gif" alt="Figure 16" /><div class="zoom" title="View Larger Image"></div></a></div><div class="figcaption"><b class="title">Fig.Â 16. </b><fig>Read range test in vertical direction.</fig></div><p class="links"><a href="/document/6872610/all-figures" class="all">View All</a></p></div>
<p></p><p>In order to estimate the system range, which is the maximum possible distance between the tag and the reader, the distance between them was continuously and slowly increased until the wireless link failed. This experiment was repeated along several directions on the horizontal plane (i.e., plane of the tag) and the range was found to be 62â73 cm, which is sufficient to keep the reader unit outside a typical cage. A similar experiment was conducted to find the system range in the vertical direction, as shown in <a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig16" class="fulltext-link">Fig.Â 16</a>, and it was found to be approximately 60 cm.</p></div>
<div class="section" id="sec8"><div class="header article-hdr"><div class="kicker">		                        SECTION VIII.</div><h2>Discussion</h2></div><p>The strategy employed in this research to make the antenna less sensitive to rat tissue variations is the use of biocompatible material as a buffer. Another approach is to add a layer of high-permittivity microwave substrate to the top radiating patch. This was not attempted because it would further increase the thickness of the tag.</p><p>Although Antenna II is about twice the size of the Antenna I due to its retro-fit extension, it comfortably fits within the space vacated by the original âfree-spaceâ antenna. In fact, it is 33% smaller than the original antenna in terms of area. Antenna I, which is 66% smaller than the original antenna, would also work if a biocompatible material with a dielectric constant of about 3.9 is applied. Both Antennas I and II are pin compatible with the tag circuit so no other changes were required to the tag circuit for its conversion to implantability.</p><p>Due to fabrication tolerances, the measured bandwidth of Antenna II is less than that predicted at the design stage, but it covers the required ISM band very well. In fact, it is a very conservative versatile design with strong potential for implantation in other animals that may have different tissue properties.</p></div>
<div class="section" id="sec9"><div class="header article-hdr"><div class="kicker">		                        SECTION IX.</div><h2>Conclusion</h2></div><p>An RFID-based active wireless medical telemetry tag unit, previously designed to work in air, was successfully converted to operate under rat skin and fat, by replacing its general-purpose antenna with a new application-specific antenna, which needs 33% less area in the tag circuit board. The operation of this implantable wireless telemetry system was successfully demonstrated in a system test in which temperature was continuously transmitted wirelessly between the tag and the monitoring (reader) station.</p><p>The thin layer of Nusil MED-1134 applied to the antenna top not only provided biocompatibility, but also acted as a buffer to prevent antenna detuning <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref5" id="context_ref_5_9">[5]</a>, and hence, possible drop-out of the wireless link due to potential variations in skin/fat permittivity or skin thickness between animals. The thickness of the fat layer above the antenna, and hence, the implanted position of the tag, is not critical either for successful operation of the implantable biotelemetry system. Insensitivity of the antenna matching to tissue parameters (dielectric constant and thickness), together with good bandwidth, make this antenna potentially suitable for implantation in other animals as well that have different tissue parameters.</p></div>
