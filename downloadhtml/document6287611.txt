<div class="section" id="sec1"><div class="header article-hdr"><div class="kicker">		                        SECTION I.</div><h2>Introduction</h2></div><p>Voltage-Controlled oscillators (VCOs) in any RF front-end consume a significant fraction of its power budget. Consequently, balancing the tradeoffs between power consumption, tuning range, tuning gain and phase noise is crucial in any VCO design. In addition, design of QVCO for image rejection, modulation and demodulation requires the consideration of quadrature error and possible tradeoff with phase noise and other performance parameters. Quadrature inaccuracies can result in residual image in a down-converted baseband mixer output. There are many factors <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref1" id="context_ref_1_1">[1]</a>â<a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref2" id="context_ref_2_1">[2]</a><a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref3" id="context_ref_3_1">[3]</a> that determine the phase noise of an oscillator, such as silicon lattice phonon scattering, MOS flicker noise and corner frequency, the loaded resonator <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">Q</span><span class="MathJax MathJax_Processed" id="MathJax-Element-1-Frame" tabindex="0"><nobr><span class="math" id="MathJax-Span-1" role="math"><span style="display: inline-block; position: relative; width: 0.003em; height: 0px; font-size: 111%;"><span style="position: absolute;"><span class="mrow" id="MathJax-Span-2"><span class="mi" id="MathJax-Span-3" style="font-family: MathJax_Math-italic;">Q</span></span></span></span></span></nobr></span><script type="math/tex" id="MathJax-Element-1">Q</script></tex-math></inline-formula>, and the final output signal-to-noise ratio (SNR). Various techniques are used for QVCO design, and the most well-known methods utilizes the cross and direct coupling of two LC-VCOs <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref4" id="context_ref_4_1">[4]</a>â<a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref5" id="context_ref_5_1">[5]</a><a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref6" id="context_ref_6_1">[6]</a><a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref7" id="context_ref_7_1">[7]</a>. Coupling and biasing techniques dominate the trend in LC quadrature VCO (QVCO) in achieving low phase noise and low quadrature error. This paper proposes a low-voltage pulse-biased folded-cascode QVCO topology targeting RFID front-end at 866 MHz, which is a well-known lower ultrahigh-frequency (UHF) RFID frequency standard in Europe, Africa, Australia, and New Zealand.</p></div>
<div class="section" id="sec2"><div class="header article-hdr"><div class="kicker">		                        SECTION II.</div><h2>Proposed LC-QVCO Architecture</h2></div><p><a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig1" class="fulltext-link">Fig.Â 1</a> shows the proposed LC-QVCO architecture using two quadrature-coupled LC-VCOs. The ports <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">{\rm I}+, {\rm I}-</span><span class="MathJax MathJax_Processing" id="MathJax-Element-2-Frame" tabindex="0"></span><script type="math/tex" id="MathJax-Element-2">{\rm I}+, {\rm I}-</script></tex-math></inline-formula> and <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">{\rm Q}+, {\rm Q}-</span><span class="MathJax MathJax_Processing" id="MathJax-Element-3-Frame" tabindex="0"></span><script type="math/tex" id="MathJax-Element-3">{\rm Q}+, {\rm Q}-</script></tex-math></inline-formula> are, respectively, the I-VCO and Q-VCO quadrature outputs along with their complementary signals. In an effort to improve the spectral purity of QVCO signals, transformer coupling has been introduced by some authors <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref8" id="context_ref_8_2">[8]</a>, <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref9" id="context_ref_9_2">[9]</a>; however, at UHF frequencies, this can result in large silicon area. Hence, in this paper, a pMOS folded-cascode structure is used to provide the mutual input excitations for quadrature generation. Compared with parallel quadrature coupling, series coupling <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref10" id="context_ref_10_2">[10]</a> using a cascode topology reduces the QVCO phase noise. In addition, the phase-noise performance is enhanced due to the lower hot carrier-induced <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref11" id="context_ref_11_2">[11]</a> drain current noise of the pMOS device in the folded cascode. A grounded center-tapped inductor <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref5" id="context_ref_5_2">[5]</a>â<a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref6" id="context_ref_6_2">[6]</a><a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref7" id="context_ref_7_2">[7]</a> is used for the LC-tank instead of the supply center-tapped inductor in most QVCO structures. This has the advantage of reduced power supply noise and improved linearity in a mixed signal application with baseband wireless circuitry. In addition, being ground center tapped, there is reduced substrate leakage currents from the inductor coils. This also results in the QVCO being less prone to self-resonance compared with other topologies with supply center-tapped or floating coils. The tail current source of the VCO-core positive feedback nMOS latch is eliminated to provide additional voltage headroom. Hence, it enjoys the advantage of oscillation under highly scaled supply voltage. Noise due to the tail current source is also avoided as a consequence bringing further performance improvement <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref12" id="context_ref_12_2">[12]</a>. The pMOS current sources biasing the cascode coupling topology also accounts for lower drain current noise compared with nMOS bias current sources. In a pMOS current source, surface states at the nSiâSiO<sub>2</sub> interface results in trapped holes (hopping valence band electrons), but the concentration of trapped holes is lower compared with trapped conduction-band electrons for an nMOS device and, hence, an inherently lower <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">1/f</span><span class="MathJax MathJax_Processing" id="MathJax-Element-4-Frame" tabindex="0"></span><script type="math/tex" id="MathJax-Element-4">1/f</script></tex-math></inline-formula> noise for the pMOS device. In order to further reduce the <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">1/f</span><span class="MathJax MathJax_Processing" id="MathJax-Element-5-Frame" tabindex="0"></span><script type="math/tex" id="MathJax-Element-5">1/f</script></tex-math></inline-formula> noise of the pMOS current sources, pulsed biasing <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref13" id="context_ref_13_2">[13]</a>â<a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref14" id="context_ref_14_2">[14]</a><a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref15" id="context_ref_15_2">[15]</a> through complementary cross coupling is used in this design to release trapped carriers at the nSi-SiO<sub>2</sub> interface. Pulsing the MOSFET overdrive between accumulation and strong inversion would thus free the carriers trapped during strong inversion resulting in reduced <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">1/f</span><span class="MathJax MathJax_Processing" id="MathJax-Element-6-Frame" tabindex="0"></span><script type="math/tex" id="MathJax-Element-6">1/f</script></tex-math></inline-formula> noise. Depletion-mode pMOS (D-PMOS) devices are used as varactor <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref16" id="context_ref_16_2">[16]</a>, <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref17" id="context_ref_17_2">[17]</a> due to the quasi-linear monotonic Capacitance versus voltage (<inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">C</span><span class="MathJax MathJax_Processing" id="MathJax-Element-7-Frame" tabindex="0"></span><script type="math/tex" id="MathJax-Element-7">C</script></tex-math></inline-formula>â<inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">V</span><span class="MathJax MathJax_Processing" id="MathJax-Element-8-Frame" tabindex="0"></span><script type="math/tex" id="MathJax-Element-8">V</script></tex-math></inline-formula>) behavior in this region of operation. Several devices are shunt-connected to increase the composite <i>C</i>â<i>V</i> tuning range. In addition, small-value metalâinsulatorâmetal (MIM) capacitance is augmented in parallel with the D-PMOS array to increase the overall varactor linearity. <a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig1" class="fulltext-link">Fig.Â 1</a> also shows the dc connection polarity of the D-PMOS varactor with gates connected to the dc ground, while, the shorted source, drain and body is connected to a variable positive voltage. In this topology, M5 and M6 constitute the core feedback latch devices of the VCOs. On the other hand, M3 and M4 are the corresponding coupling pMOS devices. The current flowing into the <i>LC</i> tank is only that due to the drain current of the pMOS coupling devices. Since only one set of bias transistors consume drain current from the supply voltage, the operating supply voltage and power consumption can be significantly reduced compared to the conventional cross-coupled regeneration circuit. The cascoding structure also reduces the effect of any injected circuit noise (white noise) from the power supply thus reducing the phase noise in the <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">{1/f^2}(={1/\Delta\omega^2})</span><span class="MathJax MathJax_Processing" id="MathJax-Element-55-Frame" tabindex="0"></span><script type="math/tex" id="MathJax-Element-55">{1/f^2}(={1/\Delta\omega^2})</script></tex-math></inline-formula> region of the spectrum. Some of the white noise at the QVCO outputs (due to QVCO output buffer or driven devices) that are not filtered by the <i>LC</i>-tank would constitute a flat noise floor beyond the <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">{1/f^2}(={1/\Delta \omega^2})</span><span class="MathJax MathJax_Processing" id="MathJax-Element-58-Frame" tabindex="0"></span><script type="math/tex" id="MathJax-Element-58">{1/f^2}(={1/\Delta \omega^2})</script></tex-math></inline-formula> phase-noise spectrum.</p></div><div class="section_2" id="sec3b"><h3>B. The use of pulsed bias technique has resulted in reduced contribution of the flicker noise component (reduced <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">{1/\Delta \omega^3}</span><span class="MathJax MathJax_Processing" id="MathJax-Element-93-Frame" tabindex="0"></span><script type="math/tex" id="MathJax-Element-93">{1/\Delta \omega^3}</script></tex-math></inline-formula> dependency) in the SSB phase-noise spectrum thus illustrating its effectiveness. The oscillator corner frequency for the <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">{1/f^3}(={1/\Delta \omega^3})</span><span class="MathJax MathJax_Processing" id="MathJax-Element-94-Frame" tabindex="0"></span><script type="math/tex" id="MathJax-Element-94">{1/f^3}(={1/\Delta \omega^3})</script></tex-math></inline-formula> phase-noise component is found to be considerably lower than the MOSFET <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">{1/f}</span><span class="MathJax MathJax_Processing" id="MathJax-Element-95-Frame" tabindex="0"></span><script type="math/tex" id="MathJax-Element-95">{1/f}</script></tex-math></inline-formula> device noise corner frequency (which is dominated by the 125 <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">\mu</span><span class="MathJax MathJax_Processing" id="MathJax-Element-96-Frame" tabindex="0"></span><script type="math/tex" id="MathJax-Element-96">\mu</script></tex-math></inline-formula>m/0.12 <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">\mu</span><span class="MathJax MathJax_Processing" id="MathJax-Element-97-Frame" tabindex="0"></span><script type="math/tex" id="MathJax-Element-97">\mu</script></tex-math></inline-formula>m nMOS devices with corner frequency at around several MHz). This is due to the reduction in the dc value of the ISF <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref3" id="context_ref_3_4">[3]</a> as a result of the pulse biasing discussed in <a ref-type="sec" anchor="sec2" class="fulltext-link">SectionÂ II</a>. The flat noise floor (<i>white of phase</i>) of the oscillator is close to â155 dBc/Hz and the overall circuit Q is around 8. As the inductor Q is usually lower than the capacitor Q, the overall resonator Q is dominated by the inductor. Hence, the phase noise, being inversely proportional to the square of the circuit Q <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref1" id="context_ref_1_4">[1]</a>, can be seen to be slightly higher with lower varactor capacitance (tuned by higher <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">{V}_{\rm var})</span><span class="MathJax MathJax_Processing" id="MathJax-Element-98-Frame" tabindex="0"></span><script type="math/tex" id="MathJax-Element-98">{V}_{\rm var})</script></tex-math></inline-formula>. The power dissipated by the QVCO includes two dynamic sources: 1) resistive Q-degraded power dissipation during energy transfer between electric and magnetic fields in the <i>LC</i> tank, and 2) short-circuit power dissipation when nMOS and/or pMOS devices in the supply-to-ground path are momentarily turned on at the same time. In addition, there is a small static power dissipation due to leakage and subthreshold currents in all of the devices including the pMOS varactors. With the pulse-biased topology, the proposed QVCO dissipates a maximum of 2.5 mW within the supply voltage scaling range, which is very attractive for low-power RFID applications.
