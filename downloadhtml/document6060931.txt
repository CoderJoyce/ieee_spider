<div class="section" id="sec1"><div class="header article-hdr"><div class="kicker">		                        SECTION I.</div><h2>Introduction</h2></div><p>Multiband multistandard concepts have gained considerable interest in modern wireless communications systems <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref1" id="context_ref_1_1">[1]</a>–<a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref2" id="context_ref_2_1">[2]</a>
<a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref3" id="context_ref_3_1">[3]</a>
<a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref4" id="context_ref_4_1">[4]</a>. To support a wide set of communication standards and to accommodate different applications in a single device, broadband transceivers are essential and inevitably in demand. A wideband RF receiver front-end architecture constructed by one single path <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref5" id="context_ref_5_1">[5]</a> provides lower cost, area, and power consumption compared to the parallel-path architectures <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref6" id="context_ref_6_1">[6]</a>, <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref7" id="context_ref_7_1">[7]</a>. The single-path wideband concept can also accommodate emerging standards for cognitive radio applications, resulting in efficiency improvement in utilizing scarce spectrum resources.</p><p>One of the major challenges in wideband receivers is the design of a wideband low-noise amplifier (LNA) that is shared among different standards. As the first block in the receiver chain, such an LNA should achieve good impedance matching, high and flat gain, and low noise figure (NF) across a wide frequency band. In addition, good linearity and low area and power consumption LNAs are required for high-performance and low-cost radios.</p><p>Recently, many wideband LNAs in CMOS technology have been reported, including distributed amplifiers <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref8" id="context_ref_8_1">[8]</a> and resistive shunt feedback amplifiers <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref9" id="context_ref_9_1">[9]</a>, <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref10" id="context_ref_10_1">[10]</a>. The former offers superior bandwidth in terms of high power consumption, large area, and deterioration of noise performance, which limits its widespread applications. The latter provides good broadband matching, noise, and gain, but it is hampered by greater power consumption, which makes them unattractive for low-power applications. Other implementations are inductor-based, such as L-degenerated broadband LNAs <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref11" id="context_ref_11_1">[11]</a>. They have good performance in terms of NF and power consumption. However, the use of area consuming on-chip bulky inductors makes them unattractive for use in upcoming wireless low-cost transceivers.</p><p>One of the wideband LNA topologies that has been widely investigated is the common-gate low-noise amplifier (CGLNA). The CGLNA is attractive compared to other topologies as it features wideband input impedance matching. Also, it offers good linearity, stability, and low power consumption. However, its main drawback is the relatively high NF <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref12" id="context_ref_12_1">[12]</a>. This is due to the input matching condition, which restricts a certain value of transconductance to be used that leads to low gain, and hence, high NF. Noise-reduction techniques are used to overcome the disadvantage of the CGLNA configuration <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref12" id="context_ref_12_1">[12]</a>–<a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref13" id="context_ref_13_1">[13]</a>
<a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref14" id="context_ref_14_1">[14]</a>
<a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref15" id="context_ref_15_1">[15]</a>
<a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref16" id="context_ref_16_1">[16]</a>
<a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref17" id="context_ref_17_1">[17]</a>. The gain boosting scheme using negative feedback employing capacitive cross-coupling <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref12" id="context_ref_12_1">[12]</a>, <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref13" id="context_ref_13_1">[13]</a>, dual negative feedback <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref16" id="context_ref_16_1">[16]</a>, and positive-negative feedback <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref17" id="context_ref_17_1">[17]</a> are applied to break the tradeoff between the input matching condition and the NF, which lead to simultaneous reduction in noise and power dissipation. However, reducing the NF below 2 dB is still challenging in CGLNAs.</p><p>In this paper, a wideband differential CGLNA employing multiple feedback is proposed. It uses three feedbacks to add more flexibility in determining the <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">g_{m}</span><script type="math/tex">g_{m}</script></tex-math></inline-formula> of the impedance matching device. This breaks the lower bound of the noise performance and leads to reduction in the NF and increase in the gain. To the best of the authors' knowledge, the proposed LNA achieves the lowest NF and highest gain among CGLNAs reported in the literature while consuming low power. It also avoids the use of bulky inductors resulting in considerable area and cost savings. The presented LNA covers frequency bands for digital video broadcasting (DVB) at 450–850 MHz, global system for mobile communications (GSM) at 900 MHz, and global positioning system (GPS) at 1.2 and 1.5 GHz, providing a practical solution for multistandard applications. This paper is organized as follows. In <a ref-type="sec" anchor="sec2" class="fulltext-link">Section II</a>, existing noise-reduction techniques for the CGLNA using negative and positive feedbacks are discussed. <a ref-type="sec" anchor="sec3" class="fulltext-link">Section III</a> covers the proposed CGLNA, showing detailed analysis for the major LNA parameters. In <a ref-type="sec" anchor="sec4" class="fulltext-link">Section IV</a>, circuit implementation is presented along with simulation results and measurements. Finally, <a ref-type="sec" anchor="sec5" class="fulltext-link">Section V</a> concludes this paper.</p></div>

The single-ended model of the transconductance boosting structure is shown in <a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig1" class="fulltext-link">Fig. 1(b)</a>. <div class="section" id="sec4"><div class="header article-hdr"><div class="kicker">		                        SECTION IV.</div><h2>Circuit Design and Measurement Results</h2></div><p>The proposed LNA with a voltage gain of 23 dB, 3-dB bandwidth of 1.76 GHz, and a minimum NF of 1.85 dB over the band is implemented. A highly linear voltage buffer is used at the LNA output to drive the 50-<inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">\Omega</span><script type="math/tex">\Omega</script></tex-math></inline-formula> load of the measuring equipment. Coupling capacitors are used between the LNA and the buffer to provide the buffer with separate dc bias. The gain and NF of the buffer are predetermined to de-embed their effect from the overall response to get the LNA response. The total schematic of the LNA with the buffer is shown in <a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig6" class="fulltext-link">Fig. 6</a>. <a ref-type="table" anchor="table2" class="fulltext-link">Table II</a> shows the transistor aspect ratios for the proposed LNA and buffer. In the layout implementation, the transistors are laid out with maximum number of fingers and close to minimum width per finger to minimize the effective series gate resistance to reduce the signal loss and improve the NF specially for the input transistor <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">M_{1}</span><script type="math/tex">M_{1}</script></tex-math></inline-formula>. To reduce the effect of the flicker noise, the lengths of the transistors are increased. The coupling capacitors, employed in the design, are implemented using a MIMCAP device supported by the IBM 90-nm CMOS process, which has a density of 5.8 <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">{\hbox{fF}}\cdot\mu{\hbox {m}}^{2}</span><script type="math/tex">{\hbox{fF}}\cdot\mu{\hbox {m}}^{2}</script></tex-math></inline-formula>. The biasing resistors are implemented using poly resistors. <a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig7" class="fulltext-link">Fig. 7</a> shows a micrograph of the fabricated CGLNA/buffer with a chip size of 1 mm × 1 mm (including the pads). The core LNA area is 0.03 <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">{\hbox {mm}}^{2}</span><script type="math/tex">{\hbox {mm}}^{2}</script></tex-math></inline-formula></p><p>The core LNA consumes 1.4 mA from a 2-V supply while the buffer consumes 10 mA. The LNA is encapsulated in a micro leadframe (QFN) open package, where the dc biases and input RF signal are applied/monitored using an FR-4 printed circuit board (PCB). The output signal is monitored using a ground–signal–ground–signal–ground (G–S–G–S–G) differential probe. This measurement setup is used to evaluate the performance of the LNA including the PCB traces and packaging effect. Baluns are used at the input and output for single-ended to differential signal conversion. <a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig8" class="fulltext-link">Figs. 8</a> –<a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig9" class="fulltext-link"></a> <a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig10" class="fulltext-link">10</a> show the post layout simulated and the measured input reflection coefficient <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">S_{11}</span><script type="math/tex">S_{11}</script></tex-math></inline-formula>, voltage gain, and NF, respectively. They are plotted versus RF input frequency up to 2 GHz after de-embedding the effect of the output buffer. The measured <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">S_{11}</span><script type="math/tex">S_{11}</script></tex-math></inline-formula> is lower than −10 dB from 100 MHz up to 1.8 GHz (<a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig8" class="fulltext-link">Fig. 8</a>). The voltage gain is measured to be 23 dB in the passband with an upper 3-dB frequency of 1.77 GHz (<a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig9" class="fulltext-link">Fig. 9</a>). The measured minimum NF is 1.85 dB at 0.7 GHz with degraded performance at the lower and higher frequencies because of the flicker noise and LNA bandwidth limitation, respectively. Across the entire 3-dB bandwidth, the average measured NF is 2 dB. These measurements show that the proposed LNA achieves an almost constant NF from 100 MHz up to its upper cutoff frequency. This property does not exist in many reported broadband LNAs, which achieve a minimum NF at a specific frequency and have a much higher NF across the entire frequency range. The input-referred intercept point, <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">{\rm IIP}_{3}</span><script type="math/tex">{\rm IIP}_{3}</script></tex-math></inline-formula>, for the proposed wideband CGLNA is measured using a two-tone test for a 300-MHz operating frequency. The two tones are applied with the same amplitude and a frequency offset of 10 MHz. An <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">{\rm IIP}_{3}</span><script type="math/tex">{\rm IIP}_{3}</script></tex-math></inline-formula> value of −2.85 dBm is obtained, as shown in <a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig11" class="fulltext-link">Fig. 11</a>. As a measure of the stability of the proposed CGLNA, <a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig12" class="fulltext-link">Fig. 12</a> shows a plot for the stability factor <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">K</span><script type="math/tex">K</script></tex-math></inline-formula> that is calculated based on the <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">S</span><script type="math/tex">S</script></tex-math></inline-formula>-parameters <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">({\hbox{LNA}}+{\hbox{buffer}})</span><script type="math/tex">({\hbox{LNA}}+{\hbox{buffer}})</script></tex-math></inline-formula> through the following expression:<disp-formula id="deqn22" class="display-formula"><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">K={1-\vert S_{11}\vert^{2}-\vert S_{22}\vert^{2}+\vert S_{11}S_{22}-S_{12}S_{21}\vert^{2}\over 2\vert S_{12}\vert\vert S_{21}\vert}.\eqno{\hbox{(22)}}</span><script type="math/tex; mode=display">K={1-\vert S_{11}\vert^{2}-\vert S_{22}\vert^{2}+\vert S_{11}S_{22}-S_{12}S_{21}\vert^{2}\over 2\vert S_{12}\vert\vert S_{21}\vert}.\eqno{\hbox{(22)}}</script></tex-math><span class="formula"><span class="link">View Source</span><img aria-describedby="qtip-0" style="display:inline;" title="Right-click on figure or equation for MathML and additional features." data-hasqtip="0" class="qtooltip moreInfo" alt="Right-click on figure for MathML and additional features." src="/assets/img/icon.support.gif" border="0" height="20" width="24" /><span class="tex tex2jax_ignore" style="display:none;">K={1-\vert S_{11}\vert^{2}-\vert S_{22}\vert^{2}+\vert S_{11}S_{22}-S_{12}S_{21}\vert^{2}\over 2\vert S_{12}\vert\vert S_{21}\vert}.\eqno{\hbox{(22)}}</span></span></disp-formula></p><p><a ref-type="table" anchor="table3" class="fulltext-link">Table III</a> compares the performance of the proposed CGLNA with that of the state-of-the-art wideband LNAs around the same frequency range. The power consumption reported is of the core LNA only. As shown in this table, the proposed broadband LNA with multiple feedback provides the minimum NF among common-gate (CG) topologies. It also has low power consumption and high gain when compared to previously reported wideband LNAs.</p></div>
<div class="section" id="sec5"><div class="header article-hdr"><div class="kicker">		                        SECTION V.</div><h2>Conclusion</h2></div><p>An inductorless broadband CGLNA employing noise reduction has been proposed in this paper. The LNA relies on multiple feedbacks to fully decouple the tradeoff between noise and input power matching. The theory shows that the proposed approach reduces the lower limit of the noise performance of the previously reported CGLNAs, allowing for an NF around 1.4 dB. Measurements of a fabricated prototype in 90-nm CMOS technology show a voltage gain of 23 dB with a 3-dB bandwidth of 1.77 GHz. A minimum NF of 1.85 dB and an <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">{\rm IIP}_{3}</span><script type="math/tex">{\rm IIP}_{3}</script></tex-math></inline-formula> of −2.85 dBm are also measured. The measured NF is lower than the best reported NF of CGLNAs. The LNA consumes 2.8 mW from a 2-V supply.</p></div>
