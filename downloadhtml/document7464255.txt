<div class="section" id="sec1"><div class="header article-hdr"><div class="kicker">		                        SECTION I.</div><h2>Introduction</h2></div><p>Aradio frequency identification (RFID) system is a versatile entity in many day-to-day life scenarios. RFID reader
 antennas operating in far-field (FF) region are very useful in inventory management, livestock supervision, traffic
 toll collection, and many other applications <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref1" id="context_ref_1_1">[1]</a>. The circularly polarized
 far-field operation is more preferred for reader antennas <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref2" id="context_ref_2_1">[2]</a> because it
 ensures detection of the RFID tag irrespective of the orientation. Broadband frequency of operation 
<a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref3" id="context_ref_3_1">[3]</a>, <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref4" id="context_ref_4_1">[4]</a> is also required for RFID
 reader antennas to receive detuned signals from tags. The conventional desktop reader antennas perform near-field (NF)
 operation, whereas handheld reader antennas perform far-field operation. The far-field tags are well known for their
 read distance. However, they fail in the presence of metals and liquid products <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref5" id="context_ref_5_1">[5]
</a>, <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref6" id="context_ref_6_1">[6]</a>. Hence, near-field tags are used. In a pharmaceutical industry,
 where both near-field tags (liquid drugs) and far-field tags (for other drugs) are employed, multiservice antennas are
 required for detecting both types of tags. The same requirement is present in inventory tracking, logistics
 management, and paper pulp industry <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref7" id="context_ref_7_1">[7]</a>. Hence, the choice of near-field and
 far-field operations is mandatory for fixed RFID readers for applications involving metals and liquid products. This
 ensures simplicity, reduced cost, and size of the system.</p><p>Integration of circularly polarized far-field operations and inductive coupling near-field operations in an RFID
 reader antenna at UHF 900Â MHz band will result in a high-performance and orientation-independent multiservice
 RFID system. There are patents on RFID readers with near- and far-field operations <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref8" id="context_ref_8_1">
[8]</a>â<a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref9" id="context_ref_9_1">[9]</a>. Here, the near-field and far-field operations are either
 in different directions or in different frequency ranges. Hence, the possibility of employing multiservice tags 
<a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref10" id="context_ref_10_1">[10]</a> is getting limited. In <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref6" id="context_ref_6_1">[6]</a> and 
<a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref11" id="context_ref_11_1">[11]</a>, efficient antenna solutions for RFID readers with near- and far-field
 operations at UHF range are introduced, but they have a linearly polarized far field and the near field is induced
 using a loop technique, which results in a nonuniform magnetic field.</p><p>A compact UHF multiservice RFID reader antenna is proposed in this letter for near-field and far-field operations.
 Various antennas with slot radiator have been proposed <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref12 13 14 15 16" id="context_ref_12 ref13 ref14 ref15 ref16_1">[12]
</a>â<a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref17" id="context_ref_17_1">[17]</a>. Among these antennas, the proposed antenna has the compact
 size. Also, the proposed antenna exhibits higher bandwidth performance along with better read range compared to 
<a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref12" id="context_ref_12_1">[12]</a> and <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref14 15" id="context_ref_14 ref15_1">[14]</a>â
<a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref16" id="context_ref_16_1">[16]</a>. The novel aspects of the proposed work are usage of integrated NF-FF
 radiator for multiservice operation and achieving circular polarization as well as uniform magnetic field generation
 over the same antenna plane.</p></div>
