<div class="section" id="sec1"><div class="header article-hdr"><div class="kicker">		                        SECTION I.</div><h2>Introduction</h2></div><p>In recent years, radio frequency identification (RFID) system in ultrahigh frequency (UHF) band is getting considerable attention because it can provide a long reading range. An RFID tag is made up of an RFID chip and a tag antenna. Many applications require RFID tags to be mounted on electrically metallic objects. Dipole antennas are the most commonly used in RFID tags of UHF band <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref1" id="context_ref_1_1">[1]</a>, <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref2" id="context_ref_2_1">[2]</a>. However, when this kind of antenna is mounted on the metallic object, its reading range is severely degraded owing to the impedance mismatching between the antenna and the chip. Although several designs based on patch antennas or planar inverted-F antennas (PIFAs) have been proposed for RFID tags mountable on metallic objects <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref3" id="context_ref_3_1">[3]</a>–<a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref4" id="context_ref_4_1">[4]</a><a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref5" id="context_ref_5_1">[5]</a>, they give a narrow impedance bandwidth. Moreover, some broadband tag patch antennas mountable on metallic objects have also been reported <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref6" id="context_ref_6_1">[6]</a>–<a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref7" id="context_ref_7_1">[7]</a><a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref8" id="context_ref_8_1">[8]</a>. However, all these reported antennas may suffer from inconvenient mounting on metallic objects because of their high profile (thickness <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview"></span><span class="MathJax" id="MathJax-Element-1-Frame" tabindex="0"><nobr><span class="math" id="MathJax-Span-1" role="math" style="width: 0.904em; display: inline-block;"><span style="display: inline-block; position: relative; width: 0.784em; height: 0px; font-size: 111%;"><span style="position: absolute; clip: rect(1.384em 1000.72em 2.526em -999.997em); top: -2.219em; left: 0.003em;"><span class="mrow" id="MathJax-Span-2"><span class="mo" id="MathJax-Span-3" style="font-family: MathJax_Main;">≥</span></span><span style="display: inline-block; width: 0px; height: 2.225em;"></span></span></span><span style="display: inline-block; overflow: hidden; vertical-align: -0.197em; border-left: 0px solid; width: 0px; height: 1.003em;"></span></span></nobr></span><script type="math/tex" id="MathJax-Element-1">\geq</script></tex-math></inline-formula>2 mm). From the point of view of facilitating installation, tag antennas that have low-profile structures with thickness of less than 1.0 mm are desirable in some applications such as notebooks.</p><p>The previous work in <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref9" id="context_ref_9_1">[9]</a> adopts meandered patches to design very low-profile tag antennas mountable on metallic planes. These antennas have a simple feeding structure and are fed by direct connection to tag chip. However, their impedance bandwidths determined from 10-dB return loss are about 12–14 MHz only, which are not large enough to cover the bandwidth (902–928 MHz) of UHF band RFID system in North America. In order to improve the bandwidth performance, a novel feeding structure using a capacitively coupled feed to excite a meandered patch antenna is presented in this letter. This feeding structure can be applied to control the real and the imaginary parts of the antenna impedance individually to achieve conjugate match with the various values of the chip impedances. Bending a long patch into four half-wavelength radiating elements makes the surface currents of all radiating elements in phase, thus making possible an enhanced antenna gain for the proposed antenna. Design considerations of the proposed antenna are described, and obtained results are presented and discussed.</p></div>

<div class="section" id="sec4"><div class="header article-hdr"><div class="kicker">		                        SECTION IV.</div><h2>Conclusion</h2></div><p>A novel low-profile meandered patch antenna with a capacitively coupled feed has been successfully implemented. Results demonstrate that the reading range is over 5 m in free space and approximately 4 m on a large metal plate. Also, the proposed antenna achieves an impedance bandwidth of 43 MHz, which covers the required bandwidth (902–928 MHz) of UHF band RFID system in North America.</p></div>
