<div class="section" id="sec1"><div class="header article-hdr"><div class="kicker">		                        SECTION I.</div><h2>Introduction</h2></div><p>With the vigorous development of wireless communications, indoor positioning systems have found widespread
 applications in medical surveillance, assisted navigation of buildings, smart wearable devices, domotics, warehouse
 management, and many other RFID systems. It also posts new demands on antennas with excellent characteristics such as
 compact, low-profile, and lightweight structures for easy installation. To accommodate different indoor positioning
 networks, such as wireless sensor networks and wireless personal area networks simultaneously, dual-band antennas are
 preferred for indoor positioning systems because they can reduce the number of antenna units and save the limited
 space effectively.</p><p>Several dual-band antennas have been developed for indoor positioning systems 
<a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref1 2 3 4 5 6" id="context_ref_1 ref2 ref3 ref4 ref5 ref6_1">[1]</a>â<a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref7" id="context_ref_7_1">[7]</a>.
 Monopole, slot, and spiral structures were adopted for hybrid ultrahigh frequency/ultrawideband (UHF/UWB) tag
 antennas in <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref1" id="context_ref_1_1">[1]</a> and <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref2" id="context_ref_2_1">[2]</a>. A wide
 frequency band was achieved through a crossed exponentially tapered-slot structure for UWB tag antenna 
<a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref3" id="context_ref_3_1">[3]</a>. These designs have realized a dual-band operation with simple and
 low-profile structure, but they all have an omnidirectional radiation pattern. In order to improve the positioning
 accuracy of targets with metallic surface and also for the benefit of human health, conductor-backed dual-band
 antennas are desired for indoor positioning systems.</p><p>Various conductor-backed dual-band antennas were designed in <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref4 5" id="context_ref_4 ref5_1">[4]</a>â
<a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref6" id="context_ref_6_1">[6]</a>. A low-profile S-shaped tag antenna was proposed in 
<a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref4" id="context_ref_4_1">[4]</a>. Recently, a low-profile wearable antenna made from textiles was presented
 in <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref5" id="context_ref_5_1">[5]</a> with dual-bandÂ electromagnetic band-gap material to reduce
 backside radiation. Cavity-backed passive RFID tag antenna applicable to metallic objects was discussed in 
<a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref6" id="context_ref_6_1">[6]</a>. Although dual-band performance and directional radiation pattern were
 obtained, these low-profile antennas still had a relatively narrow bandwidth, especially at the high-frequency band.
 In order to extend the upper bandwidth for a better localization resolution, a slot dipole structure was designed in 
<a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref7" id="context_ref_7_1">[7]</a>. A wide bandwidth was achieved, but the antenna was made of two layers of
 Rogers substrate stacked with artificial magnetic conductor structure, which made the configuration complicated and
 costly. In order to simplify the structure and reduce the fabrication cost, some cheap designs with better performance
 are required for indoor positioning systems.</p><p>This letter presents a compact, low-profile, dual-port, and dual-band antenna made of FR4 substrate. For the upper

<a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig1" class="fulltext-link">Fig. 1</a>, the U-shaped strip is printed around the edge of the top horizontal FR4

<a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig1" class="fulltext-link">Fig. 1</a>(c). An ellipse-shaped dipole is printed on the top horizontal FR4
 substrate, and the dipole is fed two symmetrical strips printed on the bottom layer of the top FR4 substrate. The
 strip lines are then connected to the vertical strips printed on the vertical FR4 substrate. The two parallel strips
 on both sides of the vertical substrate are, respectively, connected to the ground and microstrip line printed on both
 sides of the bottom FR4 substrate.</p><p>Since the dual-band antenna is designed for active tag powered by battery, its input impedance is designed to 50
 Î© instead of the usual conjugate of chip impedance. Transmission lines are designed below the ground for easy
 integration with other RF circuits. In order to alleviate the mutual coupling between the antenna and nearby circuits,
 two vertical walls are added along the narrow edges connecting both horizontal substrates, which also help to increase
 the antenna gain, especially at the UHF band.</p></div><div class="section_2" id="sec2b"><h3>B. Antenna Analysis</h3><p>It is relatively easy to understand the operation of the PIFA at the UHF band. Therefore, this section is mainly
 concerned with the wideband dipole for the upper band. In order to realize a broadband performance for a low-profile
 conductor-backed dipole, a wideband feeding mechanism is necessary. There were feeding structures in 
<a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref8" id="context_ref_8_2b">[8]</a> and <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref9" id="context_ref_9_2b">[9]</a> proposed for wideband
 applications. In <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref8" id="context_ref_8_2b">[8]</a>, a differential feeding structure with a transition from
 microstrip line to parallel strip line was proposed for direct coupling. Broadband performance was obtained, but the
 differential feeding strip requires a certain height for impedance transformation. Meanwhile, an L-shaped feeding
 strip was presented in <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref9" id="context_ref_9_2b">[9]</a> for electromagnetic coupling. Low profile and
 simple configuration were achieved, but the asymmetrical feeding structure can yield a bandwidth of 36% and
 relatively higher cross polarization.</p><p>Combining the advantages of these two different feeding mechanisms in <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref8" id="context_ref_8_2b">[8]</a>
 and <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref9" id="context_ref_9_2b">[9]</a> together, a novel low-profile feeding structure is proposed here with
 wide impedance bandwidth. As shown in <a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig1" class="fulltext-link">Fig. 1</a>(c), it consists of two L-shaped
 probes arranged symmetrically at the center of the elliptical dipole. The feeding strips can be divided into two
 parts: coupling strip and transmission line. The horizontal parts printed on the bottom layer of the top substrate are
 used to excite the ellipse patches through electromagnetic coupling. The vertical parts printed on both sides of the
 vertical substrate are a basic parallel strip line.</p><p>Compared to the direct coupling in <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref8" id="context_ref_8_2b">[8]</a>, electromagnetic coupling method
 provides more flexibility to achieve a wide impedance bandwidth. Meanwhile, the length of the vertical strips can be
 greatly shortened to realize a low profile because they are no longer needed for impedance transformation 
<a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref8" id="context_ref_8_2b">[8]</a>. Compared to the single L-probe in <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref9" id="context_ref_9_2b">[9]
</a>, a differential voltage excitation can yield a uniform current distribution and effectively suppress the cross
 polarization. Furthermore, the symmetrical feeding structure is easier to realize a wide impedance bandwidth.</p><p>For the radiating patch, elliptical shape is chosen for better impedance match because the elliptical boundary
 changes smoothly without a sharp discontinuity. As a result, the antenna impedance curve can maintain a relative flat
 response in a wide frequency range. For a compact and low-profile configuration, the patch, strip, and ground overlap
 with each other confined within a small volume. Therefore, the imaginary part of the input impedance appears
 capacitive. In order to cancel the extra capacitance, elliptical slot is etched on the ellipse patch to reduce the
 overlapped area and expose the coupling strip to provide more inductance for wideband impedance matching. Based on the
 above considerations, good impedance matching is achieved for the UWB band, as shown in 
<a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig2" class="fulltext-link">Fig. 2</a>.

Port Isolation Studies</h3><p>To improve the port isolation between two ports, lumped inductors are loaded into the two arms of the U-shaped PIFA.
 Their inductance value is optimized to be 9.5 nH. The inductor is basically a low-pass filter that can provide a
 relatively small reactance at the UHF band, but a larger reactance at the upper band. This can effectively separate
 the PIFA from the UWB antenna at the UWB band. It is observed from <a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig5" class="fulltext-link">Fig. 5</a> that
 the port isolation without loaded inductors is only 12 dB at 4.8 GHz, while it becomes 25 dB with the loaded
 inductors. The isolation is better than 21 dB over the entire upper band.</p><p>To understand the mechanism, the current distribution along the antenna at 4.8 GHz is depicted in 
<a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig4" class="fulltext-link">Fig. 4</a>. It is clear that the high-frequency current can be easily coupled from
 the ellipse to PIFA without inductors, while the coupling becomes negligible with the load inductors. On the other
 hand, the loaded inductors cause a small frequency shift at the UHF band, as shown in <a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig5" class="fulltext-link">
Fig. 5</a>. This is because the elliptical patch is too small at the UHF band and little energy can be coupled to
 the ellipse patch with or without the inductors.
