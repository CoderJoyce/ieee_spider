<div class="section" id="sec1"><div class="header article-hdr"><div class="kicker">		                        SECTION I.</div><h2>Introduction</h2></div><p>Recently, significant efforts have been dedicated to the integration of UHF mobile RF identification (RFID) systems <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref1" id="context_ref_1_1">[1]</a>. By integrating the entire system into a microdimensioned chip, more coupling effects among the components can be expected <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref2" id="context_ref_2_1">[2]</a>, <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref3" id="context_ref_3_1">[3]</a>. Transmitter (TX) leakage is a prime example. Consider that the reader transmits a continuous wave (CW), powering up the tag to function. Reader TX signals are at very high levels; they may affect reader receiver (RX) processes, where the RX receives low-level backscatters transmitted from the tag.</p><p>At the chip level, much hard work is required to alleviate the TX leakage problem since we do not even see what happens inside the microdimensioned chip. in any case, a much higher level of TX leakage signal may cause nontrivial noise, flowing to the RX path; thus, the RX process can be retarded. Many attempts have been made in the area of RF integrated circuits (ICs) <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref4" id="context_ref_4_1">[4]</a>, and many anti-TX leakage signal configurations on the RF frontend have been introduced <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref5" id="context_ref_5_1">[5]</a>. in this paper, we focus on the fundamental noise problem in the TX. Our foremost focus is the voltage-controlled oscillator (VCO). the TX leakage signal level originates from the generated RF signal, using an up-conversion mixer connected with the VCO. Since phase noise frequently occurs within oscillated signals from the VCO, phase noise within the TX leakage signal can also be amplified after the power amplifier (PA). the received tag data can be swallowed. If original phase noise in the VCO can be reduced, we can expect a less invasive RF frontend process. This is called a high-performance VCO, and it focuses on phase-noise reduction.</p><p>Several VCO design bottlenecks were considered. the complementary metalâoxide semiconductor (CMOS) technology-based VCO is very complicated when integrated with other circuits. in our industrial experience, monolithic VCO implementation has the poor quality factor of monolithic inductors, high flicker noise, and resonant frequency band limits due to poor varactor diode details. As far as we know, when moving up to the higher frequency area, it becomes very difficult to control noise. in this sense, phase noise is a key factor in determining the VCO performance grade. in a passive UHF RFID, the frequency offset between the CW and backscatters should only be 40 kHz. If phase noise in the TX leakage signal exists at 40 kHz, RX sensitivity becomes higher and a comparatively lower level of backscatters can be badly affected. This is a serious problem. the proposed VCO can oscillate TX signals at much lower phase-noise levels in an UHF RFID band (860â960 MHz). This performance is verified with the measurement results. VCO performance verification tests were carried out with designed RF IC transceiver in the UHF RFID band.</p><p>In <a ref-type="sec" anchor="sec2" class="fulltext-link">SectionÂ II</a>, we study the link budget in terms of a practical case where many readers exist and we slow conditions that readers may have. We consider and calculate the link margin in order to clarify the targeted specification (e.g., the desired phase-noise level). in <a ref-type="sec" anchor="sec3" class="fulltext-link">SectionÂ III</a>, we detail some important transceiver design considerations, components, and simulated and measured performances. in <a ref-type="sec" anchor="sec4" class="fulltext-link">SectionÂ IV</a>, we present a prototype VCO and its specifications. Finally, we present a measured performance of the proposed RF IC transceiver, including the proposed VCO, in <a ref-type="sec" anchor="sec5" class="fulltext-link">SectionÂ V</a>.</p></div>

<div class="section" id="sec3"><div class="header article-hdr"><div class="kicker">		                        SECTION III.</div><h2>Issued Components in Transceiver Design</h2></div><div class="section_2" id="sec3a"><h3>A. Frequency Synthesizer</h3><p>In Korea, a passive UHF mobile RFID band allows 19 channels for readers that use frequency hopping; however, it allocates 15 channels for channel interaction among related applications <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref6" id="context_ref_6_3a">[6]</a>â<a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref7" id="context_ref_7_3a">[7]</a><a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref8" id="context_ref_8_3a">[8]</a>.</p><p>For covering a hopping range of 910.15â913.95 MHz, a frequency synthesizer is needed. the frequency synthesizer is based on sigmaâdelta fractional-<inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">N</span><script type="math/tex">N</script></tex-math></inline-formula> architecture (see <a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig4" class="fulltext-link">Fig.Â 4</a>), consisting of an 8/9 prescaler, a pulse (<inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">N</span><script type="math/tex">N</script></tex-math></inline-formula>) counter, a swallow (<inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">S</span><script type="math/tex">S</script></tex-math></inline-formula>) counter, a sigmaâdelta modulator, a phase frequency detector (PFD), a charge pump, and a loop filter. Lna</h3><p>In direct-conversion topology, the transmitted LO signal leaks through the antenna coupler to the RX input. This effect is known as âTX leakageâ or âself-mixingâ <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref11" id="context_ref_11_3b">[11]</a>.</p><p>The TX leakage signal appearing at the input of the LNA is mixed with the LO signal, thereby generating a dc component at the output of the mixer. It contaminates the desired signal, and down-mixer output flows to the dc offset canceller (DCOC).</p><p>The RFID reader receives the signal reflected (back-scattered) from the tags in a short distance (a few meters) and, thus, the dc-offset problem is more severe than in other wireless systems.</p><p>This problem can be exacerbated when the LO signal leaks to the antenna are radiated and are subsequently reflected from fast-moving objects back to the LNA. This offset is known to be extended from zero to a few hundred hertz <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref5" id="context_ref_5_3b">[5]</a>.</p><p>Since a combination of a strong interferer and TX leakage signals flow into the LNA, LNA P1dB specifications may be stringent, requiring high linearity. A key to obtain high linearity is high input current. To increase <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">gm</span><script type="math/tex">gm</script></tex-math></inline-formula>, both <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">{V}_{\rm gs}</span><script type="math/tex">{V}_{\rm gs}</script></tex-math></inline-formula> and <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">{V}_{\rm ds}</span><script type="math/tex">{V}_{\rm ds}</script></tex-math></inline-formula> must be increased. A common gate (CG) LNA (see <a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig6" class="fulltext-link">Fig.Â 6</a>) can provide a higher <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">{V}_{\rm ds}</span><script type="math/tex">{V}_{\rm ds}</script></tex-math></inline-formula> and <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">{V}_{\rm gs}</span><script type="math/tex">{V}_{\rm gs}</script></tex-math></inline-formula>. We used a CG LNA, although its noise performance is inferior to a common source LNA, it is better for reverse isolation performance <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref12" id="context_ref_12_3b">[12]</a>. Simulated P1dB and measured P1dB values per input current level variation can be seen in <a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig7" class="fulltext-link">Fig.Â 7(a)</a> and <a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig7" class="fulltext-link">(b)</a>, respectively.

Other Configurations in TX</h3><p>The differential output signal of the level converter is filtered by a TX low-pass filter (LPF); hence, the out-of-channel spurious power can be reduced. the cutoff frequency of the TX LPF may need to be varied per reader bit rates (40â160 kb/s) if we need to cover the ISO 18000-6 Type Generation 2 tags, as well as Type B tags <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref6" id="context_ref_6_3c">[6]</a>.</p><p>The TX LPF has been implemented by three <i>RC</i> stages and its cutoff frequency varies from 80 to 320 kHz. Subsequently, the output of the LPF is mixed with the LO signal by the up-conversion mixer and then converted to the RF signal.</p></div></div>
<div class="section" id="sec4"><div class="header article-hdr"><div class="kicker">		                        SECTION IV.</div><h2>VCO Design and Implementation</h2></div><div class="section_2" id="sec4a"><h3>A. VCO Design Issues</h3><p>Several design methodologies have been used to decrease the bias noise and contribute to the overall VCO phase-noise modification. They are as follows.</p><p>The first method implies flicker noise elimination in the tail current source by replacing the n-channel metalâoxide field-effect transistor (NMOS) with a p-channel metalâoxide field-effect transistor (PMOS) <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref14" id="context_ref_14_4a">[14]</a>. PMOS flicker noise is less than NMOS, typically resulting in the hot carrier effect. for deep-submicrometer CMOS technology, hot electron noise (white noise) is the dominant source that determines noise performance <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref15" id="context_ref_15_4a">[15]</a>. At a similar bias level, minimum noise figure <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">({\rm NF}_{\min})</span><script type="math/tex">({\rm NF}_{\min})</script></tex-math></inline-formula> in NMOS is larger than that in PMOS <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref16" id="context_ref_16_4a">[16]</a>.</p><p>The second method calls for a bigger scale of tail current while maintaining the width/length (W/L) ratio. It implies that, when the current source is setup as a long channel field-effect transistor (FET), it is possible to avoid the conversion of flicker noise to the close-in phase noise of nearby oscillation frequency <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref17" id="context_ref_17_4a">[17]</a>.</p><p>The third method implies that, when the tuning sensitivity is comparatively lower, the varactor AMâFM conversion can be avoided. Constant capacitance of varactor diode attempts to reduce the AMâFM possibility so that overall tuning sensitivity can be lower while required tuning range can be covered; thus, a switched capacitor array is used <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref18" id="context_ref_18_4a">[18]</a>.</p><p>A last method implies the replacement of p-n diode varactors with MOS capacitors. MOS varactors have a steep <i>C (V)</i> characteristic, and with such a steep characteristic, the resulting oscillation frequency strongly depends on the oscillation amplitude. If not properly taken into account, this effect can limit the tuning range of the oscillator and increase the level of the <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">1/f3</span><script type="math/tex">1/f3</script></tex-math></inline-formula> phase noise <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref19" id="context_ref_19_4a">[19]</a>. Throughout these four methods, a PMOS shows less flicker noise. Thus, we only employed PMOS.
</p><div class="figure figure-full" id="fig10"><!--          Workaround for combined images.Eg.- 1000116 Fig. 5        --><div class="img-wrap"><a href="/mediastore/IEEE/content/media/22/4668610/4657395/4657395-fig-10-large.gif"><img src="/mediastore/IEEE/content/media/22/4668610/4657395/4657395-fig-10-small.gif" alt="Figure 10" /><div class="zoom" title="View Larger Image"></div></a></div><div class="figcaption"><b class="title">Fig.Â 10. </b><fig><p>VCO configuration. (a) VCO supply voltage regulator. (b) Differential Colpitts VCO.</p></fig></div><p class="links"><a href="/document/4657395/all-figures" class="all">View All</a></p></div><p></p><p>To improve the more common mode of rejection, the VCO power supply voltage should be regulated <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref20" id="context_ref_20_4a">[20]</a>. the proposed VCO configuration can be seen in <a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig10" class="fulltext-link">Fig.Â 10</a>. the reference voltage <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">{V}_{\rm ref}</span><script type="math/tex">{V}_{\rm ref}</script></tex-math></inline-formula> is equal to <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">{I}_{\rm PTAT}\times{R}_{\rm ref}</span><script type="math/tex">{I}_{\rm PTAT}\times{R}_{\rm ref}</script></tex-math></inline-formula>, where <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">{I}_{\rm PTAT}</span><script type="math/tex">{I}_{\rm PTAT}</script></tex-math></inline-formula> denotes a proportional to absolute temperature (PTAT) reference current. Therefore, <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">{V}_{\rm ref}</span><script type="math/tex">{V}_{\rm ref}</script></tex-math></inline-formula> is proportional to temperature. As a consequence, the VCO power supply is increased when temperature rises. By increasing the VCO power supply, a higher current can be set to compensate for phase-noise degradation. An external capacitor is required to filter the noise from the PTAT and the regulator out. the proposed differential Colpitts VCO is realized by connecting two identical single-ended Colpitts, suppressing the even-mode oscillation, but not the odd-mode oscillation [see <a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig10" class="fulltext-link">Fig.Â 10(b)</a>]. Overall, the design process was carried out considering the mobile RFID environment, where a low phase-noise characteristic should be clarified for higher accuracy of communication. We need to once again emphasize this fact since it is difficult to satisfy such a characteristic at a 40-kHz offset frequency; for us, phase-noise reduction was the most important goal.</p><p>Now, consider that flicker noise, which is closely related to a phase-noise characteristic, may cause phase noise over a <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">1/{f}^{3}</span><script type="math/tex">1/{f}^{3}</script></tex-math></inline-formula> area where oscillation frequency is nearby <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref19" id="context_ref_19_4a">[19]</a>. the flicker noise in the tail current source generates low-frequency random amplitude modulation (AM), which will be converted to frequency modulation (FM) due to the capacitance of the varactor <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref19" id="context_ref_19_4a">[19]</a>.</p><p>A VCO is designed to obtain optimum phase noise at <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">1/f</span><script type="math/tex">1/f</script></tex-math></inline-formula>, <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">1/{f}^{2}</span><script type="math/tex">1/{f}^{2}</script></tex-math></inline-formula>, and <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">1/{f}^{3}</span><script type="math/tex">1/{f}^{3}</script></tex-math></inline-formula> area. Even though we use a much bigger size of a current source or PMOS implementation, it is hard to expect perfect flicker noise termination; thus, we replace the MOS with poly resistors to set the VCO current to remove the bias device noise source <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref21" id="context_ref_21_4a">[21]</a>. the VCO current is programmable by varying the resistor using three digital control bits. Cross-coupled VCOs are widely used because they are easily produced and they facilitate oscillation startup. However, high sensitivity to the common mode causes worse phase-noise performance when a common mode noise occurs.</p><p>A differential Colpitts VCO reports improved phase-noise performance compared to the cross-coupled VCO <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref22" id="context_ref_22_4a">[22]</a>. Due to the differential nature, the ability of the feedback capacitor <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">{C}_{fb}</span><script type="math/tex">{C}_{fb}</script></tex-math></inline-formula> to provide negative conductance is relatively small compared to a single-end Colpitts VCO. It is essential to lower the VCO gain <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">({K}_{\rm vco})</span><script type="math/tex">({K}_{\rm vco})</script></tex-math></inline-formula> for low phase-noise design. However, the VCO tuning range is proportional to <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">{K}_{\rm vco}</span><script type="math/tex">{K}_{\rm vco}</script></tex-math></inline-formula>. To cover the entire UHF RFID band (860â960 MHz), a 5-bit digital coarse tune was applied for wide VCO frequency coverage. the p-n diode varactors instead of MOS varactors were used in the resonance tank.</p></div><div class="section_2" id="sec4b"><h3>B. <div class="section" id="sec6"><div class="header article-hdr"><div class="kicker">		                        SECTION VI.</div><h2>Conclusion</h2></div><p>The emphasis of this paper has been an adaptive solution of VCO design that considers reader collision and shortened interrogation range through the adverse phase noise that exists in a mobile RFID environment. the RF transceiver for the mobile RFID environment has been integrated into 0.35-<inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">\mu{\hbox{m}}</span><script type="math/tex">\mu{\hbox{m}}</script></tex-math></inline-formula> technology with a size of <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">{\hbox{3}}\times {\hbox{3}}\ {\hbox{mm}}^{2}</span><script type="math/tex">{\hbox{3}}\times {\hbox{3}}\ {\hbox{mm}}^{2}</script></tex-math></inline-formula>. the VCO has been designed in the transceiver at a size of <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">{\hbox{650}}\times {\hbox{850}}\ {\mu{\hbox{m}}}^{2}</span><script type="math/tex">{\hbox{650}}\times {\hbox{850}}\ {\mu{\hbox{m}}}^{2}</script></tex-math></inline-formula>. To achieve a better phase-noise specification at a 40-kHz offset, the flicker noise source, such as an active bias device, is replaced by a poly resistor. in addition, only PMOS is used for the main VCO core pair. Using a digital coarse tuner, <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">{K}_{\rm vco}</span><script type="math/tex">{K}_{\rm vco}</script></tex-math></inline-formula> is reduced for better phase-noise performance. A PTAT-based regulator is also used for a VCO power supply in order to have stable phase-noise performance with temperature variation. the VCO phase noise is â106 dBc/Hz at a 40-kHz frequency offset and the tuning range is 28.2%. the proposed differential Colpitts VCO meets the mobile RFID phase-noise specification while it presents a good PFTN FOM of 1.93 dB.</p></div>
