<div class="section" id="sec1"><div class="header article-hdr"><div class="kicker">		                        SECTION I.</div><h2>Introduction</h2></div><p>Over THE PAST decades, radio-frequency identification (RFID) technology has been refined to provide simple and economical solutions for wireless identification and data access. It is widely applied in diverse fields, such as supply chain management, access and control, healthcare, and real-time location system (RTLS) <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref1" id="context_ref_1_1">[1]</a>. Needs for wireless sensing and the success of RFID now inspire attempts to integrate a variety of sensors and RFID technologies. Promising markets, like cold chain management, military surveillance and tracking, and environmental monitoring, make âRFID sensingâ one of the most interesting RFID-based applications.</p><p>Piggyback modulation was proposed to implement RFID sensing and this technique enables the addition of small, inexpensive sensors to existing UHF RFID passive tags <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref2" id="context_ref_2_1">[2]</a>. The radar cross section (RCS) of a UHF RFID tag is used to measure the backscattered power from a tag, and it has been commonly treated as a scalar parameter for amplitude shift-keying (ASK) modulation. Analog and digital real-time signals overlaid on UHF RFID application-specific integrated circuit (ASIC) signals were captured, and a waterfall curve showed read rate reduction with increased piggybacking signal strength and modulation frequency; the viability of the piggyback modulation and proof-of-principle experiments has been established <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref3" id="context_ref_3_1">[3]</a>. In this work, the proposed RFID sensor prototype is a UHF RFID passive tag attached with a sensor coupling module. Both the amplitude and the phase of the signal backscattered from the RFID sensor prototype are modulated. A quasi-4-QAM demodulation scheme uses both amplitude and phase to detect signals modulated by two independent sources, a tag ASIC and a sensor. Thus, when the piggyback modulation is overlaid onto existing UHF RFID passive tags through wireless coupling, this technique could be used for sensing quantities such as temperature, humidity, and pressure.</p><p>This paper is an extended version of <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref4" id="context_ref_4_1">[4]</a>. In this paper, the vector backscattered signal is measured, analyzed, and demodulated in ASK and quasi-4-QAM. The principle and design of the piggyback modulation is illustrated in <a ref-type="sec" anchor="sec2" class="fulltext-link">SectionÂ II</a>. <a ref-type="sec" anchor="sec3" class="fulltext-link">SectionÂ III</a> addresses the basics of backscattering from a passive tag and the analysis of the vector backscattered signal with piggyback modulation. The measurement setup for both amplitude and phase of the backscattered signal and the experimental results of signals with piggyback modulation are presented in <a ref-type="sec" anchor="sec4" class="fulltext-link">SectionÂ IV</a>. The analysis, the numerical simulation, and the bit-error-rate (BER) performance comparison of the ASK demodulation and quasi-4-QAM demodulation are reported in <a ref-type="sec" anchor="sec5" class="fulltext-link">SectionÂ V</a>.</p></div>
<div class="section" id="sec2"><div class="header article-hdr"><div class="kicker">		                        SECTION II.</div><h2>Piggyback Modulation</h2></div><p>For a passive tag, the ASIC of the tag switches its input impedance between two states to modulate the RCS of the tag antenna. Piggyback modulation technique operates based on the same principle: the voltage controlled sensor coupling module manipulates the backscattering wave by varying the effective input impedance of the tag antenna. <a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig1" class="fulltext-link">Fig.Â 1</a> shows the block diagram of the concept of piggyback modulation.

<p></p><p>For a sensor set that has its own power source and storage device, the proposed RFID sensor, an integration of a commercial passive RFID tag and a sensor, could acquire sensing information without being illuminated by a reader, although the data transmission still relies on the backscattering modulation, like typical passive tags. This design is also applicable for sensors that can harvest power from the reader or consume no power at all. The key of the piggyback modulation is the coupling mechanism that provides a link to the reader through the existing communication channel for sensor data transmission. This coupling is implemented by attaching a coupling loop to a tag with a small gap. The coupling characterized by two-port <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">S</span><span class="MathJax MathJax_Processed" id="MathJax-Element-1-Frame" tabindex="0"><nobr><span class="math" id="MathJax-Span-1" role="math"><span style="display: inline-block; position: relative; width: 0.003em; height: 0px; font-size: 111%;"><span style="position: absolute;"><span class="mrow" id="MathJax-Span-2"><span class="mi" id="MathJax-Span-3" style="font-family: MathJax_Math-italic;">S<span style="display: inline-block; overflow: hidden; height: 1px; width: 0.063em;"></span></span></span></span></span></span></nobr></span><script type="math/tex" id="MathJax-Element-1">S</script></tex-math></inline-formula>-parameters depends on not only the material, the shape, and the size of the coupling loop, but also on the gap between the coupling loop and tag <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref3" id="context_ref_3_2">[3]</a>. The backscattering modulation is performed by two independent sources: the tag ASIC through a conductive connection and the additional sensor through the wireless coupling.</p><p>The sensor coupling module consists of a coupling loop and a PIN diode modulator, as indicated in <a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig2" class="fulltext-link">Fig.Â 2</a>. The PIN diode modulator is comprised of a capacitor, an inductor, and a PIN diode. The input impedance of the PIN diode modulator, Z<sub>PM</sub>, is controlled by the input voltage source <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">{V}_{S}</span><span class="MathJax MathJax_Processed" id="MathJax-Element-2-Frame" tabindex="0"><nobr><span class="math" id="MathJax-Span-4" role="math"><span style="display: inline-block; position: relative; width: 0.003em; height: 0px; font-size: 111%;"><span style="position: absolute;"><span class="mrow" id="MathJax-Span-5"><span class="msubsup" id="MathJax-Span-6"><span style="display: inline-block; position: relative; width: 1.144em; height: 0px;"><span style="position: absolute; clip: rect(3.186em 1000.78em 4.207em -999.997em); top: -4.021em; left: 0.003em;"><span class="texatom" id="MathJax-Span-7"><span class="mrow" id="MathJax-Span-8"><span class="mi" id="MathJax-Span-9" style="font-family: MathJax_Math-italic;">V<span style="display: inline-block; overflow: hidden; height: 1px; width: 0.183em;"></span></span></span></span><span style="display: inline-block; width: 0px; height: 4.027em;"></span></span><span style="position: absolute; top: -3.841em; left: 0.604em;"><span class="texatom" id="MathJax-Span-10"><span class="mrow" id="MathJax-Span-11"><span class="mi" id="MathJax-Span-12" style="font-size: 70.7%; font-family: MathJax_Math-italic;">S<span style="display: inline-block; overflow: hidden; height: 1px; width: 0.003em;"></span></span></span></span><span style="display: inline-block; width: 0px; height: 4.027em;"></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></nobr></span><script type="math/tex" id="MathJax-Element-2">{V}_{S}</script></tex-math></inline-formula>, which represents the sensor signal.

<p></p><p>By attaching the coupling loop section of the module to the tag antenna with a gap, the effective input impedance seen through from the tag antenna depends on the ASIC and the sensor coupling module. The modulation overlay is both physical and logical as shown in <a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig2" class="fulltext-link">Figs.Â 2</a> and <a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig3" class="fulltext-link">3</a>. Additional processing is needed for the double-layer design fabrication. However, this technique is relatively simple, compact, inexpensive, and easily attached to tags compared with redesign the ASIC. For example, the coupling loop could simply be a sticker that is positioned over the existing RFID tag antenna. The two signal sources, a tag ASIC and a sensor, operate independently and simultaneously to modulate the backscattered wave through a sensor coupling module. Without any circuit-level integration, wireless sensing is realized by using the existing channel between RFID readers and tags to piggyback the sensor data stream on top of the identification data stream.

<p></p><p>RCS is a scalar value with dimension of area, and it is proportional to the square of the amplitude of the signal scattered from an object. RCS is a measure of how detectable an object is, and it is modulated to carry information from a passive tag. Previous measurement results showed the amplitude of the backscattering is piggyback modulated for four levels <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref3" id="context_ref_3_2">[3]</a>. The outcome of the scalar piggyback modulation proved this principle and drove us to explore the potential of the vector backscattering for better performance.</p></div>

Measurement Setup</h3><p>In the measurement setup, a vector signal generator, a vector signal analyzer, a circulator, and an antenna are used; the antenna and the target under test are placed in an anechoic chamber. The schematic diagram of the setup is indicated in <a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig6" class="fulltext-link">Fig.Â 6</a>.

<p></p><p>To have a true backscattering measurement, one single antenna is used to complete both signal transmission and reception. The transmitting/receiving antenna and the target, RFID tag or RFID sensor prototype, are placed in an anechoic chamber to reduce the unwanted reflections from the environment. One three-port circulator connects the vector signal generator, transmitting/receiving antenna, and the vector signal analyzer to provide two separate paths for transmitted and received signals with isolation around 25 dB.</p><p>To capture the two different backscattering vectors from a RFID tag and the four different backscattering vectors from a RFID sensor prototype, the tag ASIC needs to be awakened by receiving a Gen2 standard forward link signal that contains request commands. Rather than using a standard UHF RFID reader to awake and communicate with a tag, a vector signal generator is used to generate the Gen2 standard forward link signal. In addition, it solves the problem of measuring signals at one fixed carrier frequency, since standard readers are designed to produce carrier frequency randomly hopping around within 902â928 MHz, which is the ISM band of UHF RFID systems in North America.</p><p>The phase measurement is viable because the vector signal generator and the vector signal analyzer are synchronized with rms phase error less than 1Â°.</p></div><div class="section_2" id="sec4b"><h3>B. Experimental Results</h3><p>Here, we present the experimental results of the vector backscattered signals with piggyback modulation in the time domain. The commercial passive UHF RFID tag AD-222 loaded with the Monza 2 ASIC is used. The carrier frequency is set and fixed at 915 MHz, which is the central frequency of the UHF RFID operation band in North America. The RF output power is set to +15.0dBm in the vector signal generator. <div class="section" id="sec5"><div class="header article-hdr"><div class="kicker">		                        SECTION V.</div><h2>Analysis of Demodulation Schemes</h2></div><p>With the piggyback modulation, two independent data sets are simultaneously modulated on the same carrier wave received by a reader. The current standard demodulation scheme is valid for the signal carrying ASIC data only. Therefore, the demodulation scheme has to be modified to detect both the ASIC data and the sensor data. Here, we present and analyze the three different demodulation schemes: the split-two-level ASK demodulation, the four-level ASK demodulation, and the quasi-4-QAM demodulation.</p><p>In the split-two-level ASK demodulation, the data detection is individually processed for the ASIC data stream and the sensor data stream. The ASIC signal demodulation is by amplitude threshold detection with a threshold <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">T_{\rm ASIC}</span><span class="MathJax MathJax_Processing" id="MathJax-Element-92-Frame" tabindex="0"></span><script type="math/tex" id="MathJax-Element-92">T_{\rm ASIC}</script></tex-math></inline-formula> performed while the embedded sensor signal is treated as noise. Due to the interference from the sensor signal, the bit error rate (BER) of the ASIC data stream is dramatically deteriorated when increasing frequency or amplitude of the piggybacked signal <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref3" id="context_ref_3_5">[3]</a>. To demodulate the sensor signal, a low-pass filter is required to recover the waveform of the sensor signal, which normally has lower bit rate. <p></p><p>Typical digital modulation schemes have all the symbols uniformly located around the origin of the <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">I</span><span class="MathJax MathJax_Processing" id="MathJax-Element-113-Frame" tabindex="0"></span><script type="math/tex" id="MathJax-Element-113">I</script></tex-math></inline-formula>â<inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">Q</span><span class="MathJax MathJax_Processing" id="MathJax-Element-114-Frame" tabindex="0"></span><script type="math/tex" id="MathJax-Element-114">Q</script></tex-math></inline-formula> plane, while in backscatter modulation all of the symbols are pulled far from the origin. This is because the received signal contains not only the return link signal but also the leakage forward link signal. Therefore, to achieve the same BER performance, a much higher signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) is required in the backscattering modulation compared with other more typical digital modulation schemes.</p><p>BER comparisons of these two demodulation schemes, in which the symbol constellation is measured by the actual experimental results, are done by numerical simulations. <div class="section" id="sec6"><div class="header article-hdr"><div class="kicker">		                        SECTION VI.</div><h2>Conclusion</h2></div><p>Vector backscattered signals of the proposed RFID sensor prototype are measured, analyzed, and demodulated in both ASK and quasi-4-QAM schemes. The principle of the piggyback modulation and the basics of the vector backscatter from a passive tag are addressed. The measurement setup for both amplitude and phase of the backscattered signal is presented. Using experimental results, we conduct the analysis and the numerical simulation of the BER performance comparison for different demodulation schemes. The results show the performance of quasi-4-QAM demodulation is superior to the four-level-ASK demodulation. Future work could include integrating actual sensor signals, designing the actuation of the sensor, developing the synchronization of the sensor signal with the ASIC backscattered signal, improving the coupling loop design, and applying this technique to different tags.</p></div>
