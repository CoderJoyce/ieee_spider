<div class="section" id="sec1"><div class="header article-hdr"><div class="kicker">		                        SECTION I.</div><h2>Introduction</h2></div><p>An Antenna subsystem is presented for the RFID <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref1" id="context_ref_1_1">[1]</a>, <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref2" id="context_ref_2_1">[2]</a> reader applications at 2.45 GHz in the near-zone tag detection. The potential applications are illustrated by the scenario in <a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig1" class="fulltext-link">Fig. 1</a>, which widely appears in the department stores <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref3" id="context_ref_3_1">[3]</a>. Here the products’ tags move on the transportation belt through a detection zone. To reduce the electromagnetic (EM) interferences arising from the nearby metal structures, the near-field focus antennas (NFAs) <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref4" id="context_ref_4_1">[4]</a>, <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref5" id="context_ref_5_1">[5]</a> have been proposed to produce narrow spot beams in the target region. As shown in <a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig1" class="fulltext-link">Fig. 1</a>, the NFAs confine the radiation energy in a small region at the cost of sacrificing the possibility of wide area coverage, which limits their applicable flexibility such as in the example where the width of transportation belt is doubled.
</p><div class="figure figure-full" id="fig1"><!--          Workaround for combined images.Eg.- 1000116 Fig. 5        --><div class="img-wrap"><a href="/mediastore/IEEE/content/media/7727/7024204/7086020/7086020-fig-1-large.gif"><img src="/mediastore/IEEE/content/media/7727/7024204/7086020/7086020-fig-1-small.gif" alt="Figure 1" /><div class="zoom" title="View Larger Image"></div></a></div><div class="figcaption"><b class="title">Fig. 1. </b><fig><p>Scenario of a near-field focus problem for RFID applications.</p></fig></div><p class="links"><a href="/document/7086020/all-figures" class="all">View All</a></p></div>
<p></p><p>To resolve the above mentioned limitations, the multi-beam NFAs <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref6" id="context_ref_6_1">[6]</a> have been also proposed, where the beam spots are created and distributed uniformly within the target zone through a beam forming network (BFN). The beams are switched from one spot to another. It provides a certain degree of flexibility at the cost of sophisticated BFN design especially when the sidelobe level (SLL) suppression is involved.</p><p>A programmable RFID <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref7" id="context_ref_7_1">[7]</a> antenna subsystem is thus proposed and examined in this letter. This subsystem is consisted of an antenna array and a set of phase shifters as well as a control processor as in the smart antennas <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref8" id="context_ref_8_1">[8]</a>, <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref9" id="context_ref_9_1">[9]</a>. The unique feature of this subsystem is that the NF spot beams can be produced arbitrarily through a computer program embedded in the processor. Not only the RFID system can be implemented in a relatively arbitrary environment, but also the antenna beam can be adaptively formed according to the mechanism in the computer program. As a result, not only the EM interferences can be relatively suppressed, the scan area can be widened in a sequential beam steering order over the detection zone. An antenna prototype is examined to validate the feasibility.</p></div>

They are described in the following.

<p></p><p>In building up the system, a set of discrete NF focus beams are first determined by using the phases of the phase shifters. Here the locations, as well as the number, of NF focus beams can be arbitrarily selected. The outputs of phase shifters at every state of controlling bits are measured, tabled and stored in MCU as illustrated by <a ref-type="table" anchor="table1" class="fulltext-link">Table I</a>. The required phases are mapped by the controller's bits in binary expressions. The spot beam at a selected focus location is formed by a conjugated phase scheme for simplification of demonstration in this letter, where the states of phase shifters are chosen to have almost equal phases of radiations at the focused point. In this scheme, each column of array antennas is first excited to radiate fields whose phases of co-polarized component are measured at focus point. The required phases are found by selecting the closest values on <a ref-type="table" anchor="table1" class="fulltext-link">Table I</a>, where the corresponding control bit expressions are recorded. The procedure is performed for every column of the array. <a ref-type="table" anchor="table2" class="fulltext-link">Table II</a> illustrates the found overall binary expressions for a few exampled beams. One may also use an optimization procedure such as genetic algorithm <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref13" id="context_ref_13_2c">[13]</a> to synthesize the desired NF beams if the SLL suppression is required.

<div class="section" id="sec4"><div class="header article-hdr"><div class="kicker">		                        SECTION IV.</div><h2>Conclusion</h2></div><p>This letter presents the architecture of a programmable antenna subsystem for the RFID reader applications. The subsystem may be programed to provide adaptive beam steering capability in the target zone of a near-field application. It allows one to realize the NF focus antenna concept with the flexibility for RFID system to make adaptation to the environments that might be changed. The future work will implement the EM optimization technique in the management computer such that the RFID reader antennas may adaptively optimize the radiation in terms of contoured patterns <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref16" id="context_ref_16_4">[16]</a> to fit the application environments for an optimum reception.</p></div>
