<div class="section" id="sec1"><div class="header article-hdr"><div class="kicker">		                        SECTION I.</div><h2>Introduction</h2></div><p>During recent years, the antennas designed with transparent materials have obtained great interest from researchers. This is due to the low thickness and lightweight structure of the transparent material compared with the conventional designs. In some applications, such as radio-frequency identification (RFID), the miniaturization of the device is a significant challenge for engineers. Presently, RFID technology is widely developed in inventory management and asset tracking systems due to its high intelligence and identifying speed <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref1" id="context_ref_1_1">[1]</a>.</p><p>Several frequencies are reported for RFID applications, such as the ultra-high frequency (UHF) band <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref2" id="context_ref_2_1">[2]</a>, microwave industrial-scientific-medical (ISM) band <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref1" id="context_ref_1_1">[1]</a>, and UHF and microwave bands <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref3" id="context_ref_3_1">[3]</a>. These days, in order to achieve compact size antennas with high data rates, the microwave bands of 2.45 GHz and 5.8 GHz are under special attention in many applications, such as warehouses; supply chains <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref4" id="context_ref_4_1">[4]</a>, <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref5" id="context_ref_5_1">[5]</a>; and access controls <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref6" id="context_ref_6_1">[6]</a>, <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref7" id="context_ref_7_1">[7]</a>.</p><p>There are various designs based on the passive RFID tags connected to an integrated-circuit (IC) chip with complex input impedance in order to receive the energy from the reader <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref2" id="context_ref_2_1">[2]</a>, <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref8" id="context_ref_8_1">[8]</a>. The active RFID tags <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref9" id="context_ref_9_1">[9]</a> are applied on a battery-powered circuit which has no need to be energized by the reader to transmit the signal <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref10" id="context_ref_10_1">[10]</a>.</p><p>Most of the previous works on the RFID antennas are performed based on the nontransparent material with relatively high thickness and no ability to implement on the surface. In order to overcome these drawbacks, the transparent thin film with one conductive layer and one substrate could be utilized. Since there is just one conductive and one dielectric layer, CPW-fed and probe-fed techniques could be used in the antenna design.</p><p>In <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref11" id="context_ref_11_1">[11]</a>, a single UHF band probe-fed transparent antenna for RFID applications is investigated using the printed conductive polymer on the polyethylene terephthalate (PET) substrate. In the design, a modified polymer is used to increase the conductivity of the material; however, it increased the visibility to the human eye. In addition, a slight deviation from the exact position of the feed line will significantly decrease the performance of the antenna. To avoid this drawback, the CPW-fed technique could be utilized, owing to ease of fabrication and implementation <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref12" id="context_ref_12_1">[12]</a>.</p><p>The AgHT-8 thin film has high transparency of above 80% to human eyes. In addition, the research on this material shows good adaptation with green technology <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref13" id="context_ref_13_1">[13]</a>. There is some research reported on the AgHT-8 transparent antenna working in UHF and microwave bands, most of which presents a broadband or an ultrawideband design <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref14" id="context_ref_14_1">[14]</a>, <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref15" id="context_ref_15_1">[15]</a>. There is a lack of research on dual-band transparent antennas.</p><p>In this letter, a dual-band CPW-fed transparent antenna in the ISM band with operating frequencies of 2.45 GHz and 5.8 GHz for RFID tags is presented.
