<div class="section" id="sec1"><div class="header article-hdr"><div class="kicker">		                        SECTION I.</div><h2>Introduction</h2></div><p>The use of rf identification (RFID) tags as elementary nodes of a wireless sensor network is not new <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref1" id="context_ref_1_1">[1]</a>â<a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref2" id="context_ref_2_1">[2]</a>
<a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref3" id="context_ref_3_1">[3]</a>
<a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref4" id="context_ref_4_1">[4]</a>. Indeed, RFID technology allows to easily build very dense networks with no-power consumption nodes and at a reasonable cost, not comparable to other technologies. However, in most of the related applications, the sensor network is basically used to extend the working range of the RFID systems by collecting the ID codes and other static information from the nodes and by sending such data to the processing center.</p><p>On the contrary, different kinds of sensor networks use more sophisticated and expensive wireless sensors as nodes. The exchanged information will be the actual measured values from the spatially distributed sensors. Such networks afford more challenging issues where the cooperative monitoring of physical parameters (including environment hostile to humans) is critical; environmental monitoring, target localization, automatic diagnostic systems, surveillance, and fire detection are only a few of the possible examples <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref5" id="context_ref_5_1">[5]</a>â<a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref6" id="context_ref_6_1">[6]</a>
<a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref7" id="context_ref_7_1">[7]</a>
<a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref8" id="context_ref_8_1">[8]</a>. Nevertheless, apart from the RFID case, the wireless interface of the sensors strongly impacts upon the costs, thus limiting the diffusion of such networks. Indeed, many different wireless technologies are mature for the transmission of data measured by generic sensors, much more than the inexpensive RFID. Compared with RFID technology, Wi-fi, Bluetooth, general packet radio service (GPRS), Universal Mobile Telecommunications System (UMTS), and global system for mobile communications (GSM) guarantee wider transmission distances and higher bit rates, as well as a larger amount of exchangeable data.</p><p>
</p><div class="figure figure-full" id="fig1"><!--          Workaround for combined images.Eg.- 1000116 Fig. 5        --><div class="img-wrap"><a href="/mediastore/IEEE/content/media/22/4909499/4806174/4806174-fig-1-large.gif"><img src="/mediastore/IEEE/content/media/22/4909499/4806174/4806174-fig-1-small.gif" alt="Figure 1" /><div class="zoom" title="View Larger Image"></div></a></div><div class="figcaption"><b class="title">Fig.Â 1. </b><fig><p>Simplified scheme of the designed RFID S-tag. The value measured from a generic sensor is used in input, and its quantized version is transmitted by means of a proper set of IDs.</p></fig></div><p class="links"><a href="/document/4806174/all-figures" class="all">View All</a></p></div>
Unluckily, the benefit/cost ratio of such technologies is not always appropriate for the addressed applications. Thus, when a capillary diffusion of physical sources is needed, a cheaper wireless technology is desirable. Moreover, the easy interfaceability with the Internet could be another added value.<p></p><p>On such a basis, the integration of wireless sensor networks with RFID-based sensor systems appears to be the most practicable way. Ultra high-frequency (UHF) RFID technology, in fact, is quite inexpensive (passive RFID tags are as cheap as few euro-cents). It is also naturally compatible with Internet <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref9" id="context_ref_9_1">[9]</a> and it allows a reading range adequate for many applications. It is worth mentioning that some UHF RFID tags with embedded sensor of temperature or of pressure are already available on the market <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref10" id="context_ref_10_1">[10]</a> and very often they are inexpensive and rather accurate; nevertheless different kinds of more complex sensors cannot be easily integrated. For this reason, a general-purpose solution would be attractive.</p><p>In this study, a cost-effective UHF sensor tag (S-tag) is presented and extensively validated. It can be connected to a generic sensor and can transmit toward the RFID reader the measured parameters.</p><p>This paper is organized as follows. <a ref-type="sec" anchor="sec2" class="fulltext-link">SectionÂ II</a> refers to the working principle and the prototype design of the S-tag. <a ref-type="sec" anchor="sec3" class="fulltext-link">SectionÂ III</a> discusses the results related to the application of the S-tag to different typologies of sensors. Conclusions are then reported in <a ref-type="sec" anchor="sec4" class="fulltext-link">SectionÂ IV</a>.</p></div>
<div class="section" id="sec2"><div class="header article-hdr"><div class="kicker">		                        SECTION II.</div><h2>UHF RFID S-Tag</h2></div><p>
</p><div class="figure figure-full" id="fig2"><!--          Workaround for combined images.Eg.- 1000116 Fig. 5        --><div class="img-wrap"><a href="/mediastore/IEEE/content/media/22/4909499/4806174/4806174-fig-2-large.gif"><img src="/mediastore/IEEE/content/media/22/4909499/4806174/4806174-fig-2-small.gif" alt="Figure 2" /><div class="zoom" title="View Larger Image"></div></a></div><div class="figcaption"><b class="title">Fig.Â 2. </b><fig><p>Example of temperature data transmission through a four-ID S-tag. The binary symbol at the S-tag input is used to discriminate the IDs to be transmitted. Vice versa, the information about whether or not an ID has been received by the RFID allows a straightforward symbol reconstruction. The difference between measured and received temperature value is due to the quantization.</p></fig></div><p class="links"><a href="/document/4806174/all-figures" class="all">View All</a></p></div>
This section focuses on the architecture of the proposed S-tag in the UHF range (865â870 MHz) and on the details of both working principles and practical realization.<p></p><p>The proposed S-tag is a passive device that can be connected to a generic sensor and can transmit, when interrogated, the measured physical parameter. The basic idea consists of: 1) designing an RFID tag provided of a wired input and coherently 2) making a well-defined digital output (in our prototype, it is the simple binary conversion of the measured data) available on the sensor. When the tag and sensor are connected one to another and are interrogated, the measured value can be read by a standard UHF RFID system and interpreted through an RFID middleware. In such a way, the system takes advantage of the standard RFID technology, preserving most of its peculiarities and maintaining the compatibility with all the devices already available and standardized worldwide <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref10" id="context_ref_10_2">[10]</a>.</p><p>Many different approaches could be followed in order to implement such a device. The direct writing on the tag memory of the data, for instance, is one of the possibilities. However in this study, a simplified, but effective approach, called âmulti-IDâ here, has been used. The realized S-tag, schematically represented in <a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig1" class="fulltext-link">Fig.Â 1</a>, samples the value measured by the sensor and transmits in real time a combination of different ID codes. Each combination corresponds to a different level so that the quantized waveform of the input will be naturally received by a standard RFID reader. More specifically, the device can associate a different ID code to each informative âbitâ of the quantized real-time value in input. Hence, a certain number of IDs is mandatory in order to effectively transmit the measured value.</p><p>The multi-ID approach can be practically implemented by following at least two different strategies. On the one hand, the âmultichipâ tags strategy, with RFID tags consisting of only one antenna and many chips, each one with its own ID code (see <a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig1" class="fulltext-link">Fig.Â 1</a>). On the other hand, the âmultitagâ strategy, where each ID is transmitted with a different tag controlled by appropriate microwave circuits. Both of them have been realized and tested by the authors. The strongest differences to be pointed out regard the size, much smaller in the multichip S-tag, and the throughput, significantly greater in the multitag one. For the sake of brevity, here we omit any further detail and we refer hereinafter to the multitag S-tag.</p><p>
</p><div class="figure figure-full" id="fig3"><!--          Workaround for combined images.Eg.- 1000116 Fig. 5        --><div class="img-wrap"><a href="/mediastore/IEEE/content/media/22/4909499/4806174/4806174-fig-3-large.gif"><img src="/mediastore/IEEE/content/media/22/4909499/4806174/4806174-fig-3-small.gif" alt="Figure 3" /><div class="zoom" title="View Larger Image"></div></a></div><div class="figcaption"><b class="title">Fig.Â 3. </b><fig><p>Illustrative scheme of the implemented S-tag.</p></fig></div><p class="links"><a href="/document/4806174/all-figures" class="all">View All</a></p></div>
In <a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig1" class="fulltext-link">Fig.Â 1</a>, for instance, the data measured by a sensor is used to control a set of integrated RF switches that select the appropriate IDs to be transmitted. Actually, as the S-tag is designed as a general-purpose device, the sensor signal must be preventively quantized through an <i>ad-hoc</i> circuit embedded in the sensor itself. The sensor is supposed to work in the range 35 Â°Câ42 Â°C and to be provided with a 4-bit binary digital output (15 valid combinations), guaranteeing a resolution of 0.5 Â°C. The measured temperature, which in the example of <a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig2" class="fulltext-link">Fig.Â 2</a>, is assumed to be 38.3 Â°C, after the quantization and the digitalization, will correspond to the binary symbol â0110â that will, hence, become the input signal of the S-tag. Consequently, the internal logic of the S-tag will enable the transmission of the two IDs associated to the second and third bit (those ending, respectively, in 752 and 753 in the figure), and will disable the transmission of the others. Hence, when an RFID reader interrogates the S-tag, it receives back only the enabled IDs, which are automatically associated to the logic state â1.â The nonreceived IDs, instead, are associated to the logic state â0.â The so-reconstructed symbol is then reconverted, resulting in temperature value of 38.5 Â°C, slightly different from the actually measured one because of the quantization effect. It is evident that, in this scenario, the transmission of the symbol â0000â could signify either the absence of the S-tag or a measured temperature under the lower threshold (35 Â°C in this case). To avoid any ambiguity, this symbol is not considered as valid. In such a way, the reading of at least one of the four IDs is a necessary and sufficient condition to ensure the presence of the S-tag.</p><p>In <a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig3" class="fulltext-link">Fig.Â 3</a>, the illustrative scheme of the realized S-tag is sketched. If humidity remains under the threshold for a time superior to 10 s (in order to ignore eventual undesired spikes), a humidifier is remotely activated for a prefixed time. In <a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig6" class="fulltext-link">Fig.Â 6</a>, the humidity rate values (both actual and received) are reported in a certain time interval. It can be observed that the server has exactly reconstructed the transmitted signal (apart from a slight and expected difference due to the quantization) and correctly activated the humidifier for a time of 4 s (pre-fixed), allowing an evident increase of the humidity rate up to a value close to the saturation. The suitability of the proposed S-tag to work in wireless sensor network nodes is thus clearly demonstrated.</p><p>The results proposed here show the S-tag capability to effectively transmit data measured by different kinds of sensors towards standard RFID readers. However, a strong research effort is still necessary in order to make the S-tag of more general interest. In fact, in the present form, its effective application is possible in all cases where the size of the cabinet hosting the sensor is at least as large as the S-tag. This is true in many contexts of practical interest; electrical energy consumption reader devices, industrial oven temperature control, and wheel pressure data transmission in cars are some of the issues the authors are faced with.</p><p>Nevertheless, in many other applications where the RFID-based data transmission is desirable, small-sized sensors hardly compatible with the current dimensions of the S-tag are used. Hence, the reduction of the S-tag size will be the next design step, so as to give full usability and versatility to the proposed device.</p><p>In addition to the miniaturization issue, the S-tag power supply is another key point. In order to feed the UHF switches and the control logic IC, the S-tag needs extra power. Since most sensors are provided with their own power supply, the solution currently adopted is to let the S-tag get power from the sensor itself. Though this strategy is satisfactory in several practical applications, alternative and more flexible solutions must be explored. In view of the realization of a monolithic IC, which should integrate UHF switches, control logic, and RFID chips, the opportunity to use the power of the RFID-reader signal, as it is for standard passive RFID tags will be studied.</p></div>
<div class="section" id="sec4"><div class="header article-hdr"><div class="kicker">		                        SECTION IV.</div><h2>Conclusions</h2></div><p>In this paper, a novel general purpose multi-ID and cost-effective RFID tag (S-tag) for sensor data transmission has been presented. When connected to the digital output of a generic sensor, regardless of the kind of measured parameter, the S-tag will transmit the sensor value by using standard RFID technology.</p><p>Results related to the application of the S-tag in very stressful conditions have been presented, demonstrating the good agreement between transmitted and received data. Moreover, examples of the use of the S-tag in very simple wireless sensor networks for temperature and humidity remote control have been reported and its good performance highlighted.</p><p>The very low cost of the solution, the compatibility with the traditional RFID technology, as well as its easy interfacing to generic sensors make the S-tag a good candidate for enhancing RFID-based wireless sensor network capabilities.</p></div>
