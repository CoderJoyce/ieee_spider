<div class="section" id="sec1"><div class="header article-hdr"><div class="kicker">		                        SECTION I.</div><h2>Introduction</h2></div><p>The 2010 report from the International Air Transport Association (IATA) states that lost baggage costs the air industry more than US $2.5 billion every year <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref1" id="context_ref_1_1">[1]</a>, corresponding to over 25 million mishandled bags globally. About 10% of the mishandlings result from poor barcode read rates. One initiative in the framework of IATA's Baggage Improvement Program was to evaluate the potential of passive UHF RFID technology to replace barcodes and reduce mishandlings. Pilot RFID implementations in several airports around the world revealed that bag-tag read-success rate improved markedly from around 85%â90% with conventional barcode tagging to over 95% with RFID <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref2" id="context_ref_2_1">[2]</a>, <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref3" id="context_ref_3_1">[3]</a>, along with cost saving associated with baggage-handling time reduction.</p><p>RFID is a fast growing technology that enables radio detection and reading of tags associated with items <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref4" id="context_ref_4_1">[4]</a>, without requiring line of sight. RFID tags carry a univocal ID code and a memory that can store relevant baggage tracking information. In the referred pilot airport implementations, passive RFID tags were attached to the classical barcode labels around the baggage handler. In a few cases, these tags got wrapped during the baggage handling, precluding 100% reading score <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref2" id="context_ref_2_1">[2]</a>.</p><p>One interesting alternative is to embed the passive RFID tag within each suitcase wall during its manufacturing process. In this way, instead of the usual disposable tag solution, the embedded tag can be reused and reprogrammed at the airport checkin desk for each travel. This solution is fairly robust and less expensive for the airports. Additionally, the new generation of tags offers reasonable memory size, enabling a wealth of new applications that are already being considered like baggage self-service kiosks, real-time baggage tracking services, etc. <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref5" id="context_ref_5_1">[5]</a>.</p><p>Common UHF RFID tags for airport trials use typical dipole configurations <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref6" id="context_ref_6_1">[6]</a> that, however, do not ensure the best reading score. The objective of this letter is to develop a new dedicated UHF passive tag solution, based on 3-D geometry <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref7" id="context_ref_7_1">[7]</a>, appropriate for embedding into the suitcase wall during the injection molding process, while presenting good omnidirectional detection, acceptable reading range, and full operation across the whole UHF RFID to ensure worldwide interoperability. A folded dipole tag configuration has been presented before for placement in sharp 90Â° corners of boxes <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref8" id="context_ref_8_1">[8]</a>. However, sharp corners are not usual in travel bags, and <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref8" id="context_ref_8_1">[8]</a> does not investigate tag performance for other conformal uses or for other placements to cope with shadowing and wall embedding constrains. Most importantly, the tag from <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref8" id="context_ref_8_1">[8]</a> does not operate in full UHF RFID world band, which prevents worldwide interoperability The design challenge in this letter also includes finding the best tag positioning in the suitcase that minimizes misreading originated by its content, by shadowing from other close or stacked bags or close presence of metal from the bag conveyors or other surfaces.</p><p>In a preliminary study, the developed tag was simply attached to the suitcase surface <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref9" id="context_ref_9_1">[9]</a>. This letter presents the results from the complete study, with the final test results of a fine-tuned RFID tag embedded in the wall of a standard injection-molded suitcase, after passing the industrial integration process. Systematic tests were conducted in a lab baggage scenario using empty and filled suitcases, with 100% reading score. Nonsystematic tests were also carried in a real airport environment with the same 100% reading score. These scores were reached with far less reader antennas than in usual RFID implementations <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref10" id="context_ref_10_1">[10]</a>.</p></div>

<div class="section" id="sec3"><div class="header article-hdr"><div class="kicker">		                        SECTION III.</div><h2>Experimental RFID Performance</h2></div><p>In order to evaluate the actual UHF RFID performance of the proposed tag for airport baggage application, the tag was embedded in the wall of real 40-L suitcases during the injection molding process. <div class="section" id="sec4"><div class="header article-hdr"><div class="kicker">		                        SECTION IV.</div><h2>Conclusion</h2></div><p>A passive folded UHF RFID tag antenna with an orthogonal arm is proposed for embedding into the wall of suitcases, which is adequate for airport automatic luggage tracking and identification. Tag wall embedding ensures tag integrity and enables its reusability in successive user travels, reducing airport costs associated with disposable tags. Results show that the tag radiation pattern is almost omnidirectional when integrated in the suitcase. Codesign of the tag and of its best position and orientation in the suitcase wall contributed to the obtained omnidirectionality and measured detection range of the order of 3 m in open space.</p><p>Experimental tests in a lab environment show that the reading score of the proposed tag is 100% when the suitcases are sliding on a conveyor belt in front of two standard RFID antennas, even when suitcases are filled with travel items and the wall containing the tag is facing down, in the bottom of a two-suitcase stack. The same detection reliability was obtained in a brief nonsystematic evaluation at the cargo area of a real airport. Although isolated misreadings can happen for unpredictable arrangements of suitcases in airport conveyors under full service conditions, still the presented results are enough to validate the proposed concept as an alternative to traditional optical barcode labels and disposable RFID tags attached to the bag handle.</p></div>
