<div class="section" id="sec1"><div class="header article-hdr"><div class="kicker">		                        SECTION I.</div><h2>Introduction</h2></div><p>RFID as a key technology of Internet of Things has aroused widespread attention. However, the environment of RFID
 systems is adversely affected by multiplex reflection interference. Consequently, there is growing concern about how
 to overcome the multiplex effect. The absorbing materials can be used to improve the multipath environment so as to
 establish a highly reliable UHF-RFID system <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref1" id="context_ref_1_1">[1]</a>. However, the size of
 traditional absorbing materials such as wedge-shaped absorbers that are generally used in microwave anechoic chambers 
<a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref2" id="context_ref_2_1">[2]</a> is too large. Radar absorbing materials such as ferrite paint 
<a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref3" id="context_ref_3_1">[3]</a> are relatively heavy in UHF-band. Novel radar absorbing materials such as
 carbon nanomaterials <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref4" id="context_ref_4_1">[4]</a> are relatively complicated on the aspect of
 preparation process. Therefore, these absorbing materials are not suitable for use in UHF-RFID systems. Metamaterial
 absorbers (MAs) <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref5" id="context_ref_5_1">[5]</a> with relatively small volume and light weight are
 prospective to solve the problem for the reliability of the systems.</p><p>MAs have experienced explosive developments since Landy <i>et al.</i> proposed a perfect absorber 
<a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref5" id="context_ref_5_1">[5]</a>. There have been large efforts in designing MAs with multiband, broadband,
 or other characteristics in microwave band <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref6 7 8 9" id="context_ref_6 ref7 ref8 ref9_1">[6]</a>–
<a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref10" id="context_ref_10_1">[10]</a>. However, in low bands such as UHF-band <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref11" id="context_ref_11_1">
[11]</a>, limited to size and extremely narrow bandwidth, the MAs are difficult to be applied to UHF-RFID systems.
 For the narrow bandwidth, using lumped elements can broaden it in low frequency <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref10" id="context_ref_10_1">[10]
</a> to a certain extent. Therefore, in consideration of the size, especially when the RFID systems are restricted
 to indoor environment (such as the RFID system for costume management as mentioned in the later experiment), we should
 reduce bulk and mass of the MAs. Thus, miniaturized metamaterial absorbers (MMAs) 
<a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref12 13" id="context_ref_12 ref13_1">[12]</a>–<a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref14" id="context_ref_14_1">[14]</a> are urgently
 necessary. However, in <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref12" id="context_ref_12_1">[12]</a>, the unit cell is not small enough (0.04

</p><div class="figure figure-full" id="fig7"><!--          Workaround for combined images.Eg.- 1000116 Fig. 5        --><div class="img-wrap"><a href="/mediastore/IEEE/content/media/7727/7855842/7482659/zuo7ab-2574885-large.gif"><img src="/mediastore/IEEE/content/media/7727/7855842/7482659/zuo7ab-2574885-small.gif" alt="Figure 7" /><div class="zoom" title="View Larger Image"></div></a></div><div class="figcaption"><b class="title">Fig. 7. </b><fig><p>Measured absorptivity of the MMA for (a) TE and (b) TM polarization.</p></fig></div><p class="links"><a href="/document/7482659/all-figures" class="all">View All</a></p></div><p></p><p>We can imagine, when both the MA and MMA have a sufficient number of units, the MA will show similar absorption
 property with the MMA [simulated results in <a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig2" class="fulltext-link">Fig. 2</a>(b) can be as a reference].
 Thus, the MA (360 × 360 mm<sup>2</sup>, 9 × 9 units) was not fabricated and measured. On the other hand,
 for RFID applications restricted to extremely compact areas, the MMA with more units can show obviously better
 performance. Thus, we just use the MMA for the following RFID test.</p><p>Here, we construct a simple UHF-RFID system for costume management under a complex environment with the MMAs. 
<a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig8" class="fulltext-link">Fig. 8</a>(a) shows a plan view of the simplified UHF-RFID system. The UHF-RFID tag
 is fixed on a plastic hanger, which is about0.9 m above the floor. The MMAs are fixed on the costume display shelf and
 separated by 1.2 m. The UHF reader antenna that can radiate bidirectionally is located in the middle of the two MMAs.
 The power of the RFID reader is set at 20 dBm to ensure its basic reading ability. In engineering, it is
 generally required that the antenna cannot read tags on the neighboring hangers. The MMA is expected to reduce
 incorrect readings of RFID so as to prevent the phenomenon of reading fleeing codes. Some other absorbing materials
 such as wedge-shaped absorbers and ferrite materials are not suitable for use in the UHF-RFID system due to their
 large bulk or mass.
</p><div class="figure figure-full" id="fig8"><!--          Workaround for combined images.Eg.- 1000116 Fig. 5        --><div class="img-wrap"><a href="/mediastore/IEEE/content/media/7727/7855842/7482659/zuo8ab-2574885-large.gif"><img src="/mediastore/IEEE/content/media/7727/7855842/7482659/zuo8ab-2574885-small.gif" alt="Figure 8" /><div class="zoom" title="View Larger Image"></div></a></div><div class="figcaption"><b class="title">Fig. 8. </b><fig><p>(a) Plan view of a simplified UHF-RFID system. (b) Coordinates used for measuring RSSI.</p></fig></div><p class="links"><a href="/document/7482659/all-figures" class="all">View All</a></p></div><p></p><p>As received signal strength indicator (RSSI) that attenuates with distance is susceptible to multipath effect, which
 can cause incorrect readings of RFID systems, we can evaluate effects of the MMA on the RFID system by observing
 changes of RSSI. In this experiment, the UHF-RFID tag is moved at intervals of 0.2 m, and RSSI read by the reader is
 averaged at each position. <a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig8" class="fulltext-link">Fig. 8</a>(b) constructs coordinates used for measuring
 RSSI. We have contrasted RSSI for three configurations [basic configuration of the RFID only, with the MMAs, with
 copper clad laminates (CCLs) the same size as the MMAs], and the results are depicted in 
<a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig9" class="fulltext-link">Fig. 9</a>.
</p><div class="figure figure-full" id="fig9"><!--          Workaround for combined images.Eg.- 1000116 Fig. 5        --><div class="img-wrap"><a href="/mediastore/IEEE/content/media/7727/7855842/7482659/zuo9abc-2574885-large.gif"><img src="/mediastore/IEEE/content/media/7727/7855842/7482659/zuo9abc-2574885-small.gif" alt="Figure 9" /><div class="zoom" title="View Larger Image"></div></a></div><div class="figcaption"><b class="title">Fig. 9. </b><fig><p>RSSI results for (a) basic configuration, (b) with CCLs, and (c) with MMAs.</p></fig></div><p class="links"><a href="/document/7482659/all-figures" class="all">View All</a></p></div><p></p><p>In the basic configuration as shown in <a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig9" class="fulltext-link">Fig. 9</a>(a), the tag can be identified
 over the whole given range, which mainly comes from direct readings of the reader. However, the wide read range is
 liable to cause incorrect readings of neighboring RFID systems. We can also find the RSSI attenuates with distance,
 but there still exist areas with irregular RSSI distribution. This indicates that there indeed exists multiplex
 reflection interference from the complex environment, which will cause incorrect readings of the native RFID system.
</p><p>Furthermore, we use CCLs for comparison to further illustrate the interference's adverse effect on the RFID
 system, as shown in <a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig9" class="fulltext-link">Fig. 9</a>(b). As CCLs are fixed on the shelf, which is
 equivalent to enhancing multiplex reflection interference artificially, the RSSI attenuates irregularly. Besides,
 there is no obvious distribution of strong received signal. These mean that the strong interference can gravely affect
 stability and reliability of the RFID system.</p><p>Therefore, the MMAs are used in the hope of improving performance of the RFID system. As shown in 
<a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig9" class="fulltext-link">Fig. 9</a>(c), the RSSI attenuates regularly with distance, and the received signal
 becomes more concentrated around the reader antenna. Moreover, areas outside the MMAs distribute low RSSI or even no
 signal, which just solves the problem of incorrect readings from neighboring RFID systems. By comparing and analyzing
 RSSI results in the three configurations, we can find that the MMAs are very effective for suppressing multipath
