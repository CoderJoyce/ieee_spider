<div class="section" id="sec1"><div class="header article-hdr"><div class="kicker">		                        SECTION I.</div><h2>Introduction</h2></div><p>Radio frequency identification (RFID) technology is rapidly developed for automated identifying and tracking objects in warehouse, supply chain, industry, and commerce. Recently, ultra-high frequency (UHF) RFID system has been receiving much attention owing to its advantages in lower cost, faster recognition rate, and longer reading distance. The UHF RFID system consists of a tag and a reader, which uses radio wave to exchange data between them. The UHF tag antenna is normally linearly polarized and oriented arbitrarily. Thus, the UHF reader antenna is required to radiate a circularly polarized (CP) wave, regardless of the tag's physical orientation <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref1" id="context_ref_1_1">[1]</a>. In addition to circular polarization, the special requirement for UHF RFID reader antenna is the isolation between the Tx and Rx signals. RFID system operates in full-duplex mode, and the reader must transmit continuous wave (CW) for the energy and backscattering of the tag. However, the tag's weak backscattered signal is essentially at the same frequency as the reader's strong transmitting CW. Hence, the isolation from Tx to Rx signal has been an important limit of the RFID reader sensitivity <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref2" id="context_ref_2_1">[2]</a>, <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref3" id="context_ref_3_1">[3]</a>.</p><p>Although circularly polarized RFID reader antennas have been extensively studied <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref4" id="context_ref_4_1">[4]</a>â<a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref5" id="context_ref_5_1">[5]</a><a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref6" id="context_ref_6_1">[6]</a><a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref7" id="context_ref_7_1">[7]</a> and have many commercial products, the isolation solution between Tx/Rx ports of RFID reader front end is not an easy task. As shown in <a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig1" class="fulltext-link">Fig.Â 1</a>, there are four approaches used to achieve high isolation. The major approaches shown in <a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig1" class="fulltext-link">Fig.Â 1(a) and (b)</a> are to use microwave isolation component, such as a circulator <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref8" id="context_ref_8_1">[8]</a>, <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref9" id="context_ref_9_1">[9]</a> and a directional coupler <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref10" id="context_ref_10_1">[10]</a>, <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref11" id="context_ref_11_1">[11]</a>. A conventional isolation component generally provides about â25 dB isolation with just 30-MHz bandwidth, not enough for the worldwide RFID band. Moreover, the isolation characteristic of the component is critically affected by antenna assembly. If the reader antenna is not well matched with the isolation component, the carrier leakage will be increased soon. Thus, it is inconvenient for mass production of the reader antenna. Another approach is to use two separate antennas for transmit and receive antennas, as shown in <a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig1" class="fulltext-link">Fig.Â 1(c)</a>. For antenna diversity, it needs sufficient space and careful consideration about the positions of two antennas. In this letter, we chose the approach to use a dual circularly polarized antenna (DCPA) structure, as shown in <a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig1" class="fulltext-link">Fig.Â 1(d)</a>. It is miniaturized by antenna integration, and the isolation principle is based on pattern diversity in a single antenna. Meanwhile, it presents separate Tx/Rx ports and can interchange them to generate right-hand circular polarization (RHCP) or left-hand circular polarization (LHCP).

<div class="section" id="sec4"><div class="header article-hdr"><div class="kicker">		                        SECTION IV.</div><h2>Conclusion</h2></div><p>A dual circularly polarized RFID reader antenna with wideband isolation has been presented. The proposed structure has a radiating patch and a BLC underneath the ground with H-shaped slots. The radiating patch is coupled by the BLC through two orthogonal H-shaped slots on the ground plane. Both the simulation and measurement results have achieved good impedance matching, axial ratio, and CP gain performance from 860 to 940 MHz. Moreover, the prototype of the proposed antenna has â25-dB isolation bandwidth of 864â929 MHz, covering both Europe (865â869 MHz) and USA (902â928 MHz) RFID bands. The prototype generates RHCP at 867 and 915 MHz, and it can also create LHCP by interchange of Tx/Rx ports. In conclusion, this antenna is suitable for wideband RFID reader application.</p></div>
