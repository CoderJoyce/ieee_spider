<div class="section" id="sec1"><div class="header article-hdr"><div class="kicker">		                        SECTION I.</div><h2>Introduction</h2></div><p>RF identification (RFID) has gained a lot of attention recently. Passive RFID technology at UHF has enabled several applications, e.g., tracking of goods in supply chain and asset management. The success of the technology is based on robust identification up to a range of 10 m with inexpensive (even less than 10 Euro cent) tags, and standards <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref1" id="context_ref_1_1">[1]</a>, <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref2" id="context_ref_2_1">[2]</a> that guarantee the interoperability of tags and readers of different manufacturers. However, the readers are bulky due to the big antennas at UHF.</p><p>Millimeter-wave RFID (MMID) enables directional reader and tag antennas with practical dimensions <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref3" id="context_ref_3_1">[3]</a>, <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref4" id="context_ref_4_1">[4]</a>. A narrow-beamed highly directional reader antenna enables localization by manual scanning or by angle-of-arrival (AoA). For ranging, similar techniques than at UHF RFID can be used, such as received signal strength (RSSI, e.g., <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref5" id="context_ref_5_1">[5]</a>) or phase (e.g., <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref6" id="context_ref_6_1">[6]</a>–<a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref7" id="context_ref_7_1">[7]</a><a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref8" id="context_ref_8_1">[8]</a>). Other advantages of MMID include wider available bandwidth, which enables high data-rate short-range communications. The drawback is that the antenna effective aperture is directly proportional to the square of the wavelength, effectively diminishing the available power in the passive tag.</p><p>The key component in the passive tag operation is the rectifier in the tag. Rectification at low power levels is inefficient. At UHF, rectifier efficiencies of 60% are reported <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref9" id="context_ref_9_1">[9]</a>. Scaling of CMOS to higher frequencies has also enabled integrated rectifiers at millimeter waves. In <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref10" id="context_ref_10_1">[10]</a>, an integrated millimeter-wave RFID tag at 60 GHz is reported with rectifier efficiency of 1.2%. The sensitivity of the tag is 2 dBm at a data bandwidth of 5 kbit/s.</p><p>In this paper, we present an alternative solution for using millimeter waves for passive RFID. We propose using an external nonlinear component between the tag antenna and UHF RFID IC for mixing millimeter waves to IF frequency suitable for a standard RFID chip, as illustrated in <a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig1" class="fulltext-link">Fig. 1</a>.

<p></p><p>The system enables passive RFID at millimeter waves with standard EPC protocol, including all the features of the UHF RFID, such as anticollision and data rates up to 640 kbit/s. In this paper, operation is demonstrated at 10 GHz, but the idea works at any carrier frequency substantially higher than the frequency of the RFID chip. HF RFID chips could also be used, but their power consumption is higher than UHF chips, leading to an even shorter range. On the other hand, semipassive UHF RFID chips have better sensitivity than passive ones, which would enable higher range.</p><p>In this paper, we use a Schottky diode as the mixing element at the tag. Capacitive mixer components, such as varactor diodes, ferroelectric varactors, or microelectromechanical systems (MEMS) resonators could diminish the conversion gain, but no suitable commercial components exist at millimeter waves <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref11" id="context_ref_11_1">[11]</a>.</p><p>This paper is organized as follows. <a ref-type="sec" anchor="sec2" class="fulltext-link">Section II</a> analyses the operation principle of the tag theoretically. Detailed simulation and design of the demonstrator tag at 10 GHz, including the range estimation, is presented in <a ref-type="sec" anchor="sec3" class="fulltext-link">Section III</a>, and experimental verification is presented in <a ref-type="sec" anchor="sec4" class="fulltext-link">Section IV</a>.</p></div>

<div class="section" id="sec3"><div class="header article-hdr"><div class="kicker">		                        SECTION III.</div><h2>Simulations</h2></div><div class="section_2" id="sec3a"><h3>A. Antenna Design</h3><p>The necessity to operate at microwave frequencies force us to operate as follows: at the transmitter side, a baseband AM-modulated signal will be produced by an ordinary digital signal processor (DSP)-based RFID reader and internally up-converted to <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">\omega _{\rm IF}</span><span class="MathJax MathJax_Processing" id="MathJax-Element-44-Frame" tabindex="0"></span><script type="math/tex" id="MathJax-Element-44">\omega _{\rm IF}</script></tex-math></inline-formula> in a range of 850–950 MHz. An external customized mixer will then perform the follow-up conversion to <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">\omega _{0} \pm \omega _{\rm IF}</span><span class="MathJax MathJax_Processing" id="MathJax-Element-45-Frame" tabindex="0"></span><script type="math/tex" id="MathJax-Element-45">\omega _{0} \pm \omega _{\rm IF}</script></tex-math></inline-formula> by a suitable oscillator tone <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">\omega _{0}</span><span class="MathJax MathJax_Processing" id="MathJax-Element-46-Frame" tabindex="0"></span><script type="math/tex" id="MathJax-Element-46">\omega _{0}</script></tex-math></inline-formula>—in this case, at 10 GHz—at the end of our reading block scheme.</p><p>The double up-conversion architecture implies a relative transmitted signal bandwidth of <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">\omega _{\rm IF} / \omega _{0} = 10\hbox{\%}</span><span class="MathJax MathJax_Processing" id="MathJax-Element-47-Frame" tabindex="0"></span><script type="math/tex" id="MathJax-Element-47">\omega _{\rm IF} / \omega _{0} = 10\hbox{\%}</script></tex-math></inline-formula> at least—by means of a suitable image-rejection mixer—and the necessity to count on wide-bandwidth antennas. An endfire traveling-wave Vivaldi planar topology was preferred due to its simple structure, high gain, good efficiency, and wide bandwidth. Its working mechanism consists of a slotline flared outward to an aperture where the wave impedance matches free space <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref12" id="context_ref_12_3a">[12]</a>. The most critical elements for the antenna performances, such as microstrip-to-slotline transition and exponential tapered parts, have been analyzed and refined by full-wave electromagnetic (EM) simulation <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref13" id="context_ref_13_3a">[13]</a> through the finite-element method. Rogers RO4350B (<inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">h = 0.762</span><span class="MathJax MathJax_Processing" id="MathJax-Element-48-Frame" tabindex="0"></span><script type="math/tex" id="MathJax-Element-48">h = 0.762</script></tex-math></inline-formula> mm, Cu <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">1/2</span><span class="MathJax MathJax_Processing" id="MathJax-Element-49-Frame" tabindex="0"></span><script type="math/tex" id="MathJax-Element-49">1/2</script></tex-math></inline-formula> oz, <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">\varepsilon _{r} = 3.66, \tan \delta = 0.004</span><span class="MathJax MathJax_Processing" id="MathJax-Element-50-Frame" tabindex="0"></span><script type="math/tex" id="MathJax-Element-50">\varepsilon _{r} = 3.66, \tan \delta = 0.004</script></tex-math></inline-formula>) was chosen for the substrate material. The layout of the antenna is shown in <a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig4" class="fulltext-link">Fig. 4</a>. The differences between measured and simulated return losses (<a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig5" class="fulltext-link">Fig. 5</a>) are most probably due to fabrication tolerance in the very narrow tapered slot in the microstrip-to-slotline transition. <p></p></div><div class="section_2" id="sec3b"><h3>B. Front-End Design Considerations</h3><p>Since the ordinary RFID chip is expected to be operating at UHF (850–950 MHz), the tag working principle will basically consist of a nonlinear harmonic generation provided by a single-diode mixer—simplest and most effective topology in the present case—with local oscillator tuning furnished by the incoming continuous wave at <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">\omega _{0}</span><span class="MathJax MathJax_Processing" id="MathJax-Element-53-Frame" tabindex="0"></span><script type="math/tex" id="MathJax-Element-53">\omega _{0}</script></tex-math></inline-formula> from the transmitter: the load will be only interested by the intermodulation product at <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">\omega _{\rm IF}</span><span class="MathJax MathJax_Processing" id="MathJax-Element-54-Frame" tabindex="0"></span><script type="math/tex" id="MathJax-Element-54">\omega _{\rm IF}</script></tex-math></inline-formula> at the chip's side. A key feature in the mixer design is the choice for a suitable diode, in this case, an Avago Technologies HSMS-286B (<inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">C_{J0} = 0.18</span><span class="MathJax MathJax_Processing" id="MathJax-Element-55-Frame" tabindex="0"></span><script type="math/tex" id="MathJax-Element-55">C_{J0} = 0.18</script></tex-math></inline-formula> pF, <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">I_{S} = 50</span><span class="MathJax MathJax_Processing" id="MathJax-Element-56-Frame" tabindex="0"></span><script type="math/tex" id="MathJax-Element-56">I_{S} = 50</script></tex-math></inline-formula> nA, <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">N = 1.08</span><span class="MathJax MathJax_Processing" id="MathJax-Element-57-Frame" tabindex="0"></span><script type="math/tex" id="MathJax-Element-57">N = 1.08</script></tex-math></inline-formula>, and <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX">\$V_{J} = 0.65\\$</tex-math></inline-formula> V). The matching section between antenna and nonlinear device was designed using a conversion gain optimization goal in EM simulation software <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref14" id="context_ref_14_3b">[14]</a>. <p></p></div><div class="section_2" id="sec3c"><h3>C. Range Estimation</h3><p>An accurate evaluation of the power available to the integrated circuit (IC) is developed by combining the reciprocity theorem with the antenna performances, computed by EM simulation <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref15" id="context_ref_15_3c">[15]</a>. Such an approach was demonstrated to be able to avoid some ineffectiveness of the Friis equation when the far-field assumption in the link analysis between Reader and Tag is disregarded (e.g., <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">{\rm dist} {&lt;} 10 \lambda</span><span class="MathJax MathJax_Processing" id="MathJax-Element-66-Frame" tabindex="0"></span><script type="math/tex" id="MathJax-Element-66">{\rm dist} {&lt;} 10 \lambda</script></tex-math></inline-formula> with <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">\lambda</span><span class="MathJax MathJax_Processing" id="MathJax-Element-67-Frame" tabindex="0"></span><script type="math/tex" id="MathJax-Element-67">\lambda</script></tex-math></inline-formula> as the free-space wavelength at the frequency of interest). Furthermore, port mismatch and power transfer effects between the antenna elements and the nonlinear subsystems are now rigorously accounted for <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref16" id="context_ref_16_3c">[16]</a>. Here we follow the classic EM formalism (<a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig9" class="fulltext-link">Fig. 9</a>) to specify a field source in terms of known current source and refer to the Norton-equivalent representation for the tag circuit (<a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig2" class="fulltext-link">Fig. 2</a>).
</p><div class="figure figure-full" id="fig9"><!--          Workaround for combined images.Eg.- 1000116 Fig. 5        --><div class="img-wrap"><a href="/mediastore/IEEE/content/media/22/5983338/5765711/5765711-fig-9-large.gif"><img src="/mediastore/IEEE/content/media/22/5983338/5765711/5765711-fig-9-small.gif" alt="Figure 9" /><div class="zoom" title="View Larger Image"></div></a></div><div class="figcaption"><b class="title">Fig. 9. </b><fig><p>Tag in presence of an IPW.</p></fig></div><p class="links"><a href="/document/5765711/all-figures" class="all">View All</a></p></div>
<p></p><p>The current source <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">I_{A} (\omega)</span><span class="MathJax MathJax_Processing" id="MathJax-Element-68-Frame" tabindex="0"></span><script type="math/tex" id="MathJax-Element-68">I_{A} (\omega)</script></tex-math></inline-formula>—depending only on the electric field <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">{\bf E}_{\bf T}</span><span class="MathJax MathJax_Processing" id="MathJax-Element-69-Frame" tabindex="0"></span><script type="math/tex" id="MathJax-Element-69">{\bf E}_{\bf T}</script></tex-math></inline-formula> of the incoming plane wave (IPW) from the transmitter—plays the role of free source in the receiver analysis and can be computed in two steps: first the incident field is temporarily suppressed and the receiving antenna is employed in a transmitting mode. At the same distance, by making use of <a ref-type="disp-formula" anchor="deqn11" href="#deqn11" class="fulltext-link">(11)</a>, the backscattered signal power is proven to fulfill a standard reader sensitivity of roughly −80 dBm—a huge noise contribution among the entire block chain should be considered. Here the atmospheric attenuation (15 dB/km at 60 GHz) can be neglected because the distances are small. For 1-m radio path length, the additional attenuation due to atmosphere is 0.015 dB.</p><p>Of course the read range might be considerably increased through a suitable active device at the reader side such as a high-gain power amplifier. Using a more directive reader antenna would increase the TX power to 30-dBm ERP, a power level comparable to a standard UHF RFID reader output. This would extend the range to 30 cm. Adding a higher gain tag antenna would also extend the range: e.g., a <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">2*2</span><span class="MathJax MathJax_Processing" id="MathJax-Element-85-Frame" tabindex="0"></span><script type="math/tex" id="MathJax-Element-85">2*2</script></tex-math></inline-formula> patch antenna provides fourfold gain, or twofold range compared to a single patch or a Vivaldi. The array is similar to UHF RFID tag antennas in size (<inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">5*5</span><span class="MathJax MathJax_Processing" id="MathJax-Element-86-Frame" tabindex="0"></span><script type="math/tex" id="MathJax-Element-86">5*5</script></tex-math></inline-formula> cm<sup>2</sup>). Further, using a semipassive tag IC, the tag sensitivity could be reduced at least to −26 dBm. Since the conversion gain also scales with the incoming power, this would only give twofold range extension. Hence, the range can be extended to over a meter. Capacitive mixing might help to extend the range further, especially with the extremely low input power of semipassive UHF RFID tags.</p></div></div>

<div class="section" id="sec5"><div class="header article-hdr"><div class="kicker">		                        SECTION V.</div><h2>Conclusion</h2></div><p>This paper has introduced a novel way to implement passive RFID at millimeter waves. The passive operation is achieved by adding an external mixing element between the tag antenna and a standard RFID chip. The mixing element converts the RFID reader signal from millimeter waves to RFID carrier frequency. The reader's TX and RX ports are similarly equipped with external mixers to convert the reader UHF output to millimeter waves and received signal millimeter-wave signal to RFID carrier frequency.</p><p>The paper has demonstrated the operation principle at 10 GHz with an EPC-compliant UHF tag IC. A Schottky diode was used as the mixing element. The diode achieved a measured conversion gain of −10 dB from millimeter waves to UHF at the operation power level of the tag. The measured sensitivity of the tag is −4 dBm. The tag was read using a standard EPC reader up to 30 cm with a data rate of 40 kbit/s. The operation range can be extended to over a meter by using more sensitive RFID chips, e.g., semipassive chips, and in the future by using a capacitive mixing element, such as a varactor diode or a ferroelectric varactor.</p></div>
