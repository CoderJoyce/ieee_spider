<div class="section" id="sec1"><div class="header article-hdr"><div class="kicker">		                        SECTION I.</div><h2>Introduction and Motivation</h2></div><p>Multiple-input–multiple-output (MIMO) systems significantly increase information rate in multipath environments by leveraging spatial diversity. Recently, MIMO techniques have been considered for wireless power transfer (WPT), simultaneously targeting energy and information transfer using coherent channel measurements between transmitter and receiver <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref1" id="context_ref_1_1">[1]</a>, <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref2" id="context_ref_2_1">[2]</a>. We present a backscatter extension to MIMO WPT that allows the system to operate with ultra-low power devices that are not capable of channel estimation or even local power measurement. This extension shifts complexity and power requirements from the mobile devices to the base station, thus enabling WPT optimization to highly power-constrained devices such as radio frequency identification (RFID) tags or low-power wireless sensor nodes. It also complements past <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref3" id="context_ref_3_1">[3]</a> and present <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref4" id="context_ref_4_1">[4]</a>–<a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref5" id="context_ref_5_1">[5]</a><a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref6" id="context_ref_6_1">[6]</a><a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref7" id="context_ref_7_1">[7]</a> efforts to introduce spatial diversity and MIMO concepts in the field of UHF RFID and backscatter communications. We provide an experimental proof of concept demonstrating that the channel information provided by a device's backscatter signal can be used to significantly enhance far-field WPT efficiency to this device, without knowledge of the local power level and without estimating channel gains at the mobile device.</p><p>In a far-field MIMO WPT system, energy over some bandwidth is injected into an environment from multiple distributed transmitters and received at a mobile device some distance away. Channel information is used to optimize gain and phase of each transmitter in order to maximize the energy transferred to the mobile device. In this letter, we leverage modulated backscatter, a communication technique widely used in UHF RFID, to perform all estimation and optimization solely at the base station. In addition to the power and complexity advantages for the mobile device, the necessary continuous synchronization between all participants in channel estimates is significantly simpler and more efficient among interconnected base stations than to a mobile transponder. <p></p><p>We show that in a typical environment, the proposed WPT optimization works for any linear backscatter device, independent of its location or configuration. It can be used to optimize power transfer to a single device or to multiple mobile devices, each with one or more antennas, and is independent of the backscatter modulation type (e.g., ASK, PSK, QAM) and the data coding. It is thus suitable for all kinds of backscatter devices and can easily be integrated into existing UHF RFID transponders. This approach is discussed in detail below. Multiple backscatter devices <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">{\rm BT}_{m}</span><span class="MathJax MathJax_Processing" id="MathJax-Element-23-Frame" tabindex="0"></span><script type="math/tex" id="MathJax-Element-23">{\rm BT}_{m}</script></tex-math></inline-formula> are addressed separately in order to obtain the channel/power information for each device while the optimization itself is done jointly.</p><p>Transmitters control their output amplitudes and phases in a concerted fashion in order to control the power distribution in the environment and thus the power transfer to the mobile backscatter devices. We denote the complex gain for transmitter <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">l</span><span class="MathJax MathJax_Processing" id="MathJax-Element-24-Frame" tabindex="0"></span><script type="math/tex" id="MathJax-Element-24">l</script></tex-math></inline-formula> at <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">f_k</span><span class="MathJax MathJax_Processing" id="MathJax-Element-25-Frame" tabindex="0"></span><script type="math/tex" id="MathJax-Element-25">f_k</script></tex-math></inline-formula> by <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">a_{l, k} = \vert a_{l, k}\vert{\rm e}^{\jmath\angle a_{l, k}} \in {\BBC}</span><span class="MathJax MathJax_Processing" id="MathJax-Element-26-Frame" tabindex="0"></span><script type="math/tex" id="MathJax-Element-26">a_{l, k} = \vert a_{l, k}\vert{\rm e}^{\jmath\angle a_{l, k}} \in {\BBC}</script></tex-math></inline-formula>, where <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">\vert a_{l, k}\vert</span><span class="MathJax MathJax_Processing" id="MathJax-Element-27-Frame" tabindex="0"></span><script type="math/tex" id="MathJax-Element-27">\vert a_{l, k}\vert</script></tex-math></inline-formula> is the gain and <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">\angle a_{l, k}</span><span class="MathJax MathJax_Processing" id="MathJax-Element-28-Frame" tabindex="0"></span><script type="math/tex" id="MathJax-Element-28">\angle a_{l, k}</script></tex-math></inline-formula> is the phase, respectively. This results in an available (incident) power level at <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">{\rm BT}_{m}</span><span class="MathJax MathJax_Processing" id="MathJax-Element-29-Frame" tabindex="0"></span><script type="math/tex" id="MathJax-Element-29">{\rm BT}_{m}</script></tex-math></inline-formula> of <disp-formula id="deqn2" class="display-formula"><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">P_{{\rm AV}m} = \sum_{k=1}^{K} \left\vert\sum_{l=1}^{L} a_{l, k} \cdot c_{lm, k} \right\vert^2 \eqno{\hbox{(2)}}</span><div class="MathJax_Display MathJax_Processing"><span class="MathJax" id="MathJax-Element-30-Frame" tabindex="0"></span></div><script type="math/tex; mode=display" id="MathJax-Element-30">P_{{\rm AV}m} = \sum_{k=1}^{K} \left\vert\sum_{l=1}^{L} a_{l, k} \cdot c_{lm, k} \right\vert^2 \eqno{\hbox{(2)}}</script></tex-math><span class="formula"><span class="link">View Source</span><img aria-describedby="qtip-0" style="display:inline;" title="Right-click on figure or equation for MathML and additional features." data-hasqtip="0" class="qtooltip moreInfo" alt="Right-click on figure for MathML and additional features." src="/assets/img/icon.support.gif" border="0" height="20" width="24" /><span class="tex tex2jax_ignore" style="display:none;">P_{{\rm AV}m} = \sum_{k=1}^{K} \left\vert\sum_{l=1}^{L} a_{l, k} \cdot c_{lm, k} \right\vert^2 \eqno{\hbox{(2)}}</span></span></disp-formula>for frequency-independent power harvesting. Adding backscatter and return link channel gains, we obtain the power level at the <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">n</span><span class="MathJax MathJax_Processing" id="MathJax-Element-31-Frame" tabindex="0"></span><script type="math/tex" id="MathJax-Element-31">n</script></tex-math></inline-formula>th MIMO receiver <disp-formula id="deqn3" class="display-formula"><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">P_{{\rm RX}nm} = \sum_{k=1}^{K} \left\vert \sum_{l=1}^{L} a_{l, k} \cdot c_{lm, k} \rho_{m, k} c_{mn, k} \right\vert^2. \eqno{\hbox{(3)}}</span><div class="MathJax_Display MathJax_Processing"><span class="MathJax" id="MathJax-Element-32-Frame" tabindex="0"></span></div><script type="math/tex; Even though the ultimate goal of the optimization is <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">P_{{\rm AV}{m}}</span><span class="MathJax MathJax_Processing" id="MathJax-Element-34-Frame" tabindex="0"></span><script type="math/tex" id="MathJax-Element-34">P_{{\rm AV}{m}}</script></tex-math></inline-formula>, the only values that can be measured directly at the base station are <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">P_{{\rm RX}nm}</span><span class="MathJax MathJax_Processing" id="MathJax-Element-35-Frame" tabindex="0"></span><script type="math/tex" id="MathJax-Element-35">P_{{\rm RX}nm}</script></tex-math></inline-formula> and <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">{\mbi B}_{{\mmb m},{\mbi k}}</span><span class="MathJax MathJax_Processing" id="MathJax-Element-36-Frame" tabindex="0"></span><script type="math/tex" id="MathJax-Element-36">{\mbi B}_{{\mmb m},{\mbi k}}</script></tex-math></inline-formula>. Since <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">P_{\rm AV}</span><span class="MathJax MathJax_Processing" id="MathJax-Element-37-Frame" tabindex="0"></span><script type="math/tex" id="MathJax-Element-37">P_{\rm AV}</script></tex-math></inline-formula> and <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">P_{\rm RX}</span><span class="MathJax MathJax_Processing" id="MathJax-Element-38-Frame" tabindex="0"></span><script type="math/tex" id="MathJax-Element-38">P_{\rm RX}</script></tex-math></inline-formula> are connected by linear channels, however, it follows that optimizing <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">P_{{\rm RX} \cdot {m}}</span><span class="MathJax MathJax_Processing" id="MathJax-Element-39-Frame" tabindex="0"></span><script type="math/tex" id="MathJax-Element-39">P_{{\rm RX} \cdot {m}}</script></tex-math></inline-formula> will in turn optimize <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">P_{{\rm AV}{m}}</span><span class="MathJax MathJax_Processing" id="MathJax-Element-40-Frame" tabindex="0"></span><script type="math/tex" id="MathJax-Element-40">P_{{\rm AV}{m}}</script></tex-math></inline-formula>. This optimization problem can be solved by singular value decomposition (SVD) of the MIMO backscatter gain matrix <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">{\mbi B}_{{\mmb m},{\mbi k}}</span><span class="MathJax MathJax_Processing" id="MathJax-Element-41-Frame" tabindex="0"></span><script type="math/tex" id="MathJax-Element-41">{\mbi B}_{{\mmb m},{\mbi k}}</script></tex-math></inline-formula> in the same way it would be done for the forward link alone; see <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref1" id="context_ref_1_2">[1]</a>. The resulting autocovariance matrix is subsequently solved for <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">\vert a_{l, k}\vert</span><span class="MathJax MathJax_Processing" id="MathJax-Element-42-Frame" tabindex="0"></span><script type="math/tex" id="MathJax-Element-42">\vert a_{l, k}\vert</script></tex-math></inline-formula> and <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">\angle a_{l, k}</span><span class="MathJax MathJax_Processing" id="MathJax-Element-43-Frame" tabindex="0"></span><script type="math/tex" id="MathJax-Element-43">\angle a_{l, k}</script></tex-math></inline-formula> using table lookup.</p><p>Alternatively, the same result can be obtained with some additional flexibility by optimization using <a ref-type="disp-formula" anchor="deqn3" href="#deqn3" class="fulltext-link">(3)</a> in a cost function, such as a weighted sum over all <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">n</span><span class="MathJax MathJax_Processing" id="MathJax-Element-44-Frame" tabindex="0"></span><script type="math/tex" id="MathJax-Element-44">n</script></tex-math></inline-formula> and <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">m</span><span class="MathJax MathJax_Processing" id="MathJax-Element-45-Frame" tabindex="0"></span><script type="math/tex" id="MathJax-Element-45">m</script></tex-math></inline-formula>. This has the additional benefit that only received power levels are required, as opposed to requiring channel gains for the SVD approach. We used nonlinear constrained/unconstrained optimization (fmincon and fminunc in MATLAB) as well as Genetic Algorithm optimization (ga), with comparable results. Power minimization has local optima (several field nulls that can be steered to the position of the backscatter device). However, even local minima provide significant power denial compared to the average power level.</p><p>Note that neither of the above approaches requires solving for the individual channel gains or the backscatter gain, thus eliminating the need for calibration or channel estimates on the backscatter transponder.</p></div>
<div class="section" id="sec3"><div class="header article-hdr"><div class="kicker">		                        SECTION III.</div><h2>Measurement Results/Proof of Concept</h2></div><p>Measurements for a proof of concept were performed using an Agilent E5062A vector network analyzer (VNA) as the heart of an MIMO base station, measuring channel transfer functions multiplexed between eight base station antennas and 100 mobile device locations covering an area 50 m<sup>2</sup> in two corridors and a hallway in an office/lab building (<a ref-type="fig" anchor="fig4" class="fulltext-link">Fig. 4</a>), equally covering line-of-sight (LOS) and non-line-of-sight (NLOS) scenarios. The observed channels are dominated by scattering and feature dense multipath components. Some strong specular components are contributed by long RF-reflective window bays and by large metal elevator doors. Vertically polarized omnidirectional patch antennas <a ref-type="bibr" anchor="ref11" id="context_ref_11_3">[11]</a> having a primary frequency range of 0.7–1.2 GHz with approximately 2 dBi gain at 1 GHz were used for the measurements. We assume an RFID-typical setup with a single unmodulated carrier at 916 MHz as energy-bearing signal. Backscatter channels were formed in post-processing by multiplying the individual single-channel transfer functions and i.i.d. circulant symmetric backscatter reflection coefficients <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">\rho_{m,k}</span><span class="MathJax MathJax_Processing" id="MathJax-Element-46-Frame" tabindex="0"></span><script type="math/tex" id="MathJax-Element-46">\rho_{m,k}</script></tex-math></inline-formula>, uniformly distributed within the unit circle (arbitrary backscatter modulation). The backscatter transponders <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">{\rm BT}_{m}</span><span class="MathJax MathJax_Processing" id="MathJax-Element-47-Frame" tabindex="0"></span><script type="math/tex" id="MathJax-Element-47">{\rm BT}_{m}</script></tex-math></inline-formula> at all <inline-formula id=""><tex-math notation="TeX"><span class="MathJax_Preview">m=1\ldots 100</span><span class="MathJax MathJax_Processing" id="MathJax-Element-48-Frame" tabindex="0"></span><script type="math/tex" id="MathJax-Element-48">m=1\ldots 100</script></tex-math></inline-formula> positions thus have the worst case of i.i.d. uniform reflection coefficients. Power levels were optimized using SVD, choosing the settings corresponding to the highest singular value for maximization and the smallest singular value for minimization.

<div class="section" id="sec4"><div class="header article-hdr"><div class="kicker">		                        SECTION IV.</div><h2>Conclusion</h2></div><p>We present a method to extend MIMO WPT optimization to milliwatt- and microwatt-class wirelessly powered devices, which typically do not have the capability to actively participate in channel estimation or perform power measurements due to their low-power and low-complexity nature. We show that backscatter signaling permits the shifting of nearly all the complexity and the power cost of the WPT optimization to the base station. In a measurement-based proof-of-concept, we have demonstrated an average WPT enhancement of 8.1 dB compared to the nonoptimized case using an 8×8 MIMO base station, over a uniform sample of 100 measurement locations in an ordinary office environment. This result assumes the worst case of unknown i.i.d. uniform linear backscatter reflection coefficients and is thus valid for all linear backscatter devices. Moreover, transponders with nonlinear power harvesters can be supported with the addition of a single RF switch.</p></div>
